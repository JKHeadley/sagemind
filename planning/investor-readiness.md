## Part 1: Foundational Business Concepts

### What is a Cap Table?

A **cap table** (capitalization table) is a spreadsheet that shows who owns what percentage of your company. Right now it's simple:

| Owner | Ownership | Notes |
|-------|-----------|-------|
| Justin Headley | 100% | Sole owner, LLC registered in Nevada |

When you take investment, a new row gets added. If Gregg invests $50K at a $2M valuation, he'd own ~2.5%, and your table grows. Every future investment, employee stock grant, or advisor equity grant adds another row.

**Why it matters:** Investors want to see a clean cap table before putting money in. It tells them exactly what they're buying and how much dilution (ownership reduction) happens over time.

### What is a SAFE Note?

**SAFE = Simple Agreement for Future Equity.** Created by Y Combinator (the startup accelerator behind Airbnb, Stripe, DoorDash).

Think of it like this: An investor gives you money NOW, but instead of immediately getting a percentage of the company, they get a promise that their money will convert into ownership LATER — when you do a bigger fundraising round.

**Key terms in a SAFE:**
- **Valuation Cap:** The maximum company value at which the investor's money converts. Example: If the cap is $2M and your company is later valued at $10M, the investor's money still converts as if it were $2M — rewarding them for investing early.
- **Discount:** A percentage off the next round's price (typically 20%). So if new investors pay $1/share, the SAFE holder pays $0.80/share.

**Important note:** SAFEs are designed for C-Corps (corporations). Since Sagemind is an LLC, a convertible note or direct equity purchase may be simpler. More on this below.

### LLC vs C-Corp: What You Need to Know

**You're currently an LLC.** This is fine for operating, but matters for investment:

| | LLC | C-Corp (Delaware) |
|---|---|---|
| Investment compatibility | Harder — can't use standard SAFEs | Standard — all investor docs work |
| Tax treatment | Pass-through (profits taxed on your personal return) | Double taxation (corporate + personal) BUT offset by QSBS tax benefits |
| QSBS benefit | Not available | Up to $10M in capital gains tax-free |
| Conversion cost | N/A | $2K-$5K in legal fees |
| Best for | Single angel, staying small | Multiple investors, planning to scale |

**Bottom line for right now:** For a single angel like Gregg, you CAN stay as an LLC and do a convertible note or direct equity sale. If you plan to raise a real seed round later (from multiple investors or a fund), you'll want to convert to a Delaware C-Corp.

**Decision needed:** Not urgent today, but before formalizing anything with Gregg.

### What is Valuation?

Valuation = what your company is worth on paper. There are two types:

- **Pre-money valuation:** What the company is worth BEFORE the investment
- **Post-money valuation:** Pre-money + the investment amount

**Example:**
- Pre-money valuation: $2,000,000
- Gregg invests: $100,000
- Post-money valuation: $2,100,000
- Gregg's ownership: $100K / $2.1M = **4.76%**

**How do you determine your valuation?** At your stage, it's a negotiation, not a formula. But here are the inputs:

1. **Revenue multiple:** Your ~$100K run rate × 10-20x = $1M-$2M (for an AI company with growth)
2. **Berkus method:** Idea ($250K) + Working product ($500K) + Team ($250K) + Traction ($250K) + Relationships ($250K) = ~$1.5M
3. **What comparable companies raised at:** Early AI services companies typically raise at $1M-$3M pre-money

**Reasonable range for Sagemind today: $1.5M-$2.5M pre-money valuation.**

### What is a Convertible Note?

Since you're an LLC, this is likely the best instrument for Gregg's investment.

A convertible note is a **loan** that converts into ownership later:
- Gregg lends you $X
- The note has an interest rate (typically 5-8%) and a maturity date (18-24 months)
- When you raise a bigger round later, the loan + interest converts into equity at a discount

**Why this works for you:**
- Compatible with LLC structure
- Delays the hard valuation conversation
- Simple legal docs
- Gregg gets rewarded for investing early (via the discount)

If you NEVER raise another round, the note becomes due at maturity — but you can negotiate extensions or convert it to equity at an agreed valuation at that point.

### What is Equity Dilution?

Every time you give someone ownership (investor, employee, advisor), your percentage goes DOWN. This is called **dilution.**

**Example over time:**

| Event | Justin & Adriana | Gregg | Employee Pool | New Investor |
|-------|-----------------|-------|---------------|-------------|
| Today | 100% | — | — | — |
| Angel round | ~95% | ~5% | — | — |
| Create option pool | ~80% | ~5% | ~15% | — |
| Seed round | ~60% | ~4% | ~12% | ~24% |

This is normal and expected. 60% of a $10M company is worth far more than 100% of a $100K company. **Dilution is the cost of growth.**

---

### What is an Option Pool?

A set-aside of ownership (typically 10-20%) reserved for future employees. You don't give it away immediately — it's a bucket you pull from as you hire.

**Example:** You reserve 15%. You hire an AI engineer and offer them 1%. That comes from the pool, not from your personal ownership.

**Why investors care:** They want to know the pool exists so future hiring doesn't dilute THEIR shares. They'll often ask you to create the pool BEFORE they invest (so the dilution comes from your shares, not theirs).

---

### What is Founder Vesting?

Even though you already own the company, investors want to see **vesting** — a schedule that says you "earn" your shares over time.

**Standard:** 4-year vesting, 1-year cliff
- After 1 year: 25% of your shares are "vested" (fully yours)
- After that: Shares vest monthly over the remaining 3 years
- If a founder leaves before 1 year: they get nothing back

**Why this exists:** It protects the company and the investor. If one founder walks away after 3 months, the remaining team isn't stuck with a huge chunk of the company in the hands of someone who's gone.

**For you:** Since Justin has been working on Sagemind for a while, you can do "reverse vesting" — credit the time already served. Example: If he's been at it for 2 years, he starts 50% vested.
