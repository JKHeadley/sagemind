# Ontology



I. DEFINITION OF ONTOLOGY

	•	Etymology: From Greek *onto-* (“being”) + *-logia* (“study of”)

	•	Core Question: *What is there?* What does it mean to *be*?



Ontology investigates:

	•	What kinds of things exist?

	•	What is their nature or essence?

	•	How do entities relate to each other?

II. HISTORICAL DEVELOPMENT

1. Ancient Ontology

	•	Parmenides: Being is one, unchanging, and eternal. Non-being is impossible.

	•	Plato: Distinction between world of forms (ideal, eternal) and world of appearances (changing, imperfect).

	•	Aristotle: Introduced substance ontology:

	•	Substance (ousia): That which exists independently.

	•	Accidents: Properties that depend on substance.

	•	Introduced categories (e.g., quantity, quality, relation, etc.).



2. Medieval Ontology

	•	Realism vs. Nominalism:

	•	*Realists* (e.g., Aquinas): Universals (like “redness”) exist independently of the mind.

	•	*Nominalists* (e.g., Ockham): Universals are just names; only particulars exist.

	•	Ontology integrated with theology (e.g., hierarchy of being, God as Being itself).



3. Modern Ontology

	•	Descartes: Dualism – mind and body as two kinds of substances.

	•	Spinoza: Monism – only one substance (God/Nature).

	•	Leibniz: Monadology – the universe is made of simple, immaterial substances called monads.

	•	Kant: Denied we can know *things-in-themselves* (noumena); we only know appearances (phenomena).



4. Contemporary Ontology

	•	Heidegger: Differentiated between *being* (Sein) and *beings* (Seiendes); focused on *Dasein* (human being as the one who questions being).

	•	Analytic ontology (Quine, Kripke): Focused on formal logic, language, and existence criteria (“What entities does our theory commit us to?”).

	•	Process ontology (Whitehead): Reality is fundamentally made up of processes/events, not static substances.



1. Core Ontological Tenets of Yogācāra

A. Cittamātra: “Mind-Only” or “Consciousness-Only”

	•	There is no external, independently existing world. All phenomena are manifestations of mind (citta).

	•	This is not solipsism but a denial of a subject-object duality—objects are not separate from consciousness.

	•	Reality is a stream of cognitive experience or appearances within consciousness.



“It is like a dream, a mirage, or an illusion: the appearances are there, but they do not exist independently.”

B. Trisvabhāva (Three Natures / Three Modes of Existence)

This is Yogācāra’s key ontological model for how phenomena arise and are misapprehended:

	1.	Parikalpita-svabhāva (Imagined/Constructed Nature)

	•	The mistaken belief in the duality of subject and object.

	•	This is the realm of conceptual imputation (*vikalpa*), language, and false imagination.

	•	Example: Believing a rope is a snake.

	2.	Paratantra-svabhāva (Other-dependent Nature)

	•	The actual dependently arisen flow of experiences, mental imprints (*vāsanās*), and causes/conditions.

	•	It refers to the non-dual stream of consciousness in its raw, unfiltered arising.

	•	This is not “real” yet is the basis for conventional existence.

	3.	Pariniṣpanna-svabhāva (Perfected Nature)

	•	The realization of the lack of duality between perceiver and perceived.

	•	The emptiness of both the constructed and dependent natures.

	•	This is the ultimate truth—not a substance but a direct, non-dual realization.



These three are not three separate things, but three ways of experiencing a single cognitive flow.



2. Eight Consciousnesses (Aṣṭa-vijñāna)

Yogācāra ontology builds on a layered model of the mind:

	1.	Five sense consciousnesses

(sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch)

	2.	Mano-vijñāna (Mental consciousness)

	•	Processes thoughts, ideas, conceptual activity.

	3.	Manas (Defiled mind or self-grasping consciousness)

	•	Clings to the *ālaya* as a “self.”

	•	Root of ignorance and duality.

	4.	Ālaya-vijñāna (Storehouse consciousness)

	•	The deep background consciousness.

	•	Stores karmic seeds (*bīja*), habits, impressions.

	•	All appearances emerge from this storehouse, like waves from the ocean.



The ālaya-vijñāna is neither a permanent soul nor annihilated at liberation—it is transformed (not erased) into wisdom.



3. Karma, Seeds, and Imprints (Bīja and Vāsanā)

	•	Every action, thought, and perception plants a seed (bīja) in the ālaya-vijñāna.

	•	These seeds ripen into future experiences and perceptions.

	•	What we take to be an “external world” is actually the unfolding of karmic seeds.



This links ontology to ethics and phenomenology—your reality is shaped by your mental and karmic conditioning.



4. Transformation and Liberation (Vijñāna-pariṇāma)

	•	Liberation comes not by destroying consciousness, but by transforming it:

	•	The eight consciousnesses are transformed into the five wisdoms of a Buddha.

	•	Especially, the ālaya is transformed into mirror-like wisdom (ādarsajñāna).

	•	The realization of non-duality, emptiness of self, and the dependent nature of experience brings bodhi (awakening).



5. Emptiness (Śūnyatā) in Yogācāra

	•	Yogācāra does affirm emptiness, but its focus is on how the illusion of duality arises.

	•	All three natures are ultimately empty of independent, self-existent nature (svabhāva).

	•	Emptiness in Yogācāra is ontological and cognitive—a correction of mistaken projections.



6. Critiques and Comparisons

A. Vs. Mādhyamaka

	•	Mādhyamaka emphasizes negation, Yogācāra offers a positive phenomenology of mind.

	•	Yogācāra often misunderstood as metaphysical idealism—some Yogācārins (like Sthiramati) explicitly deny that mind has independent reality.

	•	Later thinkers (like Śāntarakṣita) synthesized both in the Yogācāra-Mādhyamaka school.



B. Vs. Western Idealism

	•	Unlike Berkeleyan idealism, Yogācāra does not posit an individual mind creating the world.

	•	It’s closer to a non-dual phenomenology where distinctions between seer and seen dissolve.



7. Practical Implications

	•	Meditative focus on discerning how appearances arise in the mind.

	•	Deconstructing subject-object duality through insight.

	•	Training to transform perception through ethical and meditative purification of the storehouse.

	•	The path leads to the realization of the perfected nature, i.e., *non-conceptual gnosis* (*jñāna*) of ultimate reality.
