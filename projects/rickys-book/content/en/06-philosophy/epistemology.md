# Epistemology



1. Definition and Core Questions

Epistemology comes from the Greek *epistēmē* (knowledge) and *logos* (study or discourse). It asks:

	•	What is knowledge?

	•	How is knowledge acquired?

	•	What do people know?

	•	What justifies a belief?

	•	Can we know anything at all?

2. Types of Knowledge

There are several kinds of knowledge:

A. Propositional Knowledge (“knowing that”)

Knowledge of facts or truths.

E.g., “The Earth orbits the Sun.”

To know a proposition *P*, traditionally three conditions must be met (this is the Tripartite Theory of knowledge):

	1.	Belief – You believe P.

	2.	Truth – P is true.

	3.	Justification – You are justified in believing P.



B. Procedural Knowledge (“knowing how”)

Knowledge of how to do something.

E.g., how to ride a bike or play the piano.

C. Acquaintance Knowledge (“knowing by experience”)

Direct experience or familiarity.

E.g., knowing your hometown or a person.

3. Key Concepts

A. Belief

A mental attitude that something is the case. Not all beliefs are knowledge.

B. Truth

There are different theories of truth:

	•	Correspondence Theory: A statement is true if it corresponds to reality.

	•	Coherence Theory: A statement is true if it coheres with a set of beliefs.

	•	Pragmatic Theory: A statement is true if it works or is useful.

	•	Deflationary Theory: Saying “P is true” adds nothing; it’s just asserting P.



C. Justification

How do we know our belief is valid or reasonable?

Theories of Justification:

	•	Foundationalism: Some beliefs are basic and self-evident; others are built upon them.

	•	Coherentism: Beliefs are justified if they cohere with a system of beliefs.

	•	Reliabilism: Beliefs are justified if they are produced by a reliable process.

	•	Infinitism: Justification involves an infinite chain of reasons.



Foundational Assumptions of Yogācāra Epistemology

Yogācāra (“Practice of Yoga” or “School of Awareness”) is one of the major schools of Mahāyāna Buddhism, concerned with the nature of consciousness and perception. Its epistemology is primarily phenomenological and idealistic, meaning it emphasizes conscious experience and often denies that external reality exists independently of consciousness.



At its core, Yogācāra treats experience as layered and structured by consciousness itself, not by any independent, objective external world.



In Western terms, it’s somewhat comparable to:

	•	Phenomenology (Husserl, Merleau-Ponty) – where perception is grounded in lived experience.

	•	Transcendental Idealism (Kant) – where the world as we experience it is shaped by categories and forms inherent in our mind.

	•	German Idealism (Fichte, Schelling) – where the external world is an appearance of consciousness.

	•	Modern Constructivism & Cognitive Science – which explore how the mind actively constructs perception.



Three Modes of Knowledge and Their Validity

Yogācāra epistemologists, especially Dignāga and Dharmakīrti, advanced a sophisticated system with two valid sources of knowledge (they rejected testimony and induction as primary). These are:



1. Direct Perception

	•	Immediate, non-conceptual experience.

	•	Includes sensory experiences and internal mental events (like thoughts or emotions).

	•	Valid as a bare presentation of what appears, without conceptual overlay.

	•	Comparable to raw qualia or pre-reflective experience in phenomenology.



Western Analogy: Think of direct perception as raw sense data before being categorized (like in Locke or Russell), or pre-linguistic intuition in phenomenology.



2. Inferential Knowledge

	•	Conceptual and mediated.

	•	Comes from logical reasoning based on prior perception.

	•	Used to establish things not immediately perceivable (e.g., inferring fire from smoke).

	•	Inference is valid if it tracks a real causal relationship.

Western Analogy: This is similar to deductive reasoning or Kant’s idea of how we apply categories to organize sensory experience.



The Mind-Only Framework

Yogācāra is often described as “Mind-Only” (though this is a simplification). This doesn’t mean “nothing exists,” but that what we take as external reality is inseparable from how the mind projects and structures it.



Three Natures (translated roughly into English):

	1.	Imagined Nature – How we falsely impose subject-object duality (me vs. world).

	2.	Interdependent Nature – The flow of cause and effect in experience (how perceptions arise dependent on prior conditioning).

	3.	Perfected Nature – The realization that there is no duality, no “thing” apart from awareness.



Western Analogy:



	•	The “imagined nature” is like Kant’s empirical realism, where we project unity and categories onto the world.

	•	The “interdependent nature” is like causal conditioning or systems theory in cognitive science.

	•	The “perfected nature” resembles Heidegger’s notion of Being or non-dual awareness in some mystical traditions.



Consciousness Layers

Yogācāra proposes a model of layered consciousness:



1. Moment-to-Moment Awareness

	•	The stream of thoughts, sensations, emotions. It’s constantly shifting.

	•	Comparable to working memory or the phenomenal field in Western terms.



2. Judging or Structuring Mind

	•	Applies labels and concepts, creates the sense of “I” and “object.”

	•	Think of it like the cognitive ego, or Kant’s synthetic unity of apperception—the self that organizes experience.



3. Storehouse Consciousness (deepest layer)

	•	Holds the seeds (dispositions, tendencies) of all experiences.

	•	These seeds “ripen” into experiences in the upper layers.

	•	Not a permanent soul, but a continuity of impressions.

Western Analogy:



	•	This is similar to the unconscious in Freud or Jung, but more like a karmic database of tendencies.

	•	It also resembles connectionist models in cognitive science (like neural nets storing weights).



Error and Delusion in Yogācāra

For Yogācāra, most ordinary perception is deluded not because it’s hallucinating, but because it mistakes what is constructed for what is real.

	•	We think “I see a tree out there,” but that’s actually a mental construction based on prior impressions, conceptual labeling, and karmic seeds.

	•	Error is not in the data, but in how we interpret it as external, enduring, or separate.



Western Analogy: This is akin to Naïve Realism vs Critical Realism – the idea that our perceptions are not transparent windows to the world.



Liberation Through Epistemology

Yogācāra doesn’t study knowledge just for its own sake, but because false knowledge causes suffering.

	•	Realizing the non-duality of perception and consciousness leads to liberation.

	•	The path involves refining one’s understanding from deluded perception to purified perception.

	•	Eventually, the perceiving mind itself “quiets” and non-dual awareness is realized.



Western Analogy: In a very loose way, this resembles how in existential therapy or psychoanalysis, insight into false projections leads to freedom.
