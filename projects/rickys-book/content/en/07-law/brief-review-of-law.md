# Brief Review of Law



I. Definition of Law

At its core, law is a system of rules and principles established by a society or authority to regulate behavior, resolve disputes, and maintain order. But law is not monolithic—it exists on multiple levels:

	1.	Positive Law – Law enacted by human institutions (statutes, codes, constitutions).

	2.	Natural Law – The idea that laws exist in nature or reason and are discoverable through moral or logical insight.

	3.	Customary Law – Norms that develop over time through traditional practices.

	4.	Divine Law – Rules believed to be given by a deity (Sharia, Halakha, Canon Law).

	5.	Symbolic Law – Laws encoded within myth, ritual, and metaphysical cosmology (e.g., Ma’at in Egypt, Ṛta in Vedic India).



II. Core Functions of Law

	1.	Order and Predictability – Ensures stability in society.

	2.	Justice – Provides a mechanism for equity and rectifying wrongs.

	3.	Authority and Legitimacy – Upholds the power of the governing system.

	4.	Social Engineering – Shapes behavior and moral values over time.

	5.	Conflict Resolution – Provides avenues for dispute resolution (courts, tribunals).



III. Historical Development of Law

A. Ancient Legal Codes

	•	Code of Ur-Nammu (2100 BCE) – Earliest known law code from Sumer.

	•	Code of Hammurabi (1754 BCE) – Babylonian law, principle of “lex talionis” (eye for an eye).

	•	Ma’at (Egypt) – Divine order, truth, balance. The Pharaoh was the living judge of Ma’at.

	•	Dharmaśāstra (India) – Codes of righteous conduct, spiritual law, and duties.

	•	Twelve Tables (Rome) – First codification of Roman law (c. 450 BCE).

	•	Halakha (Jewish law) and Sharia (Islamic law) – Religious legal systems with spiritual and moral dimensions.



B. Classical Law

	•	Roman Law – Influenced civil law traditions; concepts like contracts, torts, persons, and property.

	•	Greek Law – Emphasized natural law, justice, and democratic procedures.

	•	Byzantine Law (Justinian Code) – Synthesized Roman legal knowledge into the *Corpus Juris Civilis*.



C. Medieval Law

	•	Canon Law – The law of the Catholic Church.

	•	Feudal Law – Based on obligations, oaths, and land tenure.

	•	Islamic Sharia – Derived from Qur’an, Hadith, Ijma (consensus), Qiyas (analogy).



D. Modern Legal Systems

	•	Civil Law – Originated in continental Europe; based on codified statutes (e.g., Napoleonic Code).

	•	Common Law – Developed in England; based on precedent (case law).

	•	Socialist Law – Emerged from Marxist-Leninist ideology; emphasizes state control.

	•	Customary Law – Continues in tribal societies and indigenous legal systems.

VI. Philosophical Foundations of Law

1. Natural Law

	•	Key Thinkers: Aristotle, Aquinas, Cicero

	•	Law reflects universal moral principles derived from reason or divinity.



2. Legal Positivism

	•	Key Thinkers: Jeremy Bentham, H.L.A. Hart

	•	Law is what the state enacts—morality is separate from legality.



3. Legal Realism

	•	Law is shaped by judges, society, and lived experience—not just texts.

4. Critical Legal Studies

	•	Law is not neutral; it reflects and reinforces social power structures (Marxist, Feminist, Postcolonial critiques).

5. Hermeneutic / Phenomenological Law

	•	Law as an interpretive system; meaning is derived from legal texts, context, and consciousness (Gadamer, Ricoeur).

VII. Symbolism and Metaphysics of Law

Symbols:

	•	Scales of Justice – Balance and impartiality.

	•	Sword – Coercive power.

	•	Blindfold – Objectivity, neutrality.

	•	Gavel – Authority of the judge.

	•	Crown / Throne / Tablets – Divine or sovereign legitimacy.



Esoteric Dimensions:

	•	In many traditions, law is seen as cosmic order made visible.

	•	*Ṛta* in Vedic thought (cosmic law, prefiguring Dharma).

	•	*Ma’at* in Egypt (balance, justice, cosmic harmony).

	•	Kabbalistic interpretations of Torah law as divine structuring of creation.



VIII. Law as a Social System

Niklas Luhmann’s theory sees law as an autopoietic system—self-producing and self-referential. Law isn’t just about control but about communication, boundary-making, and identity in a complex society.



IX. Law and Power

Law is not neutral—it’s often an instrument of:

	•	Class control (Marxism).

	•	Cultural hegemony (Gramsci).

	•	State sovereignty and legitimacy (Weber).

	•	Colonial and imperial expansion (Legal pluralism in colonial regimes).



But it can also be a tool for resistance, through:

	•	Civil disobedience.

	•	Strategic litigation.

	•	Legal activism and reform.



X. Evolutionary Trajectories and the Future of Law

	1.	Digital Law – Dealing with AI, data privacy, cybercrime.

	2.	Transnational Law – Blurring of national boundaries in commerce and rights.

	3.	Ecological Jurisprudence – Recognizing legal personhood of rivers, ecosystems (e.g., Whanganui River in NZ).

	4.	Restorative and Indigenous Justice – Healing-based systems challenging punitive models.

	5.	Algorithmic Law – Predictive policing, automated contracts (smart contracts), and ethical concerns in machine-governed systems.
