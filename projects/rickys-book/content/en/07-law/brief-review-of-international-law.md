# Brief Review of International Law



1. Definition and Nature of International Law

International law is a body of legal norms, rules, and standards that govern the relationships between sovereign states, international organizations, and, increasingly, individuals and corporations across national borders.



Key Characteristics:

	•	Decentralized: No global legislature or enforcement authority like in domestic systems.

	•	Consent-based: States are bound by international law primarily through consent, e.g., treaties.

	•	Horizontal system: Primarily regulates peer-to-peer relationships between sovereign states.

2. Sources of International Law (Article 38(1) of the ICJ Statute)

a. Treaties and Conventions

	•	Legally binding agreements between states or international organizations.

	•	Examples: UN Charter, Geneva Conventions, Paris Agreement.



b. Customary International Law

	•	Arises from consistent state practice accompanied by *opinio juris* (belief that the practice is legally obligatory).

	•	Example: The prohibition of torture, genocide, or piracy.



c. General Principles of Law

	•	Shared legal concepts recognized in most national legal systems.

	•	Examples: Good faith, pacta sunt servanda (agreements must be kept), due process.



d. Judicial Decisions and Scholarly Writings

	•	Subsidiary means for interpreting rules.

	•	Influence comes from courts like ICJ, ICTY, ICTR, and writings by scholars like Hugo Grotius or Antonio Cassese.



3. Historical Evolution

a. Classical Period (16th–19th centuries)

	•	Grotius (1583–1645): Father of modern international law.

	•	Focus on laws of war, sovereignty, diplomatic immunity.



b. Post-Westphalian Order (1648 onward)

	•	Treaty of Westphalia affirmed state sovereignty and non-interference.



c. League of Nations and Interwar Period

	•	Attempted collective security post-WWI; largely failed.

d. Post-1945: United Nations and Modern Framework

	•	UN Charter (1945) foundational.

	•	Rise of human rights law, decolonization, global economic governance.



4. Branches of International Law

a. Public International Law

	•	Governs relations between states and international organizations.



Sub-branches:

	•	Law of Treaties

	•	International Humanitarian Law (e.g., Geneva Conventions)

	•	Human Rights Law (e.g., Universal Declaration of Human Rights)

	•	Law of the Sea (e.g., UNCLOS)

	•	Diplomatic and Consular Law (e.g., Vienna Conventions)

	•	Environmental Law (e.g., Kyoto, Paris Agreements)

	•	Refugee and Migration Law

	•	Disarmament Law (e.g., NPT, CWC)

	•	Space Law (e.g., Outer Space Treaty)



b. Private International Law (Conflict of Laws)

	•	Determines jurisdiction, applicable law, and enforcement in cross-border private disputes.

	•	Example: International child custody, commercial disputes, torts.



c. Supranational Law

	•	Law made by entities above the nation-state, e.g., European Union law.

	•	Often has direct effect on individuals (unlike most international law).



5. International Legal Institutions

a. United Nations (UN) System

	•	Security Council: Peace and security; can authorize force.

	•	General Assembly: Non-binding resolutions, moral authority.

	•	International Court of Justice (ICJ): Disputes between states, advisory opinions.

	•	International Law Commission (ILC): Codifies and develops international law.



b. International Criminal Court (ICC)

	•	Prosecutes individuals for genocide, war crimes, crimes against humanity, and aggression.

	•	Independent from the UN; Rome Statute (2002).



c. Specialized Tribunals

	•	ICTY, ICTR, Special Court for Sierra Leone.

	•	Ad hoc, post-conflict justice mechanisms.



d. Arbitration Bodies

	•	International Centre for Settlement of Investment Disputes (ICSID)

	•	Permanent Court of Arbitration (PCA)

e. Human Rights Courts

	•	European Court of Human Rights (ECHR)

	•	Inter-American Court of Human Rights

	•	African Court on Human and Peoples’ Rights

6. Principles of International Law

	1.	Sovereign Equality of States

	2.	Non-Intervention

	3.	Prohibition of Use of Force

	4.	Peaceful Settlement of Disputes

	5.	Self-Determination of Peoples

	6.	International Cooperation

	7.	Respect for Human Rights and Humanitarian Law

7. Enforcement Mechanisms and Challenges

a. Diplomatic and Political Pressure

	•	Sanctions, shaming, public outcry.

b. International Courts

	•	Binding decisions, but depend on state compliance.



c. Domestic Incorporation

	•	Many states internalize international law into national legal systems.



d. Collective Enforcement

	•	Through UN Security Council, NATO, coalitions, or regional organizations.



Problems:

	•	Lack of enforcement powers

	•	Power asymmetry (great powers often avoid accountability)

	•	Selective application and double standards

8. Contemporary Issues and Debates

	•	Legitimacy and reform of the UN Security Council

	•	Universal jurisdiction vs. sovereignty (e.g., arrest warrants for leaders)

	•	Climate change litigation

	•	Cyberwarfare and international law

	•	Space and extraterrestrial governance

	•	International corporate accountability

	•	Colonialism and reparations debates

	•	Hybrid warfare and “lawfare”

	•	Rise of non-state actors (terrorist groups, militias, corporations)



9. Philosophical and Theoretical Perspectives

a. Natural Law

	•	International law grounded in universal moral principles (e.g., Grotius, Aquinas).



b. Positivism

	•	International law is derived from state consent and practice (e.g., Hans Kelsen).



c. Realism

	•	Law is power-driven; states follow it only when it aligns with interests (e.g., Morgenthau).



d. Constructivism

	•	Law is shaped by norms, identities, and shared understandings.



e. Critical Legal Studies / Third World Approaches (TWAIL)

	•	Challenges Eurocentric foundations; sees law as a tool of domination.



10. Future of International Law

	•	Increasing pressure for reform to reflect multipolarity.

	•	Enhanced role for AI, blockchain, and automated enforcement.

	•	Decolonial legal movements challenging foundational assumptions.

	•	Potential development of global constitutionalism or planetary law.
