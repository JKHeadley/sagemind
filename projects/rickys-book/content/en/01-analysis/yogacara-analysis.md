# Yogacara Analysis



1. Vijñapti-Mātra Analysis Protocol

(Cognition-Only Deconstruction Method)

Purpose:

To isolate and deconstruct cognitive projections and realize that all phenomena are manifestations of consciousness (*vijñapti-mātra*), not external independent realities.

Applicability:

Use when analyzing personal experiences, perceptions, or ideologies to uncover internal biases, constructed realities, and delusions of externality.

Materials Needed:

	•	A journal or digital note-taking tool

	•	A recent perception, experience, or mental image

	•	Quiet space for reflection or meditative absorption

Procedure:

Step 1: Isolate the Object of Experience

	•	Select a single moment or object of perception (e.g. “I felt anger during a conversation,” or “I saw a red car and felt anxious”).

	•	Write down all sensory and emotional components.

Step 2: Dissect the Perceptual Layers

	•	Separate raw sense data from added mental labels or assumptions.

	•	Raw: “Red shape moving fast.”

	•	Added: “A dangerous person is following me.”

	•	Identify any conceptual overlays, including personal or cultural conditioning.

Step 3: Identify Projected Content (Parikalpita)

	•	Ask: What in this experience is a mental imputation?

	•	Examples: Judgments, roles, symbols, assumptions of agency or intent.

Step 4: Analyze Dependent Origination (Paratantra)

	•	Trace the causes and conditions that led to this experience:

	•	Prior impressions (vāsanās)?

	•	Triggering events?

	•	Cultural or emotional frameworks?

Step 5: Meditate on Non-Dual Nature (Pariniṣpanna)

	•	Briefly rest in awareness of the experience’s constructed nature.

	•	Reflect: This object is neither entirely internal nor external—it is an interdependent manifestation of mind.

	•	Drop the subject-object split and rest in non-conceptual awareness if possible.

Notes:

	•	Repeat regularly with different experiences to build deeper insight.

	•	Works well when paired with *mindfulness of mental events* practices (*cittānupassanā*).

	•	Advanced practitioners may visualize the “fading” of the object into cognitive space.

2. Trisvabhāva Analysis Protocol

(Three Natures Method for Deconstructing Perceptions)

Purpose:

To systematically deconstruct any object of experience by analyzing it through the Three Natures: *Imputed* (Parikalpita), *Dependent* (Paratantra), and *Perfected* (Pariniṣpanna). This reveals the fabricated, contingent, and empty nature of phenomena.

Applicability:

Use for analyzing worldviews, persistent emotional reactions, philosophical positions, interpersonal conflicts, political ideologies, or religious beliefs.

Materials Needed:

	•	A written or mental target (e.g., a thought, belief, or situation)

	•	Journal or digital medium

	•	Quiet space for reflection

Procedure:

Step 1: Define the Object Clearly

	•	Write down or mentally hold the object of analysis.

Example: *“The belief that my self-worth depends on success.”*

Step 2: Analyze the Imputed Nature (Parikalpita-svabhāva)

*This is the wholly constructed, imagined, or falsely conceived aspect of the object.*

	•	Ask:

	•	What is being falsely projected here?

	•	Is this assumption based on language, identity, story, or duality?

	•	What concepts am I clinging to as real?

	•	Write out all conceptual overlays, identities, or narratives.

	•	Example: “Success is objectively valuable,” “Failure defines who I am.”

Step 3: Analyze the Dependent Nature (Paratantra-svabhāva)

*This is the object as a product of causes and conditions.*

	•	Ask:

	•	What dependent factors gave rise to this experience or belief?

	•	What psychological, social, or cultural causes shaped it?

	•	Is it sustained by specific habits, attachments, or memories?

	•	Map out the web of conditioning.

	•	Example: “This belief formed through parental expectations, schooling, past rewards, and traumas.”

Step 4: Realize the Perfected Nature (Pariniṣpanna-svabhāva)

*This is the empty, non-dual, and interdependent reality of the object—its true suchness.*

	•	Ask:

	•	Can this object be found apart from causes, concepts, or perception?

	•	Does it exist as a truly separate entity?

	•	What remains when conceptual overlays and causality are dropped?

	•	Rest the mind in this insight. Note that the object lacks independent existence and is empty of duality.

	•	Try to experience the object as a dynamic flow, rather than a fixed essence.

Optional Add-On: Use with Dialectics

	•	Argue *for* the object’s independent reality.

	•	Then systematically refute your own arguments using the Three Natures.

	•	This adds cognitive rigor and sharpens insight.

Notes:

	•	This method reveals that objects are not “things” but processes of consciousness.

	•	Repetition deepens the intuitive grasp of emptiness (*śūnyatā*).

	•	Advanced use: Combine with dream analysis, emotional reactions, or meditative visualization.



3. Eight Consciousness Mapping Protocol

(Layered Cognitive Analysis Using Yogācāra’s Mental Model)

Purpose:

To trace the formation of any thought, reaction, or experience through the eight layers of consciousness—unpacking surface perception, egoic grasping, and subconscious tendencies.

Applicability:

Ideal for analyzing dreams, sudden emotional reactions, behavioral patterns, ingrained beliefs, or trauma imprints. Especially useful in personal introspection, psycho-spiritual work, or advanced meditation.

Materials Needed:

	•	Journal or introspection tool

	•	A specific mental event or reaction

	•	A map or reference of the eight consciousnesses (can be memorized)

Procedure:

Step 1: Identify the Triggering Event

	•	Choose a specific event or experience:

e.g., *“I became irritated when someone interrupted me.”*

Step 2: Sensory Breakdown (1st to 5th Consciousness)

	•	Identify the raw sensory inputs:

	•	What did you see, hear, feel?

	•	What was the intensity of the sense impression?

	•	Ask: *Was there anything unusual about the sensory input itself?*

Step 3: Discursive Construction (6th – Manovijñāna)

	•	How did the thinking mind interpret this?

	•	What labels, stories, or judgments arose?

	•	Were memories or past experiences triggered?

	•	Example: “They don’t respect me,” “I always get cut off.”



Step 4: Ego-Filtering (7th – Kliṣṭa-manas)

	•	What sense of “I” was threatened or reinforced?

	•	Was there pride, shame, anxiety, possessiveness?

	•	Was this a habitual identity reaction?

	•	Ask: *Who is the “I” that felt irritated? What identity was at stake?*

Step 5: Latent Causes (8th – Ālayavijñāna)

	•	Reflect: What deep-seated karmic traces or habits (*vāsanās*) might have given rise to this reaction?

	•	Has this happened before? Is there a pattern?

	•	What conditions from the past are bearing fruit now?



Step 6: Re-integrate the Chain of Arising

	•	Map the experience as a causal cascade:

	1.	Sense data

	2.	Discursive interpretation

	3.	Egoic reactivity

	4.	Latent seeds ripening

	•	Acknowledge: *This mental event is not “mine” but a temporary process resulting from complex conditions.*

Step 7: Non-Identification Reflection

	•	Let the object or experience dissolve back into awareness.

	•	Reflect on the non-self nature (*anātman*) of all eight consciousnesses:

	•	They arise from causes, they are not eternal, and they are not “you.”



Notes:

	•	Use visual diagrams to trace repeated patterns across time.

	•	Works well with dream journaling or post-conflict reflection.

	•	Over time, you may begin to recognize when a reaction is seeded in the 8th consciousness vs. constructed in the 6th or 7th.

4. Yogic Perception Development Protocol

(Training Protocol for Non-Conceptual Insight)

Purpose:

To develop *yogipratyakṣa*, or direct yogic perception—non-conceptual, accurate awareness of reality as it truly is (*tathatā*). This transcends inferential analysis by cultivating deep intuitive insight into emptiness, interdependence, and cognition itself.

Applicability:

Best suited for practitioners seeking to move from intellectual understanding to direct realization. Useful for spiritual inquiry, insight meditation, and penetrating deeply held illusions.

Materials Needed:

	•	Meditation seat or quiet place

	•	Previous training in observation, mindfulness, or concentration

	•	Optionally: a guiding text (e.g., *Madhyānta-vibhāga*, *Yogācārabhūmi*, or *Śāntarakṣita*)

Procedure:

Step 1: Prepare the Ground with Inferential Analysis

	•	Choose a topic such as:

	•	*Impermanence (anitya)*

	•	*Non-self (anātman)*

	•	*Emptiness (śūnyatā)*

	•	*Mind-only (vijñapti-mātra)*

	•	Use logical inference or scriptural contemplation to get a preliminary conceptual grasp.

	•	Example: “All phenomena arise in dependence upon causes.”



Step 2: Settle the Mind (Samatha Foundation)

	•	Use breath or object-based meditation to calm and stabilize awareness.

	•	Aim for one-pointedness (*ekāgratā*) without strain.

	•	Duration: 10–30 minutes depending on experience.



Step 3: Introduce the Object of Insight (Vipassanā Entry)

	•	Gently bring the chosen topic into awareness—not by thinking *about* it, but by holding it like a lens or seed.

	•	Example: “Let me observe the impermanence of this breath, this thought, this emotion.”

	•	Observe without adding concepts—just witnessing.

Step 4: Observe the Arising and Dissolving of Experience

	•	Watch:

	•	How thoughts arise and fade.

	•	How sensations appear, change, vanish.

	•	How the sense of “I” flickers and reconstitutes.

	•	Do not interfere or explain. Just see.

Step 5: Dissolve Conceptual Structures

	•	Allow labels to fade:

	•	Let “breath” become sensation.

	•	Let “self” become just another object.

	•	Gently return attention to bare phenomena when narrative reappears.

Step 6: Penetrate Through Non-Dual Insight

	•	Eventually, distinctions like subject-object, perceiver-perceived may blur.

	•	Recognize:

	•	The observer is part of what is observed.

	•	There is no fixed standpoint—just flowing appearances within awareness.

Step 7: Rest in Direct Knowing

	•	Allow the mind to rest in this luminous, spacious clarity.

	•	This is *yogipratyakṣa*—knowing that is not mediated by thought, symbols, or inference.

	•	No need to describe or verify—just abide.



Notes:

	•	This practice can’t be forced; insight arises when conditions are right.

	•	Early signs of progress include:

	•	Reduced conceptual proliferation (*prapañca*)

	•	Spontaneous clarity

	•	Non-reactivity

	•	Eventually, this becomes the foundation for *pariniṣpanna-svabhāva* realization.

5. Dialectical Refutation & Affirmation Protocol

(Vitandā–Siddhānta Method for Deconstructing and Clarifying Views)

Purpose:

To test and refine philosophical, spiritual, or personal views by formally alternating between refutation (vitandā) and affirmation (siddhānta). The method sharpens clarity, exposes hidden assumptions, and uproots deep-seated cognitive distortions.



Applicability:

Use for challenging worldviews, analyzing dogmatic positions, confronting internal contradictions, or preparing for rigorous discourse. Useful for both inner work and formal dialectic (debate, writing, etc.).

Materials Needed:

	•	Statement or thesis to analyze

	•	Pen and paper (or digital workspace)

	•	Familiarity with basic Yogācāra terminology and logical forms

Procedure:

Step 1: Formulate the Provisional Thesis (Abhyupagama)

	•	Choose a belief or view you currently hold or wish to analyze.

	•	Examples:

	•	“Consciousness ceases at death.”

	•	“The self is a permanent entity.”

	•	“Only sensory data are real.”

	•	Write it as clearly and precisely as possible.

	•	This becomes your target proposition.



Step 2: Enter the Role of Refuter (Vitandāvādin)

	•	Argue only to refute, not to propose an alternative.

	•	Examine the proposition for:

	•	Self-contradictions

	•	Unstated assumptions

	•	Dependence on conceptual projections (Parikalpita)

	•	Neglect of conditionality (Paratantra)

	•	Ignorance of emptiness (Pariniṣpanna)

	•	Use Yogācāra insights to critique:

	•	Is the proposition dualistic?

	•	Does it reify a conceptual construct?

	•	Does it rely on a flawed epistemic source (e.g., unexamined memory)?

Optional: Use Classical Tools

	•	Apply *pramāṇas* (means of knowledge): perception (*pratyakṣa*), inference (*anumāna*), scriptural authority (*śabda*).

	•	Test whether the view can be sustained under these lenses.

Step 3: Switch to Affirmation (Siddhānta)

	•	Reformulate or clarify the original view—or offer a more refined one.

	•	Your goal now is to state what can be affirmed from a Yogācāra standpoint.

	•	What survives rigorous analysis?

	•	What accords with direct perception and dependent origination?

	•	Express it as:

	•	A clarified insight (e.g., “What we call ‘self’ is a stream of karmic imprints manifesting as consciousness.”)

	•	A non-dual formulation (e.g., “There is cognition, but no inherent cognizer.”)



Step 4: Recursive Deepening

	•	Repeat Steps 2 and 3 multiple times.

	•	With each cycle:

	•	Refine your logic.

	•	Cut subtler assumptions.

	•	Move closer to conceptual silence and direct seeing.



Step 5: Return to Emptiness (Śūnyatā-Saṃjñā)

	•	End the session by intentionally dissolving all formulations—true or false—into emptiness-awareness.

	•	Reflect:

	•	Even valid affirmations are skillful means.

	•	The ultimate view is non-abiding in any view (*sarva-dṛṣṭi-pratiṣṭhāna*).



Notes:

	•	Pairs beautifully with the Trisvabhāva and Yogic Perception protocols.

	•	Use Socratic questioning or Madhyamaka-style reductios to amplify depth.

	•	Practice in dyads or writing exercises to simulate live debate.
