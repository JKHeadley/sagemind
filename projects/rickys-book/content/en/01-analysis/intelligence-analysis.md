# Intelligence Analysis



1. Structured Analytic Techniques (SATs)

Definition

Structured Analytic Techniques (SATs) are formalized methods designed to improve analytic reasoning, reduce cognitive bias, and produce more rigorous, transparent, and justifiable conclusions in complex decision-making.

Origins

	•	Developed within the intelligence community, especially after major failures like the Iraq WMD fiasco.

	•	Pioneered by Richards J. Heuer (author of *Psychology of Intelligence Analysis*), among others.

	•	Influenced by decision science, cognitive psychology, and systems theory.



Goals

	•	Improve objectivity and transparency in analysis.

	•	Counter cognitive biases (confirmation bias, availability heuristic, mirror imaging).

	•	Foster collaborative thinking and structured dissent.



Here’s a decision tree to help you choose the right Structured Analytic Technique (SAT) depending on your situation, cognitive needs, and analytic goals. This will walk you through your intent, the nature of the problem, and the type of reasoning required.



STRUCTURED ANALYTIC TECHNIQUE DECISION TREE

1. What is the primary purpose of your analysis?

A. To break down a complex problem or visualize relationships → Go to Step 2

B. To generate new ideas or explore different perspectives → Go to Step 3

C. To test competing explanations or hypotheses → Go to Step 4

D. To challenge current thinking or assumptions → Go to Step 5

E. To anticipate the future or forecast possibilities → Go to Step 6

F. To support or justify a decision → Go to Step 7

Step 2: Problem Decomposition / Visualization

Ask: Do you need to map actors, influences, or causal chains?

	•	Yes → Use:

	•	Mind Mapping (idea organization)

	•	Link Analysis (relationships/networks)

	•	Argument Mapping (claims and evidence)

	•	Cross-Impact Matrix (interactions of variables)

	•	Key Assumptions Check (foundational beliefs)



Step 3: Idea Generation

Ask: Are you stuck in one perspective or trying to imagine alternatives?

	•	Yes → Use:

	•	Brainstorming (free-form ideation)

	•	Red Teaming (adversary perspective)

	•	Devil’s Advocacy (structured dissent)

	•	Team A / Team B (dual competitive analysis)

	•	Outside-In Thinking (external drivers)



Step 4: Hypothesis Evaluation

Ask: Do you need to assess the truth or likelihood of multiple explanations?

	•	Yes → Use:

	•	Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)

	•	Diagnostic Reasoning (most differentiating evidence)

	•	Indicators & Signposts (monitoring future signals)

	•	High-Impact / Low-Probability Analysis (worst-case thinking)



Step 5: Challenge Analysis

Ask: Do you suspect blind spots, bad data, or wish to stress-test your thinking?

	•	Yes → Use:

	•	Premortem Analysis (assume failure and trace it back)

	•	Quality of Information Check (reliability & credibility)

	•	Structured Self-Critique (bias awareness)

	•	Four Ways of Seeing (empathetic/relational perspective)



Step 6: Scenario Generation / Forecasting

Ask: Are you trying to prepare for multiple possible futures?

	•	Yes → Use:

	•	What-If Analysis (single variable change)

	•	Cone of Plausibility (plausible range of futures)

	•	Backcasting (future → present pathway)

	•	Alternative Futures Analysis (based on key drivers)



Step 7: Decision Support

Ask: Are you choosing between multiple options or policies?

	•	Yes → Use:

	•	Decision Matrix (options ranked by weighted criteria)

	•	Force Field Analysis (supporting/opposing pressures)

	•	Trade-Off Analysis (risk-benefit breakdown)

	•	Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) (quantitative scoring)



Bonus: Cross-Cutting Enhancements

	•	Use Key Assumptions Check early in any project.

	•	Apply Red Teaming to stress-test any forecast or strategy.

	•	Combine ACH + Indicators for real-time monitoring systems.



I. DECOMPOSITION & VISUALIZATION TECHNIQUES

1. Mind Mapping

Purpose: Visually organize ideas, actors, or events related to a central topic.



Steps:

	1.	Write the central concept in the center of a blank page.

	2.	Branch outward with major categories or themes.

	3.	Add sub-branches with more specific details or related elements.

	4.	Use color coding, images, or links to show relationships.

	5.	Continuously expand and reorganize as needed.



Use for: Organizing preliminary data, brainstorming, or breaking down large questions.



2. Link Analysis

Purpose: Map out relationships and flows between people, groups, or entities.



Steps:

	1.	Identify all entities (people, organizations, events).

	2.	Draw nodes (entities) and edges (relationships).

	3.	Label relationship types (financial, familial, command structure, etc.).

	4.	Look for hubs, bridges, clusters—critical elements.

	5.	Analyze for centrality, vulnerability, or control points.



Use for: Criminal networks, terrorism, supply chains, or influence operations.



3. Argument Mapping

Purpose: Deconstruct and evaluate the logic of an argument.



Steps:

	1.	Identify the main conclusion.

	2.	Break down the supporting premises.

	3.	Connect evidence to premises and premises to the conclusion.

	4.	Identify counterarguments or assumptions.

	5.	Evaluate logical consistency and sufficiency of support.



Use for: Legal analysis, academic debates, policy rationale.



4. Key Assumptions Check

Purpose: Uncover and critically evaluate underlying beliefs.



Steps:

	1.	List all assumptions in the analysis (implicit and explicit).

	2.	For each:

	•	Is it necessary?

	•	Is it valid (based on facts)?

	•	Could it be wrong? What if it is?

	3.	Prioritize those that are most critical and least certain.

	4.	Explore implications if assumptions fail.



Use for: Early-stage scoping or prior to finalizing judgments.



5. Cross-Impact Matrix

Purpose: Identify how different variables influence each other.



Steps:

	1.	List key variables/events along rows and columns of a grid.

	2.	For each cell, assess how the row variable influences the column variable (e.g., ++, +, 0, –, ––).

	3.	Look for cascading effects or feedback loops.

	4.	Prioritize high-impact interactions for monitoring or intervention.



Use for: Systems thinking, scenario planning, crisis simulations.



II. IDEA GENERATION TECHNIQUES

1. Brainstorming

Purpose: Rapidly generate diverse ideas.



Steps:

	1.	Define a clear focus question.

	2.	Generate ideas without evaluation or censorship.

	3.	Record every idea.

	4.	Group or cluster related ideas.

	5.	Evaluate and refine ideas after the session.



Use for: Early-stage ideation, problem scoping.



2. Red Team Analysis

Purpose: Role-play adversaries or critics to expose blind spots.



Steps:

	1.	Define your own position or assumption.

	2.	Form a “Red Team” that adopts an alternative or opposing perspective.

	3.	Red Team develops a critique or scenario from the alternative view.

	4.	Compare both views and identify gaps or vulnerabilities.

	5.	Adjust strategies or conclusions accordingly.



Use for: War gaming, corporate strategy, policy debates.



3. Devil’s Advocacy

Purpose: Intentionally challenge the dominant view.



Steps:

	1.	Assign someone the role of Devil’s Advocate.

	2.	They present the strongest possible critique of the prevailing conclusion.

	3.	Analysts respond to the critique with evidence or counterarguments.

	4.	Reassess whether the original conclusion still holds.



Use for: Stress-testing confidence in judgments.



4. Outside-In Thinking

Purpose: Identify external forces that could affect the system.



Steps:

	1.	Define the internal system or problem.

	2.	List external influences (e.g., politics, weather, culture, law).

	3.	Explore how each external factor could affect the system.

	4.	Assess likelihood and impact.



Use for: Vulnerability analysis, systems thinking.



5. Team A / Team B Analysis

Purpose: Competing analyses to surface differing assumptions.



Steps:

	1.	Create two or more independent analytic teams.

	2.	Each team works independently on the same problem.

	3.	Teams brief their findings to a neutral panel.

	4.	Facilitate comparison and synthesis of divergent views.



Use for: High-stakes intelligence or military assessments.

1. Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)

(See previous message for full breakdown.)

2. Diagnostic Reasoning

Purpose: Evaluate evidence by how well it distinguishes among hypotheses.



Steps:

	1.	List all hypotheses and relevant evidence.

	2.	Identify which pieces of evidence discriminate between them.

	3.	Rate the diagnosticity of each piece (High, Medium, Low).

	4.	Give greater weight to high-diagnostic evidence.

	5.	Identify which hypothesis is most consistent with key differentiators.



Use for: Intelligence, law enforcement, medical diagnostics.



3. Indicators and Signposts

Purpose: Monitor ongoing events for signs supporting a hypothesis.



Steps:

	1.	Define a hypothesis or scenario.

	2.	Identify observable indicators that would occur if it were true.

	3.	Rank indicators by specificity and diagnostic power.

	4.	Create a signpost tracking system.

	5.	Update regularly to track evolving developments.



Use for: Strategic warning, policy forecasting.



4. High Impact / Low Probability (HILP) Analysis

Purpose: Explore “Black Swan” events.



Steps:

	1.	Identify high-impact outcomes (e.g., war, collapse).

	2.	Explore unlikely causes or paths to those outcomes.

	3.	Evaluate early warning signs and vulnerabilities.

	4.	Develop mitigation strategies or contingency plans.



Use for: Crisis planning, disaster risk analysis.



IV. CHALLENGE ANALYSIS TECHNIQUES

1. Quality of Information Check

Purpose: Evaluate evidence for credibility and relevance.



Steps:

	1.	List each piece of evidence used.

	2.	Assess source credibility (provenance, track record).

	3.	Assess information reliability (direct/indirect, corroborated?).

	4.	Flag low-quality evidence that may distort the analysis.



Use for: Intelligence vetting, legal evidence review.



2. Premortem Analysis

Purpose: Assume failure and work backward to prevent it.



Steps:

	1.	Assume the decision has failed in the future.

	2.	Ask: “What went wrong?”

	3.	List all possible contributing factors.

	4.	Identify which are plausible and preventable.

	5.	Adjust plan or strategy to mitigate risks.



Use for: Strategic planning, operational design.



3. Structured Self-Critique

Purpose: Identify personal or group biases in analysis.



Steps:

	1.	Review your analytic process step-by-step.

	2.	Ask: What biases might have influenced each step?

	3.	Identify where you may have overlooked alternatives or dismissed counterevidence.

	4.	Invite external reviewers or peers to challenge your logic.



Use for: Final-stage assessment of analysis.



4. Four Ways of Seeing

Purpose: Explore perceptions between opposing actors.



Steps:

	1.	Identify two actors (e.g., Country A and Country B).

	2.	Map:

	•	How A sees A

	•	How A sees B

	•	How B sees B

	•	How B sees A

	3.	Analyze differences, blind spots, and misperceptions.

	4.	Use to improve diplomacy or conflict strategy.



Use for: International relations, negotiations.



V. SCENARIO GENERATION TECHNIQUES

1. What-If Analysis

Purpose: Explore impact of a change in a key variable.



Steps:

	1.	Identify a single variable and a hypothetical change.

	2.	Ask: “What happens if X changes?” (e.g., what if leader is assassinated?)

	3.	Project consequences through relevant domains.

	4.	Consider cascading impacts.



Use for: Crisis simulation, counterfactual analysis.



2. Cone of Plausibility

Purpose: Explore a range of plausible futures.



Steps:

	1.	Define the current baseline.

	2.	Identify driving forces and uncertainties.

	3.	Construct multiple scenarios from likely to wildcard.

	4.	Position them in a “cone” from present to future.

	5.	Monitor for movement toward any path.



Use for: Strategic forecasting, future planning.



3. Backcasting

Purpose: Work backward from a desired future outcome.



Steps:

	1.	Define a future state or goal.

	2.	Ask: “What needed to happen before this?”—and repeat.

	3.	Map a step-by-step timeline backwards to the present.

	4.	Identify key decisions and tipping points.



Use for: Mission planning, long-term strategic vision.



4. Alternative Futures Analysis

Purpose: Develop radically different, plausible futures.



Steps:

	1.	Identify 2–3 major drivers of change.

	2.	Determine how these drivers might vary or interact.

	3.	Combine them into 4 distinct scenario frameworks.

	4.	Develop detailed narratives for each.

	5.	Explore implications and signposts.



Use for: Policy design under uncertainty.



VI. DECISION SUPPORT TECHNIQUES

1. Decision Matrix

Purpose: Compare options using weighted criteria.



Steps:

	1.	List options as rows, criteria as columns.

	2.	Assign weights to each criterion based on importance.

	3.	Score each option on each criterion.

	4.	Multiply score × weight, sum for each option.

	5.	Compare totals.



Use for: Resource allocation, strategic choices.



2. Force Field Analysis

Purpose: Identify forces for and against a change or decision.



Steps:

	1.	Define the change or decision.

	2.	List driving forces and restraining forces.

	3.	Evaluate strength of each.

	4.	Identify ways to strengthen drivers or weaken resistors.



Use for: Organizational change, reform planning.



3. Trade-Off Analysis

Purpose: Clarify costs and benefits of competing options.



Steps:

	1.	List key trade-offs (e.g., cost vs speed, security vs privacy).

	2.	For each option, assess how it handles each trade-off.

	3.	Consider risk tolerance and decision context.

	4.	Choose option that best balances critical needs.



Use for: Policy, military, or business decisions.



4. Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA)

Purpose: Quantitatively assess options across many dimensions.



Steps:

	1.	Define criteria and assign weights.

	2.	Score each option quantitatively.

	3.	Normalize scores (e.g., scale 0–1), multiply by weights.

	4.	Sum for each option.

	5.	Conduct sensitivity analysis to test for robustness.



Use for: Formal policy decisions, resource prioritization.
