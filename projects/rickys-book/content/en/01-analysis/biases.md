# Biases



Here’s a detailed breakdown of key cognitive biases — mental shortcuts or distortions that can influence how we perceive, interpret, and remember information. These biases are often categorized by their function or domain (e.g., memory, decision-making, social cognition).



1. Memory Biases

These affect how we recall past events.

	•	Confirmation Bias: Tendency to remember or search for information that confirms one’s preconceptions.

	•	*Example:* Remembering only the times a superstition “worked” and forgetting when it didn’t.

	•	Hindsight Bias (“I-knew-it-all-along effect”): Believing, after an event, that it was predictable.

	•	*Example:* Saying you knew a company would fail after it collapses.

	•	Consistency Bias: Incorrectly remembering one’s past attitudes or behavior as more similar to the present.

	•	*Example:* Thinking you always held the same political beliefs you have now.

	•	Rosy Retrospection: Remembering the past as more positive than it was.

	•	*Example:* Believing a stressful vacation was “the best time ever.”

	•	Misattribution: Attributing a memory to the wrong source.

	•	*Example:* Thinking you read something in a book when you actually heard it on a podcast.



2. Decision-Making Biases

These influence our choices and judgments.

	•	Anchoring Bias: Relying too heavily on the first piece of information encountered.

	•	*Example:* A product seems cheaper if it’s marked down from a higher “original” price.

	•	Availability Heuristic: Estimating the likelihood of events based on how easily they come to mind.

	•	*Example:* Thinking plane crashes are common because they’re often in the news.

	•	Loss Aversion: The pain of losing is psychologically more powerful than the pleasure of gaining.

	•	*Example:* Refusing to sell a losing stock because the loss feels too painful.

	•	Status Quo Bias: Preferring things to remain the same.

	•	*Example:* Sticking with a bad service provider just because switching feels like a hassle.

	•	Sunk Cost Fallacy: Continuing a behavior due to previously invested resources (time, money, effort).

	•	*Example:* Finishing a bad movie because you’ve already watched an hour of it.

	•	Framing Effect: Decisions are influenced by how information is presented.

	•	*Example:* People are more likely to choose a surgery if told it has a 90% survival rate than a 10% mortality rate.



3. Attribution Biases

These affect how we assign causes to others’ behavior.

	•	Fundamental Attribution Error: Overemphasizing personal traits while underestimating situational factors.

	•	*Example:* Assuming someone is rude because they’re a bad person, not because they’re having a bad day.

	•	Actor-Observer Bias: Attributing our own actions to situations but others’ actions to their character.

	•	*Example:* “I was late because of traffic; he was late because he’s irresponsible.”

	•	Self-Serving Bias: Attributing successes to internal factors and failures to external ones.

	•	*Example:* “I passed the test because I’m smart; I failed because the test was unfair.”



4. Social and Group Biases

	•	Ingroup Bias: Favoring members of one’s own group.

	•	*Example:* Thinking your team’s bad behavior is justified, but the other team’s is not.

	•	Outgroup Homogeneity Effect: Believing people in outgroups are more similar to each other than they really are.

	•	*Example:* Assuming “all [insert group] are the same.”

	•	Halo Effect: One positive trait (like attractiveness) influences overall judgment.

	•	*Example:* Thinking someone is smart because they’re good-looking.

	•	False Consensus Effect: Overestimating how much others agree with your beliefs or behavior.

	•	*Example:* Assuming everyone loves the same movie you do.



5. Belief and Perception Biases

	•	Confirmation Bias (again, but crucial here too): Seeking info that supports what we already believe.

	•	Cognitive Dissonance: Mental discomfort from holding conflicting beliefs or behaviors, often leading to self-justification.

	•	*Example:* A smoker downplaying health risks to justify smoking.

	•	Negativity Bias: Negative events have a stronger psychological impact than positive ones.

	•	*Example:* One bad review ruins your perception of an otherwise well-reviewed product.

	•	Optimism Bias: Believing you’re less likely than others to experience negative events.

	•	*Example:* “It won’t happen to me.”



6. Statistical and Probability Biases

	•	Base Rate Neglect: Ignoring general probability in favor of specific information.

	•	*Example:* Assuming someone is a librarian because they’re quiet, ignoring the base rate of librarians in the population.

	•	Gambler’s Fallacy: Believing that past events affect the probability of future independent events.

	•	*Example:* Thinking a roulette wheel is “due” for red after a streak of blacks.

	•	Hot-Hand Fallacy: Believing someone is “on a roll” even in random processes.

	•	*Example:* Thinking a basketball player will keep scoring because they’ve made several shots.
