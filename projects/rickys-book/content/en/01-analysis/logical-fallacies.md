# Logical Fallacies



Here’s a comprehensive list and breakdown of logical fallacies, organized by type. This includes both formal and informal fallacies, with examples and explanations for each.

I. FORMAL FALLACIES

These occur when there’s an error in the logical structure of an argument, regardless of the content.

1. Affirming the Consequent

	•	Form: If A, then B. B, therefore A.

	•	Example: If it rains, the ground gets wet. The ground is wet, so it must have rained.

	•	Issue: Other causes could make the ground wet.



2. Denying the Antecedent

	•	Form: If A, then B. Not A, therefore not B.

	•	Example: If I study, I will pass. I didn’t study, so I won’t pass.

	•	Issue: You might pass through other means.



3. Undistributed Middle

	•	Form: All A are C. All B are C. Therefore, all A are B.

	•	Example: All dogs are animals. All cats are animals. Therefore, all dogs are cats.

	•	Issue: Middle term (“animals”) isn’t distributed properly.



II. INFORMAL FALLACIES

These arise from the content or context rather than structure.

A. FALLACIES OF RELEVANCE

Arguments that appeal to irrelevant information.

1. Ad Hominem

	•	Attacks the person instead of the argument.

	•	*“You’re wrong because you’re a fool.”*

2. Tu Quoque (You Too)

	•	Accuses the opponent of hypocrisy.

	•	*“You say smoking is bad, but you smoke too.”*

3. Appeal to Authority (Argumentum ad Verecundiam)

	•	Uses authority instead of evidence.

	•	*“A Nobel Prize winner said it, so it must be true.”*

4. Appeal to Emotion

	•	Manipulates emotion instead of reason.

	•	*“Think of the children!”*

5. Appeal to Popularity (Ad Populum)

	•	Argues something is true because it’s widely believed.

	•	*“Everyone believes it, so it must be true.”*

6. Appeal to Tradition

	•	Asserts something is right because it’s always been done that way.

	•	*“We’ve always done it this way.”*

7. Appeal to Force (Ad Baculum)

	•	Uses threat or coercion instead of logic.

	•	*“Agree with me or you’ll lose your job.”*

8. Genetic Fallacy

	•	Judges something based on its origin.

	•	*“That idea came from a corrupt person, so it’s false.”*

B. FALLACIES OF AMBIGUITY

1. Equivocation

	•	Uses a word in two different senses.

	•	*“A feather is light. What is light cannot be dark. So, a feather cannot be dark.”*

2. Amphiboly

	•	Ambiguity due to grammatical structure.

	•	*“I saw the man with the telescope.” (Who has the telescope?)*

3. Accent

	•	Changes meaning based on emphasis.

	•	*“We should not speak ill of our friends.”*

4. Composition

	•	Assumes what’s true of parts is true of the whole.

	•	*“Each brick is small, so the building is small.”*

5. Division

	•	Assumes what’s true of the whole is true of parts.

	•	*“The team is the best, so each player is the best.”*

C. FALLACIES OF PRESUMPTION

1. Begging the Question (Petitio Principii)

	•	Assumes the conclusion in the premises.

	•	*“God exists because the Bible says so, and the Bible is true because God wrote it.”*

2. Complex Question

	•	A loaded question that assumes something unproven.

	•	*“Have you stopped cheating on tests?”*

3. False Dilemma (False Dichotomy)

	•	Presents two options when others exist.

	•	*“You’re either with us or against us.”*

4. Slippery Slope

	•	Argues one step will lead to extreme outcomes.

	•	*“If we allow gay marriage, next people will marry animals.”*

5. False Cause (Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc)

	•	Assumes causation from sequence.

	•	*“The rooster crowed, then the sun rose. So, the rooster caused the sun to rise.”*

6. Hasty Generalization

	•	Makes a broad claim based on few examples.

	•	*“I met two rude people from New York, so all New Yorkers are rude.”*

7. Faulty Analogy

	•	Compares things that aren’t similar in relevant ways.

	•	*“Employees are like nails. Just as nails must be hit to work, so must employees.”*

8. Straw Man

	•	Misrepresents an argument to refute it.

	•	*“You want better health care? So you want a socialist dictatorship.”*

9. No True Scotsman

	•	Redefines a group to exclude counterexamples.

	•	*“No Scotsman puts sugar on porridge.” “But my uncle is a Scotsman and he does.” “Then he’s not a true Scotsman.”*

10. Circular Reasoning

	•	Argument repeats the conclusion as a premise.

	•	*“I’m trustworthy because I said I am.”*

11. Appeal to Ignorance (Ad Ignorantiam)

	•	Claims something is true because it hasn’t been proven false (or vice versa).

	•	*“No one has proven aliens don’t exist, so they must exist.”*

12. Misleading Statistics

	•	Uses biased or flawed data to mislead.

	•	*“This medicine works 90% of the time!” (But only tested on 10 people.)*
