# Brief Review of Music Theory



I. Foundations of Music Theory

1. Sound Basics

	•	Pitch: The highness or lowness of a sound (measured in Hertz).

	•	Frequency: Vibrations per second. Higher frequency = higher pitch.

	•	Timbre: Tone color or quality that distinguishes instruments.

	•	Dynamics: Volume (e.g., *piano* = soft, *forte* = loud).

	•	Duration: How long a note is held.



2. The Musical Alphabet

	•	Notes: A B C D E F G (repeating cyclically)

	•	Sharps (#) raise a note by a semitone; flats (b) lower it.



3. Intervals

	•	Distance between two notes.

	•	Measured in half steps (semitones) and whole steps.

	•	Examples:

	•	Minor 2nd = 1 semitone

	•	Major 2nd = 2 semitones

	•	Perfect 5th = 7 semitones

II. Scales and Keys

1. Major Scale

Formula: W-W-H-W-W-W-H

Example (C major): C-D-E-F-G-A-B-C

Has a “happy” sound.

2. Natural Minor Scale

Formula: W-H-W-W-H-W-W

Example (A minor): A-B-C-D-E-F-G-A

Has a “sad” sound.

3. Harmonic and Melodic Minor

	•	Harmonic Minor raises the 7th note of the natural minor.

	•	Melodic Minor raises both the 6th and 7th ascending but reverts to natural minor descending.

4. Key Signatures

	•	Identify the scale a piece is in.

	•	Sharps or flats are placed on the staff to show the key.

	•	Circle of Fifths is a tool to visualize key relationships.



III. Chords and Harmony

1. Chords

	•	Triads: 3-note chords (root, third, fifth)

	•	Major: root + major 3rd + perfect 5th

	•	Minor: root + minor 3rd + perfect 5th

	•	Diminished: minor 3rd + diminished 5th

	•	Augmented: major 3rd + augmented 5th

	•	Seventh Chords: Add a 7th above the root

	•	Major 7th (maj7), Minor 7th (min7), Dominant 7th (7)



2. Chord Progressions

	•	Common sequences:

	•	I–IV–V–I (classical & pop)

	•	ii–V–I (jazz)

	•	vi–IV–I–V (modern pop)

	•	Functional Harmony: Chords serve roles—Tonic (I), Subdominant (IV), Dominant (V).



IV. Rhythm and Meter

1. Note Values

	•	Whole, Half, Quarter, Eighth, Sixteenth

	•	Dots and ties modify duration.

2. Time Signatures

	•	Top number: beats per measure

	•	Bottom number: type of note that gets the beat

	•	4/4 = common time

	•	3/4 = waltz time

	•	6/8 = compound meter

3. Syncopation and Polyrhythm

	•	Syncopation: stressing weak beats

	•	Polyrhythm: multiple rhythms happening simultaneously

V. Modes and Advanced Scales

1. Modes

	•	Derived from the major scale by starting on different degrees:

	•	Ionian (Major)

	•	Dorian

	•	Phrygian

	•	Lydian

	•	Mixolydian

	•	Aeolian (Minor)

	•	Locrian

2. Pentatonic and Blues Scales

	•	Pentatonic: 5-note scale (e.g., C-D-E-G-A)

	•	Blues: minor pentatonic + “blue note” (e.g., flat 5)

3. Exotic Scales

	•	Whole Tone, Octatonic, Byzantine, Indian Ragas, etc.

	•	Useful in jazz, world music, and avant-garde composition.

VI. Counterpoint and Voice Leading

1. Counterpoint

	•	The art of combining independent melodies.

	•	Species counterpoint (Palestrina style) uses strict rules.

2. Voice Leading

	•	Smooth movement of chord tones.

	•	Avoid parallel 5ths/8ths in traditional harmony.

VII. Form and Structure

1. Common Forms

	•	Binary (AB), Ternary (ABA)

	•	Sonata Form: Exposition, Development, Recapitulation

	•	Rondo: ABACADA

	•	Fugue: Based on imitative counterpoint

2. Popular Forms

	•	Verse-Chorus

	•	12-bar blues

	•	AABA (Tin Pan Alley)

VIII. Modern Music Theory Concepts

1. Jazz Harmony

	•	Extended chords: 9ths, 11ths, 13ths

	•	Modal interchange and substitutions

	•	Improvisation using modes and scales

2. Serialism and Atonality

	•	12-tone row (Schoenberg)

	•	No tonal center

3. Set Theory and Neo-Riemannian Theory

	•	Tools to analyze post-tonal and contemporary music.

IX. Notation and Symbols

1. Staff Notation

	•	Treble and bass clefs, ledger lines

	•	Accidentals: sharp (#), flat (b), natural

2. Chord Symbols

	•	Cmaj7, G7, Am, Dm7b5

3. Dynamics and Articulations

	•	Crescendo, decrescendo, staccato, legato, accents

X. Ear Training and Application

1. Intervals and Chords by Ear

	•	Solfege (Do-Re-Mi)

	•	Recognizing cadences (Perfect, Plagal, Deceptive)

2. Transcription and Analysis

	•	Writing down what you hear

	•	Analyzing form, harmony, rhythm in existing music
