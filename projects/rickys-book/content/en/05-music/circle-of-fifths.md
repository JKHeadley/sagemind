# Circle of Fifths



The Rule of Fifths—often called the Circle of Fifths—is a foundational concept in music theory that reveals the relationships among the 12 tones of the chromatic scale, their corresponding key signatures, and how keys relate harmonically. It is essential for understanding harmony, modulation, and composition.



1. What is a “Fifth”?

A perfect fifth is an interval spanning five notes in a diatonic scale. For example:

	•	From C to G is a perfect fifth:

	•	C–D–E–F–G → 5 scale degrees.



This interval has a frequency ratio of 3:2, making it one of the most consonant intervals after the octave.

4. Enharmonic Equivalents

	•	B major = C♭ major

	•	F# major = G♭ major

	•	C# major = D♭ major

These keys sound the same but are written differently depending on context.

5. Uses of the Rule of Fifths

a. Key Signature Memorization

Helps you quickly determine how many sharps or flats a key has.

b. Modulation

Modulating to keys a fifth apart sounds natural and smooth. Common modulations:

	•	C major → G major (up a fifth)

	•	A minor → E minor (up a fifth)



Modulation to closely related keys (within 1–2 steps on the circle) maintains harmonic coherence.

c. Chord Progressions

Progressions based on fifths sound strong and directional. Common examples:

	•	Cycle of fifths progression: D7 → G7 → C7 → F7 → Bb7

	•	This is the basis for turnarounds, jazz progressions, and baroque sequences.



d. Harmonic Function and Tension

Descending fifths (e.g. V → I) resolve tension effectively. For example:

	•	G7 → C major (V7 → I)



This is the cadential function and is central to functional harmony.



6. Circle of Fifths in Composition

	•	In classical composition, composers often move around the circle to create narrative tension.

	•	In jazz, the ii–V–I progression often loops parts of the circle.

	•	In pop and rock, the I–V–vi–IV progression (C–G–Am–F) uses adjacent relationships on the circle to produce emotional resonance.



7. Visual Representation

Imagine a clock:

	•	12 o’clock: C major

	•	1 o’clock: G major (1 sharp)

	•	2 o’clock: D major (2 sharps)

	•	… etc., moving clockwise adds sharps

	•	Moving counterclockwise adds flats.



At the bottom (6 o’clock), F♯ major and G♭ major meet.



8. Advanced: Chromatic Mediant and Distant Key Relationships

	•	Keys that are not adjacent on the circle have fewer common tones.

	•	Modulating to these creates dramatic contrast (e.g., C major to E major).



9. Mnemonics for Key Signatures

Sharps (Order):

F–C–G–D–A–E–B
