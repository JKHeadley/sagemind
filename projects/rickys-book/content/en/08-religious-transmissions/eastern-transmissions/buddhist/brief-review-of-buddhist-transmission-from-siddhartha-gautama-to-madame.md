# Brief Review of Buddhist Transmission from Siddhartha Gautama to Madame Blavatsky



I. The Axial Birth: Siddhartha Gautama and the Śramaṇa Revolution (5th–6th c. BCE)

Context:

	•	India’s religious ferment: The Upanishads question Vedic ritualism. Meanwhile, forest renunciants (śramaṇas) explore direct mystical experience, rejecting caste-bound orthodoxy.



Gautama’s Contribution:

	•	Radical experientialism: Gautama’s teaching was not dogma but a method to end suffering through direct insight into impermanence (anicca), not-self (anatta), and dependent origination (paṭiccasamuppāda).

	•	First syncretism: Incorporated yogic meditative techniques from Jainism and other ascetic lineages, but rejected extreme austerity.



Key Breakthrough: The Buddha doesn’t just reject the soul (atman), he replaces metaphysical speculation with a psychological-phenomenological praxis.



II. The Council Era and Early Fragmentation (4th c. BCE – 1st c. CE)

The Councils:

	•	Following the Buddha’s death, oral teachings were codified, first in Pāli, later Sanskritized.

	•	First schism: Sthavira (Elders) vs. Mahāsāṃghika (Great Assembly). This wasn’t just about rules—it was a fissure between conservatism and visionary cosmology.



Key Development:

	•	Mahāsāṃghikas begin mythologizing the Buddha, planting seeds for multiple Buddhas, celestial realms, and cosmic Bodhisattvas—all pivotal for Mahāyāna.



III. Hellenistic Syncretism in Gandhāra (2nd c. BCE – 2nd c. CE)

Greek-Buddhist Crossroads:

	•	After Alexander the Great’s conquests, Greek polities in Bactria and Gandhāra sponsor Buddhist art and philosophy.

Synthesis:

	•	Buddha now depicted anthropomorphically, inspired by Apollo-like images.

	•	Greek notions of nous, logos, and gnosis encounter Buddhist ideas of citta, dharma, and nirvāṇa.

	•	The Greco-Buddhist kings (like Menander/Milinda) support dialogues like the *Milindapañha*, where Greek logic and Buddhist metaphysics blend.



Philosophical Resonance: Both Greek Stoicism and Buddhist śūnyatā regard detachment as central to wisdom. Stoic *apatheia* parallels Buddhist *upekkhā* (equanimity).



IV. Mahāyāna Emerges: Cosmic Vision and Compassion (1st–4th c. CE)

The Shift:

	•	Mahāyāna introduces Bodhisattvas as central archetypes: beings who renounce final nirvāṇa to help all others awaken.

	•	New scriptures (Lotus Sūtra, Avataṃsaka Sūtra) appear, often claimed to be teachings of the “true” deeper Dharma.



Innovations:

	•	Buddha-nature (tathāgatagarbha): All beings contain the potential for Buddhahood.

	•	Emptiness (śūnyatā) in Nāgārjuna’s Madhyamaka: Nothing exists independently—reality is a web of co-arising.



Syncretic Echoes:

	•	Zoroastrian and Gnostic influences may filter in via Central Asia:

	•	Amitābha’s Pure Land mirrors Paradise motifs.

	•	Cosmic Buddhas resemble Aeons from Gnostic cosmology.



V. Vajrayāna and the Tantric Explosion (6th–9th c. CE)

Context:

	•	In Bengal, Kashmir, and the Himalayas, Buddhism fuses with Tantric Hinduism and native shamanic traditions.

Features:

	•	Ritual technologies: Mantras, mandalas, deity yoga, alchemical inner practices.

	•	Philosophical synthesis of Madhyamaka and Yogācāra with Shaiva non-dualism.

	•	The Five Buddha Families represent facets of enlightened awareness, psychological archetypes, and cosmic principles.



Syncretic Overlays:

	•	Vajrayāna blends:

	•	Śiva and Heruka

	•	Kālī and Vajrayoginī

	•	Chakra systems and subtle bodies—deep links with Nātha Siddha traditions.



Tibetan Innovation: Local Bon deities are “converted” into Dharma protectors. Gods once feared are now guardians of the path—a psycho-spiritual transmutation.



VI. East Asian Syncretisms (1st–9th c. CE)

China:

	•	Daoist metaphysics reframe Buddhist ideas:

	•	Emptiness = Dao

	•	Meditation = non-action

	•	Rebirth seen through Confucian moral causality.

Major Schools:

	•	Tiantai: Harmonizes all Buddhist teachings into one cosmic vehicle.

	•	Huayan: Philosophical masterpiece; Indra’s Net is a metaphor for infinite interpenetration—a vision echoed in quantum holism today.

	•	Chan (Zen): Strips away metaphysics, emphasizing direct experience—echoes of Daoist spontaneity.



Japan:

	•	Shingon (esoteric Vajrayāna), Tendai (Tiantai), and Zen blend with Shinto animism.

	•	Syncretic gods emerge: Kannon (Avalokiteśvara) becomes both a Buddhist Bodhisattva and a native kami.



VII. Buddhism in Decline and Diaspora (10th–13th c. CE)

India:

	•	Islamic invasions destroy centers like Nalanda, Vikramashila.

	•	Many Buddhist concepts absorbed into Advaita Vedānta and Tantric Shaivism:

	•	Māyā, karma, non-dualism, even bodhicitta echoed in jnāna yoga.



Survival:

	•	Theravāda thrives in Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia.

	•	Tibetan Buddhism continues Vajrayāna legacy in exile.



VIII. Buddhism Encounters Europe (16th–19th c. CE)

Early Encounters:

	•	Jesuits in China record Buddhist ideas; call it “atheistic Confucianism.”

	•	Orientalists like Burnouf, Schopenhauer, and Müller begin serious study.



Misinterpretations:

	•	Buddhism seen as:

	•	Nihilistic

	•	Morally superior but spiritually “empty”

	•	Yet Romantic thinkers see Buddhism as a rational and mystical counterpoint to Christianity.



IX. Theosophy and the Occult Revival (19th c. CE)

H.P. Blavatsky (1831–1891)

	•	Co-founder of the Theosophical Society (1875), claimed contact with Tibetan Mahatmas.

	•	*Isis Unveiled* (1877) and *The Secret Doctrine* (1888) synthesize Buddhism, Neoplatonism, Hermeticism, Gnosticism, and Kabbalah.



Core Esoteric Reframings:

	•	Shunyata becomes the “Great Breath”, a pulsating astral field.

	•	Avalokiteśvara = the Logos, divine compassion, or Christ Consciousness.

	•	Buddhas and Bodhisattvas = Solar and planetary intelligences within a cosmic hierarchy.

	•	Maitreya = the coming World Teacher; merges Buddhist eschatology with Christian millennialism.



Conclusion: The Cosmic Arc

From Gautama’s psychological revolution in the forests of India to Blavatsky’s occult reinterpretation of Buddhist archetypes as cosmic intelligences, Buddhism’s journey is a story of continuous transmutation. It has merged with:

	•	Greek rationalism and aesthetics

	•	Persian cosmology

	•	Daoist metaphysics

	•	Tantric ritualism

	•	Christian esotericism

	•	Occult theosophy

Each turn of the Dharma Wheel brings new symbolic mutations, new layers of myth, and fresh psychological depth. Buddhism doesn’t just survive through syncretism—it reincarnates, again and again, to meet the minds of every age.
