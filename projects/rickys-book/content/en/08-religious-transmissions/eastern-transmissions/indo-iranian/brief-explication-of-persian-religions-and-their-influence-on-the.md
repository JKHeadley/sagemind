# Brief Explication of Persian Religions and their Influence on the Abrahamic Faiths



1. Zoroastrianism

Zoroastrianism is one of the world’s oldest monotheistic religions, dating back to approximately the 6th century BCE, founded by the prophet Zoroaster (or Zarathustra). It has profoundly influenced various religious traditions, including Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, particularly with its concepts of good versus evil, the afterlife, and divine judgment.

Key Beliefs:

	•	Ahura Mazda: The supreme god, representing all that is good, just, and true. Ahura Mazda is the creator of the universe and embodies wisdom and light.

	•	Angra Mainyu (Ahriman): The evil spirit, representing chaos, destruction, and lies. Angra Mainyu stands in opposition to Ahura Mazda and seeks to corrupt creation.

	•	Dualism: Zoroastrianism is fundamentally dualistic, meaning it believes in the cosmic struggle between good and evil, truth and lie, order and chaos.

	•	The Threefold Path: The religion promotes the ethical principles of good thoughts (humata), good words (hukhta), and good deeds (hvarshta).

	•	The Afterlife: Zoroastrians believe in a judgment after death, where souls are weighed based on their deeds. Those who are righteous are rewarded with eternal life in the “House of Song,” while the wicked are cast into the “House of Lies.”



Rituals and Practices:

	•	Fire Worship: Fire is a symbol of Ahura Mazda’s divine light and purity, often kept alive in temples as an object of reverence.

	•	The Avesta: The sacred texts of Zoroastrianism, which include hymns, prayers, and rituals.

	•	Nowruz: The Zoroastrian New Year, which celebrates the spring equinox and symbolizes renewal and rebirth.



Zoroastrianism had a major influence on Persian culture, especially during the Achaemenid (Persian) Empire, and remains practiced today by a small community, primarily in Iran and India (Parsis).

2. Zurvanism

Zurvanism is an ancient religious belief system that emerged as a variant of Zoroastrianism, focusing on Zurvan, the god of time and infinity, as the supreme deity.



Key Beliefs:

	•	Zurvan: Unlike Zoroastrianism, which emphasizes Ahura Mazda as the sole deity, Zurvanism posits that Zurvan, as the god of infinite time and space, is the source of both Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu. This introduces a form of monism, where all forces (good and evil) originate from one source.

	•	Cosmic Duality: Zurvanism introduces a slightly different dualism from Zoroastrianism. While Zoroastrianism has a clear opposition between Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu, Zurvanism depicts the creation of both divine and evil forces as coming from the same primordial source: Zurvan.

	•	Predestination: According to Zurvanism, the fates of individuals and the universe are determined by time (Zurvan) and predestination. This contrasts with the more free-willed view in mainstream Zoroastrianism.



Decline:

Zurvanism was mostly practiced during the Sassanian Empire (224–651 CE) but eventually faded, as the orthodox Zoroastrian faith became more dominant.

3. Mithraism

Mithraism, often called the Cult of Mithras, was a mystery religion that flourished in the Roman Empire from the 1st to the 4th century CE, though it had its roots in the ancient Persian worship of Mithra, a god of light and covenants.



Key Beliefs:

	•	Mithra: Mithra is the god of light, truth, and contracts, representing the force of order against chaos. He is often depicted as slaying a bull, a symbolic act of creation and purification.

	•	Cosmic Struggle: Mithraism is rooted in the concept of a cosmic struggle between good (Mithra) and evil (dark forces). Mithra is considered a mediator between humanity and the divine.

	•	Mystery Religion: Mithraism was secretive, with initiates undergoing various rituals and ceremonies to gain spiritual knowledge and progress toward salvation. It was heavily focused on brotherhood, strength, and moral purity.

	•	Initiations and Rituals: Initiates went through stages, with rituals that included the slaying of a bull (symbolic of purification), shared meals, and promises of immortality after death.

	•	Influence on Christianity: There are similarities between Mithraism and early Christianity, including the figure of Mithra being associated with concepts such as rebirth and eternal life. Some scholars suggest that Mithraism may have influenced early Christian practices and theology.



Decline:

Mithraism began to decline after the rise of Christianity as the state religion of the Roman Empire, but its influence on Roman religious and social practices was significant.

4. Persian Religions as a Whole

Persian religious traditions were highly diverse, evolving over millennia from the early Indo-Iranian traditions to the highly developed systems found in Zoroastrianism, Mithraism, and other regional belief systems.

Ancient Persian Beliefs:

	•	Indo-Iranian Roots: Early Persian religion was part of the broader Indo-Iranian religious tradition, which shared many similarities with the Vedic religions of India. These traditions worshipped a pantheon of gods and spirits tied to natural forces, such as fire, water, and the sun.

	•	Ahura Mazda and Other Deities: Alongside Ahura Mazda in Zoroastrianism, there were a number of lesser deities, such as Asha Vahishta (the spirit of truth), Vohu Manah (the spirit of good mind), and Mithra (the god of covenants), each with specific roles in maintaining order and righteousness.



Influence on Other Religions:

	•	Zoroastrianism’s Impact on Abrahamic Faiths: Concepts of a single god, judgment after death, and resurrection in Zoroastrianism were later reflected in Jewish, Christian, and Islamic traditions.

	•	Mithraism’s Spread in the Roman Empire: As the Romans expanded into Persia, Mithraism became a significant cult, especially among the military and elite.



These various religious systems contributed richly to the development of Persian culture and to the broader religious landscape, shaping not only Persian civilization but also much of the ancient world’s spiritual practices.

The Persian religions—particularly Zoroastrianism, Zurvanism, and Mithraism—had a significant influence on the development of the Abrahamic faiths (Judaism, Christianity, and Islam), especially in terms of theological concepts, cosmology, eschatology, and religious practices. Here’s a breakdown of how these Persian traditions shaped the Abrahamic religions:



1. Influence of Zoroastrianism on the Abrahamic Faiths

Zoroastrianism, as one of the world’s earliest monotheistic faiths, contributed to the theological development of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam in various ways. The key areas of influence are:

Monotheism and Dualism

	•	Monotheism: Zoroastrianism’s emphasis on a single, supreme god, Ahura Mazda, was a precursor to the more defined monotheism seen in Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. Before Zoroastrianism, many ancient Near Eastern religions were polytheistic. Zoroastrianism’s shift to a singular divine figure (though not fully identical, as Zoroastrianism retains a cosmic dualism) reinforced the concept of one god, a central feature of all three Abrahamic faiths.

	•	Dualism: Zoroastrianism’s dualistic nature, the conflict between Ahura Mazda (the good spirit) and Angra Mainyu (the evil spirit), influenced the later development of the Abrahamic faiths’ concepts of good versus evil. In Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, this cosmic battle between a benevolent god and a malevolent force (e.g., Satan in Christianity and Islam, or the Sitra Achra in Kabbalistic Jewish mysticism) mirrors Zoroastrianism’s struggle between truth and falsehood.



Eschatology: Judgment Day and Resurrection

	•	Judgment Day: Zoroastrianism’s belief in a final judgment at the end of the world, where souls are judged for their actions in life and are either rewarded with eternal life or punished, influenced the eschatological views of the Abrahamic faiths. This is similar to the concepts of Judgment Day in Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, where the righteous are rewarded and the wicked are condemned.

	•	Resurrection of the Dead: Zoroastrians believe in the resurrection of the dead in the last days. This idea was incorporated into Jewish, Christian, and Islamic beliefs. The concept of the bodily resurrection in these faiths, particularly in Christianity (where Jesus’ resurrection is central) and Islam (where believers are resurrected for judgment), reflects Zoroastrian eschatological thought.



Heaven and Hell

	•	Heaven and Hell: Zoroastrianism describes a division between the righteous (who go to a place of eternal reward, often called the House of Song) and the wicked (who go to a place of punishment, sometimes referred to as the House of Lies). This idea of an afterlife was absorbed into the Christian and Islamic conceptions of heaven and hell, where the soul is sent after death based on its deeds in life.



Angels and Demons

	•	Angels and Demons: Zoroastrianism introduced a complex hierarchy of divine and malevolent beings. For example, the Amesha Spentas (Holy Immortals) were akin to angels, while the Daevas represented malevolent spirits or demons. This dichotomy of good and evil spirits later mirrored the development of angelology and demonology in Judaism, Christianity, and Islam.



2. Influence of Zurvanism on the Abrahamic Faiths

While Zurvanism had less of a direct impact than Zoroastrianism, its ideas about time, fate, and the nature of the divine also made subtle contributions.



Predestination and Divine Will

	•	Predestination: Zurvanism’s emphasis on time (Zurvan) as a cosmic, omnipresent force that determines fate influenced later concepts of predestination in Christianity (particularly in Calvinist thought) and Islam (with the belief in Qadar, or divine predestination). In these traditions, the idea that human lives and actions are preordained by a higher power is similar to the Zurvanist idea that time, as an infinite force, controls the fate of all things.



Monism vs. Dualism

	•	Monism and the Source of Both Good and Evil: Zurvanism’s view that both Ahura Mazda and Angra Mainyu (good and evil) originated from Zurvan (the god of time) may have influenced the Abrahamic view of a single, omnipotent god who created both the forces of good and evil, though in the Abrahamic traditions, God is considered wholly good, and evil exists as a result of free will or rebellion (such as Satan’s fall).



3. Influence of Mithraism on the Abrahamic Faiths

Mithraism was particularly influential in the Roman Empire during the time of early Christianity, and some scholars believe it influenced certain Christian rituals and theological concepts.



Birth of a Divine Figure

	•	Birth of Mithras: Mithraism celebrated the birth of Mithras on December 25th, which later became the date of Jesus’ birth in Christian tradition. Although the date may be coincidental, it’s likely that Mithraism’s celebration of the winter solstice had an indirect influence on the Christian choice of December 25th as the date of Jesus’ nativity.



Sacramental Meal and Communion

	•	Shared Meal: In Mithraism, there was a ritualistic meal that symbolized the sharing of divine grace, often involving the consumption of bread and wine. This is strikingly similar to the Christian Eucharist (the Lord’s Supper), where bread and wine represent the body and blood of Jesus. While these rituals likely developed independently, the similarities suggest some degree of mutual influence or parallel development.



Salvation and the Afterlife

	•	Mithra’s Role as Savior: Mithras was often seen as a mediator between humanity and the divine, offering salvation through initiation and a final judgment. This concept of a divine mediator in Mithraism parallels the role of Jesus Christ in Christianity, who is viewed as a mediator between humanity and God, offering salvation through his death and resurrection.



The Bull Sacrifice

	•	Bull Slaying: The most iconic image of Mithra is the slaying of the bull, which symbolizes life, death, and regeneration. This act of slaying and the consumption of the bull’s life force could be seen as analogous to the sacrifice of Christ in Christianity, where Jesus’ death on the cross is viewed as an act of redemption for humanity.



Cosmic Struggle and Final Victory

	•	Victory Over Darkness: Mithra’s battle with the forces of darkness and his eventual triumph mirrors Christian eschatology, where Christ ultimately conquers Satan and evil at the end of time. Similarly, both religions emphasize the victory of light over darkness, symbolizing the triumph of good over evil.



4. Shared Themes Across All Persian Religions

	•	Angels, Demons, and Spiritual Warfare: The idea of spiritual warfare and the existence of benevolent and malevolent beings in Zoroastrianism and Mithraism also influenced the Abrahamic faiths, especially the notion of spiritual battle in Christian and Islamic theology, where angels and demons engage in ongoing conflict.

	•	Covenantal Relationships: The concept of a covenant (as seen in Zoroastrianism with Ahura Mazda and his followers) may have influenced the development of the Abrahamic covenants in Judaism and Christianity, especially in how humanity enters into a relationship with the divine.



Conclusion

The Persian religions provided key concepts and structures that influenced the Abrahamic faiths in profound ways. Zoroastrianism’s dualistic cosmology, eschatological views, and notions of judgment, resurrection, and salvation had a lasting impact, while Zurvanism introduced ideas of time and predestination that influenced certain theological discussions. Mithraism, with its mysteries, salvation themes, and cosmic struggles, left a particularly noticeable imprint on early Christianity, contributing to the development of key practices and theological concepts.
