# Brief Review of Advaita Vedanta



Advaita Vedānta (“non-dual end of the Vedas”) is one of the most influential and philosophically rigorous schools of Indian thought. It is a school of Vedānta, the system of thought based on the Upaniṣads, Bhagavad Gītā, and Brahma Sūtras.



Here’s a full breakdown:

1. Core Principle: Non-Duality (Advaita)

	•	Advaita = *a-* (“not”) + *dvaita* (“two”) → “not two” or “non-dual.”

	•	It teaches that Brahman (the ultimate reality) is the only truth. All multiplicity and difference are illusory (Māyā).

	•	Atman = Brahman → The individual self (Atman) is identical with the universal, impersonal absolute (Brahman).



2. Foundational Texts

Advaita Vedānta relies on prasthāna traya:

	1.	Upaniṣads (Śruti) – Philosophical sections of the Vedas.

	2.	Bhagavad Gītā (Smṛti) – A synthesis of bhakti and jñāna paths.

	3.	Brahma Sūtras – Logical summaries that reconcile contradictions in the Upaniṣads.



3. Founding Figure: Śaṅkara (Adi Śaṅkarācārya, c. 8th century CE)

	•	Systematized and revived Advaita philosophy.

	•	Wrote commentaries (bhāṣyas) on all three prasthānas.

	•	Argued against rival schools: Mīmāṁsā, Nyāya, Sāṃkhya, Yoga, Buddhism, and theistic Vedānta schools like Viśiṣṭādvaita and Dvaita.



4. Metaphysics

Brahman

	•	Nirguṇa Brahman: Brahman without attributes – the true, unchanging reality.

	•	Saguṇa Brahman (Īśvara): The personal god, a manifestation of Brahman in Māyā – a provisional reality for the sake of devotion.



Atman

	•	The innermost self.

	•	Realized through introspection and discrimination (*viveka*).

	•	Ultimately identical with Brahman.

Māyā

	•	The power that projects multiplicity and veils Brahman.

	•	Causes superimposition (*adhyāsa*) – the illusion of difference.

	•	Not real (*sat*), not unreal (*asat*) – anirvacanīya (indescribable).



5. Epistemology

Means of Knowledge (Pramāṇa):

Advaita accepts six pramāṇas:

	1.	Pratyakṣa – Perception

	2.	Anumāna – Inference

	3.	Upamāna – Analogy

	4.	Arthāpatti – Postulation

	5.	Anupalabdhi – Non-perception

	6.	Śabda – Verbal testimony (especially from the Vedas)



But ultimate knowledge (*jñāna*) comes from śruti (scripture), especially mahāvākyas like:

	•	*Tat tvam asi* (“You are That”)

	•	*Aham brahmāsmi* (“I am Brahman”)



6. Soteriology (Liberation: Mokṣa)

	•	Liberation is self-realization: realizing one’s identity with Brahman.

	•	No need for rituals or deities ultimately – only knowledge (*jñāna*) frees.

	•	The jīvanmukta (liberated while living) has transcended the illusion of separateness.



7. Practices and Pathways

Though it emphasizes knowledge, Advaita outlines four means (*sādhana catuṣṭaya*):

	1.	Viveka – Discrimination between the real and unreal.

	2.	Vairāgya – Dispassion toward ephemeral pleasures.

	3.	Ṣaṭ-sampatti – Six virtues (calmness, self-control, etc.)

	4.	Mumukṣutva – Intense desire for liberation.



Three stages of practice:

	1.	Śravaṇa – Hearing the teachings.

	2.	Manana – Reflecting on them.

	3.	Nididhyāsana – Deep meditation and absorption.



8. Major Commentators and Lineages

	•	Śaṅkara’s disciples:

	•	Padmapāda (Vivaraṇa school)

	•	Sureśvara (Vārttika school)

	•	Vācaspati Miśra (Bhāmatī school)

	•	Later Advaitins: Vidyāraṇya, Madhusūdana Sarasvatī, and others.

	•	The tradition split into sub-schools:

	•	Vivaraṇa school (emphasizes pure consciousness)

	•	Bhāmatī school (emphasizes individual mind and cognition in realization)

10. Criticisms and Debates

	•	Buddhist philosophers accused it of reifying a permanent self.

	•	Dvaita Vedāntins criticized it as nihilistic or deceptive.

	•	Modern thinkers have debated its practicality or elitism (knowledge-centric over action or devotion).



11. Modern Influence

	•	Influenced Swami Vivekananda, Ramana Maharshi, Nisargadatta Maharaj, and Neo-Vedānta (e.g., Sarvepalli Radhakrishnan).

	•	Popular in Western non-dual spiritual movements (e.g., Rupert Spira, Mooji, Eckhart Tolle).

	•	Cross-pollinated with Kashmir Śaivism, Sufi metaphysics, and Neoplatonism in comparative circles.
