# Brief Analysis of the Tauroctany



The tauroctony is the central iconographic scene in the cult of Mithras, a mystery religion that flourished in the Roman Empire from the 1st to the 4th centuries CE. The term comes from the Greek *tauros* (bull) and *kteinein* (to kill), and it refers to the act of Mithras slaying a bull. This ritual scene was placed in almost every *mithraeum* (temple dedicated to Mithras), usually in the apse or as a central relief.



I. Core Elements of the Tauroctony Scene

	1.	Mithras slaying the bull

	•	Mithras is usually shown wearing a Phrygian cap and Persian-style garb.

	•	He is typically kneeling on the bull’s back, pulling the bull’s head backward while plunging a dagger into its neck or shoulder.

	•	Mithras often looks away from the bull, not at it—a notable and deliberate detail.

	2.	The bull

	•	A powerful white bull, central to the scene.

	•	Its blood is often shown as giving life, not as something merely being spilled.

	3.	Animals surrounding the bull

	•	Dog and serpent: Often depicted drinking the blood from the bull’s wound.

	•	Scorpion: Clutches the bull’s testicles, symbolizing death or entropy.

	•	Sometimes a lion is also present, symbolizing fire or solar force.

	4.	Cave background

	•	The scene often takes place in a cave, echoing the cave in which Mithras was believed to have slain the bull. This microcosmic cave represents the cosmos.

	5.	Zodiac and Celestial Elements

	•	The zodiacal signs, especially Taurus (bull) and Scorpio, are often subtly or overtly embedded.

	•	Figures of Sol (the Sun) and Luna (the Moon) frequently appear, witnessing or responding to the tauroctony.

	•	Sometimes the cosmic dome or celestial globe arches over the scene.



II. Symbolic Interpretations

	1.	Astronomical Interpretation

	•	Tauroctony as celestial allegory:

	•	Mithras represents the Sun or a cosmic force.

	•	The bull = The Zodiac from Taurus to Sagittarius

	•	The act of slaying Taurus may symbolize the precession of the equinoxes or the end of the Age of Taurus (c. 2000 BCE) and the ushering in of the Age of Aries.

	•	Dog = Canis Major, Serpent = Hydra, Scorpion = Scorpius.

	•	Mithras slaying the bull is thus a cosmic realignment, not a literal sacrifice.

	2.	Vegetation and Fertility Rite

	•	The bull’s blood and semen are linked to fertility, renewal, and life-giving force.

	•	Death (the bull’s) enables rebirth of the world, possibly echoing Near Eastern dying and rising god motifs (e.g., Tammuz, Osiris).

	3.	Initiation Allegory

	•	The tauroctony reflects the spiritual journey of the initiate:

	•	The cave = initiation chamber and the cosmos.

	•	Mithras’ actions = the mystic’s overcoming of the animal nature (bull) and accessing higher cosmic power.

	•	Mithras turns away from the bull as an act of divine detachment—doing what must be done for a higher cause without personal investment.

	4.	Moral Dualism

	•	The scene shows tension between life and death, creation and destruction, light and darkness—central to Zoroastrian and Neoplatonic thought, both of which influenced Mithraism.

	•	Mithras as a figure of cosmic order imposes harmony by slaying chaos (the primal bull).
