# Brief Review of Shaivism



Shaivism (also spelled Shaivite tradition) is one of the major streams of Hinduism, centering on the worship of Shiva as the Supreme Being. It is not a monolith but a broad umbrella covering many different sects, philosophies, rituals, texts, and regional expressions.



Below is a thorough breakdown of Shaivism, organized into key categories:



I. Core Beliefs and Philosophy

Shaivism is both theistic (devotion to Shiva as God) and monistic (identifying the self with God), depending on the school.



1. Shiva as Supreme

	•	Shiva is not only a destroyer (as in the Trimurti) but the source, sustainer, and dissolver of the cosmos.

	•	He is often depicted in a paradoxical form: ascetic and householder, creator and destroyer, still and dynamic (e.g., *Nataraja*).



2. Key Concepts

	•	Pati-Pashu-Pasha (The Lord, the soul, and the bondage): A triadic formula across Shaiva schools.

	•	Anava (ego/ignorance), Karma, and Maya bind the soul (Pashu).

	•	Liberation (moksha) is achieved when these are removed through divine grace and discipline.

	•	Jiva = Shiva (in monistic schools) — the individual soul is ultimately not different from the Supreme.



3. Atman and Moksha

	•	Liberation is the realization of unity with Shiva, often through intense yogic discipline, devotion, or grace (Anugraha).

	•	Shaivism, especially in non-dual schools, emphasizes jnana (knowledge) over mere ritual.



II. Major Schools of Shaivism

Shaivism is divided into several distinct philosophical schools:

1. Kashmir Shaivism (Trika Shaivism)

	•	Non-dual (Advaita) system from Kashmir (c. 8th–12th centuries CE).

	•	Founded by Vasugupta, expanded by Abhinavagupta, Utpaladeva, and others.

	•	Emphasizes Spanda (vibration) and Pratyabhijña (recognition) of one’s identity as Shiva.

	•	Shakti is central — reality is a dynamic interplay of Shiva (consciousness) and Shakti (power/energy).



2. Shaiva Siddhanta

	•	A dualist-theist tradition (popular in South India, esp. Tamil Nadu).

	•	Teaches that Shiva is separate from the soul, and liberation is attained through His grace.

	•	Strong temple culture, liturgy, and ritual.

	•	Canon includes Tirumurai, Periya Puranam, and Agamas.

	•	Revered poet-saints: Nayanmars, especially Appar, Sundarar, and Manikkavacakar.



3. Pashupata Shaivism

	•	One of the oldest Shaivite sects, dating to at least 2nd century CE.

	•	Founded by Lakulisha.

	•	Emphasizes extreme asceticism and internal discipline.

	•	Worship of Shiva as Pashupati (Lord of animals/souls).

	•	Had a strong presence in Gujarat and central India.



4. Kapalika and Aghora Traditions

	•	Tantric, transgressive sects.

	•	Known for practices in cremation grounds, use of skulls, ritual nudity, and non-normative behavior to transcend duality.

	•	Worship the fierce forms of Shiva, like Bhairava.

	•	Aghoris are their modern descendants (though distinct in practice today).



5. Lingayatism (Veerashaivism)

	•	Emerged in Karnataka in the 12th century, founded by Basava.

	•	Rejected Vedic rituals, caste hierarchy, and Brahmin mediation.

	•	Worship Shiva in the form of the Ishtalinga (personal linga worn on the body).

	•	Emphasizes personal devotion, social equality, and monotheism.

	•	Has a distinct canon: Vachanas.



6. Tantric Shaivism

	•	Rooted in Agama scriptures, distinct from Vedic orthodoxy.

	•	Uses mantras, yantras, mudras, kundalini yoga, and ritual worship.

	•	Worship of Shiva and Shakti in various forms (e.g., Bhairava, Ardhanarishvara).

	•	Strong emphasis on initiation (diksha) and secret knowledge.



III. Texts and Scriptures

1. Agamas and Tantras

	•	Core to ritual, cosmology, and yoga in Shaivism.

	•	Classified into Shaiva, Rudra, and Bhuta Agamas.



2. Vedas and Upanishads

	•	Shaiva interpretations of the Vedas, especially Rudram, Shatarudriya, Kaivalya Upanishad, Shvetashvatara Upanishad (seen as proto-Shaiva).



3. Shaiva Puranas

	•	Shiva Purana, Linga Purana, Skanda Purana, Vayu Purana, etc.

	•	Mythological, cosmological, and theological narratives.



4. Shaiva Philosophical Works

	•	Spanda Karika, Pratyabhijñāhṛdayam, Tantraloka (Abhinavagupta), Shiva Sutras.



IV. Deities and Symbolism

1. Shiva

	•	Forms: Nataraja (Lord of Dance), Bhairava (Fierce), Dakshinamurti (Teacher), Ardhanarishvara (Half-woman), Mahadeva, etc.

	•	Symbols: Linga, Trident (Trishula), Crescent Moon, Ganga, Third Eye, Drum (Damaru), Serpents.



2. Parvati / Shakti

	•	Consort and dynamic power (Shakti) of Shiva.

	•	Appears as Durga, Kali, Tripura Sundari, Annapurna, etc.



3. Ganesha and Kartikeya

	•	Sons of Shiva and Parvati.

	•	Worshiped widely; Ganesha is the remover of obstacles, Kartikeya is the god of war and youth.

V. Rituals, Practices, and Initiations

1. Puja and Temple Worship

	•	Especially central in Shaiva Siddhanta and Lingayatism.

	•	Daily worship of the linga with water, milk, ashes (vibhuti), and sacred chants.



2. Mantra and Tantra

	•	Mantras such as Om Namah Shivaya, Panchakshari, and Shiva Gayatri.

	•	Tantra includes ritual worship, fire ceremonies, and yogic visualizations.



3. Yoga and Meditation

	•	Especially central in Kashmir Shaivism and Tantric Shaivism.

	•	Use of Kundalini, chakra systems, and spanda meditation (vibration awareness).



4. Initiation (Diksha)

	•	Required in most Tantric and Agamic schools.

	•	Bestows access to mantras, inner rituals, and higher realization.

VI. Shaivism and Society

1. Temple Culture

	•	Major temples: Chidambaram, Kashi Vishwanath, Kedarnath, Amarnath, Thillai, Pashupatinath, Ellora.



2. Shaiva Saints

	•	Nayanmars (Tamil poet-saints), Basava and other Lingayat leaders, Abhinavagupta, Lakulisha.

3. Relation to Other Traditions

	•	Shares many features with Shaktism, Tantra, and Yoga.

	•	Often contrasts with Vaishnavism (Vishnu-centered), especially in theology and ritual priorities.



VII. Influence and Spread

	•	India: Most influential in Kashmir, Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Maharashtra, Gujarat, and Nepal.

	•	Southeast Asia: Deeply influential in Cambodia (Angkor Wat), Java, Bali, Thailand.

	•	Himalayan Traditions: Integrated into Tibetan and Bhutanese esoteric systems (especially Bhairava worship).

	•	Syncretism: Incorporated into Sufi, Buddhist, and even Christian mystic traditions in some localized contexts.



VIII. Contemporary Relevance

	•	Revived interest in Kashmir Shaivism among spiritual seekers.

	•	Lingayat movement still vibrant in Karnataka.

	•	Aghora and cremation-ground Shaivism continue to challenge social norms.

	•	Integral to yoga communities through kundalini, tantra, and advaita teachings.
