# Brief Review of Taoism



I. ORIGINS & HISTORICAL CONTEXT

A. Pre-Taoist Roots

Taoism emerged during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods (770–221 BCE), a time of intense political instability and philosophical experimentation in China.



Before formal Taoism, proto-Daoist elements were already present in:

	•	Shamanic traditions of ancient China

	•	Yin-yang cosmology and Five Phases (Wuxing)

	•	The Book of Changes (I Ching) — a divination manual foundational to Taoist metaphysics



B. Philosophical Taoism (Daojia)

Founders:

	•	Laozi (Lao Tzu) – Legendary author of the *Dao De Jing*

	•	Zhuangzi (Chuang Tzu) – Mystic philosopher who expanded Taoism into deeper metaphysical realms



This early Taoism focused more on philosophical inquiry, nature mysticism, and individual spiritual cultivation, not yet a formal religion.



II. KEY TEXTS

1. Dao De Jing (Tao Te Ching) – Laozi

	•	Core themes: Non-action (Wu Wei), spontaneity (Ziran), natural order, reversal (fan).

	•	81 poetic verses

	•	Both a mystical manual and a political treatise on ruling without coercion



2. Zhuangzi (Chuang Tzu)

	•	Allegorical, humorous, and paradoxical

	•	Explores: Relativism, the limits of knowledge, freedom from social norms, unity with the Dao

	•	Key metaphors: *Butterfly dream*, *useless tree*, *empty boat*

3. Liezi (less influential but later canonized)

	•	Blend of Taoism and folk tales; more practical than the Zhuangzi

4. I Ching (Yijing)

	•	Though older, later integrated into Taoism as a symbolic and divinatory foundation for understanding change (Yi) and transformation (Hua).



III. CORE PHILOSOPHICAL CONCEPTS

A. Dao (Tao)

	•	The Way: ineffable, eternal, formless, source of all

	•	Cannot be named or grasped conceptually (*“The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao”*)



B. De (Te)

	•	Virtue or power in harmony with the Dao

	•	Different from Confucian moral virtue — De arises from authentic alignment, not social obligations



C. Wu Wei (Non-Action or Effortless Action)

	•	Acting without force or ego

	•	Allowing events to unfold naturally, like water flowing downhill

D. Ziran (Spontaneity / Naturalness)

	•	True nature when unconditioned by artificial norms

	•	“Self-so” or “so-of-itself”

E. Yin-Yang and Wuxing

	•	Yin-Yang: complementary opposites within unity (e.g., dark/light, soft/hard)

	•	Wuxing: Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water — dynamic elements in nature and the body



IV. TAOIST COSMOLOGY

Creation

	•	Dao gives rise to the One (Taiyi), the One to Two (Yin and Yang), the Two to Three, and the Three to the 10,000 things (all phenomena)

	•	The universe is in a constant state of transformation

Immortality & Spirit

	•	Hun (spiritual soul) and Po (corporeal soul) — dual soul theory

	•	Aim of Taoist alchemy: preserve, refine, and eventually liberate the Hun to reunite with the Dao



V. RELIGIOUS TAOISM (Daojiao)

A. Institutionalization

From the Han dynasty (206 BCE – 220 CE) onward, Taoism evolved into a religious system, influenced by both:

	•	Indigenous folk religion

	•	Competing systems like Confucianism and Buddhism

B. Key Religious Schools

1. Celestial Masters (Tianshi Dao)

	•	Founded by Zhang Daoling in the 2nd century CE

	•	Claimed revelation from Laozi as a deity

	•	Introduced structured priesthood, rituals, sacred texts, and moral codes

2. Shangqing (Highest Clarity)

	•	Focused on visualization, internal alchemy, and meditative ascent

	•	Heavy influence from Buddhist meditation and astral travel

3. Lingbao (Numinous Treasure)

	•	Strongly syncretic — combined Taoism, Mahayana Buddhism, and folk religion

	•	Introduced cosmic deities, salvational rituals, and universalist soteriology

4. Quanzhen (Complete Perfection) – Later Development (Song-Yuan Dynasties)

	•	Monastic Taoism, emphasized:

	•	Moral purity

	•	Meditation

	•	Alchemy

	•	Influenced by Chan (Zen) Buddhism

VI. PRACTICES

A. Internal Alchemy (Neidan)

	•	Transforming internal energies to attain immortality

	•	Key stages:

	1.	Refining jing (essence) into qi (vital energy)

	2.	Refining qi into shen (spirit)

	3.	Refining shen into emptiness (xu) / Dao

B. External Alchemy (Waidan)

	•	Creating elixirs from minerals (esp. mercury and cinnabar)

	•	Often dangerous; eventually gave way to Neidan

C. Meditation & Visualization

	•	Inner contemplation of cosmic gods or the Dao

	•	Circulating qi in the body (Microcosmic Orbit)

D. Qigong & Tai Chi

	•	Physical movements to harmonize qi, breath, and awareness

	•	Practiced for health, martial arts, and spiritual refinement

E. Rituals and Talismans

	•	Purification, exorcisms, ancestor offerings

	•	Use of fu (talismans) and incantations

VII. THE DAOIST PANTHEON

	•	Laozi becomes Taishang Laojun, a high deity

	•	The Three Pure Ones (Sanqing):

	1.	Yuanshi Tianzun (Primordial Heaven Lord)

	2.	Lingbao Tianzun (Mystic Treasure Lord)

	3.	Daode Tianzun (Lord of Dao and Virtue, i.e., deified Laozi)

	•	Eight Immortals, local gods, star spirits, and more

IX. MODERN TAOISM

	•	Practiced in China, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Southeast Asia, and globally

	•	Influences Traditional Chinese Medicine, Feng Shui, Martial Arts

	•	In the West: sometimes merged with New Age spirituality, ecological philosophy, or psychology

X. SYMBOLS AND ICONOGRAPHY

	•	Taijitu (Yin-Yang symbol) – Balance of opposites

	•	Bagua (Eight Trigrams) – Used in Feng Shui and I Ching

	•	Dragon and Tiger – Symbolize internal alchemical polarities

	•	Gourd, staff, peach, crane – Common Taoist immortality motifs



I. EXOTERIC INITIATION STAGES (Religious Taoism)

Stage 1: Ordination / Transmission (受箓 Shoulu)

	•	Ritual initiation into a lineage (Daozong), often performed by a master-priest.

	•	The initiate receives:

	•	Register (Lu): a spiritual license linking the practitioner to celestial deities

	•	Name change: A Daoist name is conferred to mark rebirth

	•	Talismanic empowerment

	•	Deity connection: often with specific guardian spirits



Purpose:

Establish karmic and spiritual credentials; align with a celestial order; prepare the ground for deeper transformation.

Stage 2: Celestial Covenant (盟誓 Mengshi)

	•	A vow to the Three Pure Ones and Daoist pantheon

	•	The initiate promises to:

	•	Cultivate virtue (De)

	•	Abide by specific precepts (Jie)

	•	Perform rituals and services properly



Parallels: Similar to a baptismal oath or bodhisattva vow



Stage 3: Transmission of Esoteric Teachings

	•	The disciple is taught:

	•	Scripture recitation

	•	Ritual protocols

	•	Incantations (Zhou)

	•	Talismanic calligraphy

	•	Astrological and cosmological knowledge

Note: The disciple may only access deeper alchemical teachings after demonstrating moral integrity and ritual discipline.



II. ESOTERIC INITIATION STAGES (Neidan/Internal Alchemy)

Neidan maps a transformational journey through three main stages, which echo yogic and tantric processes of purifying the subtle body. The goal is to refine Jing (essence) → Qi (energy) → Shen (spirit) → Xu (emptiness) → Dao (unity).



Stage 1: Jing Refinement (炼精化气 Lian Jing Hua Qi)

Symbolic Level: Earth

Elemental Polarity: Yin

Body Focus: Kidneys, sexual essence, lower dantian



Practices:

	•	Physical discipline, celibacy or sexual alchemy

	•	Breath control

	•	Fasting and purification

	•	Qigong to accumulate and stabilize Jing

Goal:

	•	Transmute sexual/physical essence into vital energy (Qi)

	•	Build an “inner cauldron” in the lower dantian

Stage 2: Qi Refinement (炼气化神 Lian Qi Hua Shen)

Symbolic Level: Heaven and Earth in interaction

Elemental Polarity: Yin-Yang balance

Body Focus: Heart, lungs, middle dantian



Practices:

	•	Circulating Qi through the microcosmic orbit (Du and Ren channels)

	•	Breath retention and inner visualization

	•	Meditation on cosmic correspondences (stars, organs, deities)



Goal:

	•	Stabilize Qi to cultivate Shen (spirit)

	•	Attain clarity, luminosity, and emotional detachment

Stage 3: Shen Refinement (炼神还虚 Lian Shen Huan Xu)

Symbolic Level: Heaven

Elemental Polarity: Yang

Body Focus: Upper dantian (head/third eye)



Practices:

	•	Deep inner meditation and emptiness practices

	•	Visualization of celestial deities merging with the spirit

	•	Returning Shen to the Void (Xu): transcending identity



Goal:

	•	Refine the spirit into cosmic emptiness

	•	Achieve return to the Dao (反哺归真 Fanbu Guizhen) — the “return to original truth”



Stage 4: Union with the Dao (合道 He Dao)

Symbolic Level: The Dao beyond polarity

Elemental Polarity: Transcendent

Body Focus: None — dissolution into formlessness



Practices:

	•	Stillness meditation

	•	Dreamwork, astral travel, subtle body shedding

	•	Becoming an Immortal (Xian) or Perfected (Zhenren)

Goal:

	•	Immortality — not in the physical sense, but as a perpetual union with the Dao

	•	Sometimes seen as reincarnation on a higher plane or celestial absorption

III. SYMBOLIC MAPS OF INITIATION

1. The Three Treasures

	•	Jing (Essence) – Body

	•	Qi (Energy) – Breath/Life-force

	•	Shen (Spirit) – Mind/Soul

All must be refined back to Xu (Emptiness) and then to Dao (Unity).
