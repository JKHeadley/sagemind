# Shamans of The Eurasian Steppes



I. Cultural and Spiritual Foundations of the Eurasian Steppes

1. Shamanic and Animistic Worldview

	•	Core Features:

	•	Belief in a spirit world accessible through altered states.

	•	Sky Father (Tengri) worship, central to Turko-Mongolic cosmologies.

	•	Earth Mother or chthonic forces in balance with the sky.

	•	Totemism, ancestral spirits, animal guides.

	•	Use of drums, ecstatic dance, and psychoactive substances for trance states.

	•	Cosmology:

	•	Three-tiered cosmos: Upper world (gods/sky), middle world (humans), and lower world (spirits/dead).

	•	World Tree (akin to the Norse Yggdrasil) connecting the realms.



2. Nomadic Tribal Structure and Myth

	•	Chiefs often seen as semi-divine or shamanic figures.

	•	Horse culture was deeply spiritual—horses were sacrificed, buried with the dead, and associated with the sun and travel to the afterlife.

	•	Burial kurgans (mounds) were major religious-political sites, often aligned with cosmological principles.



II. Religious Developments in the Steppe and Surrounding Zones

1. Tengrism (Turko-Mongolic Spirituality)

	•	Supreme deity Tengri (Heaven), worshipped across Huns, Göktürks, Mongols.

	•	Integration of shamanism, ancestor worship, and sacral kingship.

	•	Emphasized harmony with nature, fate, and the sky.

	•	Genghis Khan and other rulers traced divine authority to Tengri.



2. Scythian-Sarmatian Religion

	•	Strong influence from Iranian (Zoroastrian and pre-Zoroastrian) beliefs:

	•	Worship of fire, sun, and sacred drink (haoma, related to Soma).

	•	Burial with gold items symbolizing the divine and death journey.

	•	Gendered warrior-deities and serpent symbolism.

	•	Female warrior cults—precursors to Amazon myths—possibly had matriarchal shamanic elements.



3. Influence of Indo-European Religious Ideas

	•	The Proto-Indo-Iranians likely originated in the steppes (Andronovo culture).

	•	Shared motifs:

	•	Fire worship.

	•	Horse sacrifice (Ashvamedha parallels).

	•	Dualism (Devas vs. Asuras in Vedic, Ahura vs. Daeva in Iranian thought).

	•	Spread of chariot technology and solar cults.



III. Transmission to Sino-Tibetan Cultures

1. Via Mongolic and Turkic Influence

	•	Tibetan Bon: Pre-Buddhist religion had shamanic roots, possibly influenced by steppe Tengrism and Siberian shamanism.

	•	Oracular practices in Tibet (Nechung Oracle) resemble nomadic trance oracles.

	•	Manichaean and Nestorian Christianity spread into Tang China via Uyghur and Sogdian networks—these were steppe-mediated.



2. Daoist-Alchemical Parallels

	•	Sky-earth duality in Daoism overlaps with Tengri-Earth Mother polarity.

	•	Some Chinese immortality and mountain cults share cosmology with shamanic ascent/descent practices.

	•	Wu shamans in early Chinese culture had steppe-like roles.



IV. Transmission to Indo-Iranian Traditions

1. Iranian Traditions

	•	Zoroastrianism emerged from a milieu influenced by steppe dualism and fire worship.

	•	Use of haoma parallels shamanic plant use.

	•	Fravashis (ancestral spirits) echo steppe ancestor cults.

	•	Scythian and Sarmatian tribes carried Iranian religion into the steppe, creating a syncretic zone between steppe animism and Iranian theism.



2. Indian (Indo-Aryan) Traditions

	•	Rigvedic deities like Dyaus Pitar (Sky Father) and Agni (fire god) reflect steppe cosmology.

	•	Vedic horse sacrifice (Ashvamedha) shows direct continuity with steppe horse cults.

	•	Soma rituals parallel shamanic ecstasy and plant-spirit communication.

	•	Epic figures like Indra and Rudra may have shamanic-warrior roots.



V. Hybrid Religious Forms Along Trade Routes

1. Sogdian and Bactrian Syncretism

	•	Central Asia was a melting pot:

	•	Zoroastrianism, Buddhism, Manichaeism, and Nestorianism mingled.

	•	Sogdians (Iranian traders) were key in diffusing steppe and Iranian spiritual motifs to China and India.

	•	Artistic motifs (winged deities, solar disks) spread along the Silk Road.



2. Uyghur and Mongol Religious Pluralism

	•	Uyghurs practiced Manichaeanism, Buddhism, and Tengrism simultaneously.

	•	Mongols under Genghis and Kublai Khan allowed diverse religious practice:

	•	Their imperial ideology was modeled on divine right from Tengri and included Buddhism, Islam, and Christianity as instruments of rule.



VI. Symbolic and Mythological Legacy

1. Horse and Eagle Symbolism

	•	Represents ascent, vision, and freedom.

	•	Spread into Indo-Iranian (Garuda, Simurgh) and Tibetan (wind horse) iconography.



2. World Tree/Yggdrasil Motif

	•	Axis mundi found in steppe, Norse, Iranian, and Siberian cosmologies.

	•	Used to explain spiritual ascent, cosmic order, and sacred geography.



3. Sacral Kingship

	•	Steppe rulers seen as divinely sanctioned.

	•	Similar to Chakravartin concept in Indian Buddhism and Mandate of Heaven in China.

	•	Shaman-kingship fusion common (e.g. Mongol Khans, Scythian chieftains).



VII. Enduring Influences

	•	Buddhist Tantra in Tibet absorbed steppe shamanic techniques.

	•	Indian Vedic ritualism and cosmic order reflect steppe cosmology.

	•	Chinese imperial ideology incorporated steppe-style divinely ordained rulership.

	•	Islamic mysticism (Sufism) in Central Asia retained steppe ecstatic practices, especially in Naqshbandi orders.
