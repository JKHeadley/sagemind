# Brief History of Second Temple Judaism



I. Historical Overview

1. From Hasmoneans to Herod (140–37 BCE)

	•	Hasmonean dynasty (from Maccabean revolt) ruled as priest-kings.

	•	Internal divisions and rivalries led to a civil war between Hyrcanus II and Aristobulus II.

	•	63 BCE: Roman general Pompey intervened and annexed Judea as a client state.

	•	Jerusalem was captured, the Temple was desecrated (Pompey entered the Holy of Holies), but not destroyed.



2. Herodian Dynasty (37 BCE – 6 CE)

	•	Herod the Great (r. 37–4 BCE) appointed King of Judea by Rome.

	•	A client-king, Idumean by ancestry (converted to Judaism).

	•	Renovated and massively expanded the Second Temple, making it a symbol of Jewish identity—but was distrusted by many Jews.

	•	After Herod’s death, Judea was divided among his sons.

	•	Mismanagement and unrest followed, leading to Rome making Judea a province in 6 CE.



3. Roman Province of Judea (6–66 CE)

	•	Ruled by Roman prefects (e.g., Pontius Pilate, 26–36 CE).

	•	Roman rule was resented due to:

	•	High taxes

	•	Idolatrous symbols (e.g., imperial standards with Caesar’s image)

	•	Harsh governance and disrespect for Jewish customs

	•	Appointment of high priests by Roman authorities instead of by hereditary priestly lineage



II. Religious and Cultural Landscape

1. The Second Temple

	•	Center of Jewish religious, economic, and social life.

	•	Daily sacrifices, pilgrimages, and rituals occurred under the priestly class (mainly Sadducees).

	•	Annual festivals (Passover, Sukkot, Yom Kippur) brought Jews from across the diaspora.



2. Key Jewish Sects

a. Pharisees

	•	Popular among the common people.

	•	Advocated Torah + oral law (later formed the basis of the Talmud).

	•	Emphasized ethical behavior, purity laws, and resurrection of the dead.

	•	Often clashed with Sadducees and Roman authorities.



b. Sadducees

	•	Aristocratic priestly families, controlled the Temple.

	•	Rejected oral law; accepted only the written Torah.

	•	Denied resurrection, angels, or afterlife.

	•	Cooperated with Rome to maintain status quo.



c. Essenes

	•	Ascetic, apocalyptic group (possibly linked to the Dead Sea Scrolls).

	•	Lived in isolation (e.g., Qumran community).

	•	Believed the Temple was corrupt and awaited divine intervention.

	•	Practiced ritual purity and communal life.



d. Zealots

	•	Revolutionary nationalists who sought violent uprising against Rome.

	•	Inspired by Hasmonean precedent of revolt.

	•	Provoked the First Jewish-Roman War (66–73 CE).



e. Sicarii

	•	Radical offshoot of the Zealots.

	•	Used assassination (daggers—sica) against collaborators and Roman sympathizers.



III. Messianic Expectations

	•	Roman oppression intensified apocalyptic and messianic hopes.

	•	Many expected a Davidic warrior-messiah to defeat Rome and purify the Temple.

	•	Alternative expectations included priestly-messiahs (as seen in Dead Sea Scrolls).

	•	Several messianic claimants arose (e.g., Jesus of Nazareth, Theudas, “the Egyptian”), often ending in Roman crackdowns.



IV. Jesus Movement and Early Christianity

	•	Jesus preached during Roman rule (~27–30 CE), emphasizing a coming kingdom of God.

	•	Viewed by followers as Messiah and divine; viewed by Roman and Jewish elites as a potential revolutionary threat.

	•	Executed by crucifixion under Pontius Pilate.

	•	Early followers (e.g., James, Peter, Paul) formed the early Jesus movement—a sect within Judaism.

	•	Increasing friction with both Roman authorities and mainstream Jewish groups led to gradual separation into Christianity.



V. The First Jewish-Roman War (66–73 CE)

Causes:

	•	Growing hatred toward Roman occupation and corrupt Temple elite.

	•	Provocations by Roman governors (e.g., plundering the Temple treasury).

	•	Rise of Zealot factions and open rebellion in Jerusalem.

Key Events:

	•	66 CE: Jewish rebels took Jerusalem; war began.

	•	70 CE: Roman general Titus besieged Jerusalem; the Second Temple was destroyed.

	•	Temple never rebuilt—cataclysmic turning point for Judaism.



Aftermath:

	•	Masada fell in 73 CE.

	•	Judaism transformed:

	•	Temple-centered worship ended.

	•	Rise of Rabbinic Judaism, rooted in Pharisaic traditions.

	•	Jewish population decimated or exiled, diaspora expanded.



VI. Diaspora and Hellenistic Influence

	•	Judea was only part of a larger Jewish world.

	•	Significant Jewish populations in:

	•	Alexandria (Egypt)

	•	Babylonia

	•	Asia Minor and Rome

	•	Greek language was common among Jews of the diaspora.

	•	Translations like the Septuagint emerged, as well as Philo’s allegorical theology, blending Judaism with Stoic-Platonist thought.

VIII. Esoteric & Apocalyptic Texts

	•	Rise in apocalyptic literature: e.g., 1 Enoch, 4 Ezra, 2 Baruch, War Scroll.

	•	Themes:

	•	Cosmic battles between good and evil

	•	Coming judgment

	•	Dual messianic figures (kingly + priestly)

	•	Expectation of New Jerusalem

	•	Some texts influenced Christian Revelation and Qur’anic eschatology.



IX. Legacy and Transformation

	•	Destruction of the Second Temple (70 CE) reshaped Judaism and birthed Christianity:

	•	Judaism adapted via Rabbinic models, Mishnah, and later Talmud.

	•	Christianity spread throughout the empire, incorporating Jewish apocalyptic and ethical ideas while diverging theologically.

	•	The trauma of the Temple’s destruction became a lasting symbol in Jewish memory and liturgy.
