# Yeshu ha-Notzri



The Talmud contains a few scattered references to Jesus (commonly referred to as Yeshu or Yeshu ha-Notzri), but it does not provide a detailed or straightforward account of the crucifixion like the Gospels or the Qur’an. Instead, it references Jesus obliquely, and often in the context of polemic, satire, or allegory. Most references come from the Babylonian Talmud, and they must be read with care due to later censorship and emendations by Christian authorities.



Here are the most important references related to the execution or death of Jesus in the Talmud:



1. Sanhedrin 43a

*“On the eve of Passover, Yeshu was hanged. For forty days before the execution took place, a herald went forth and cried, ‘He is going forth to be stoned because he has practiced sorcery and enticed Israel to apostasy. Anyone who can say anything in his favor, let him come forward.’ But nothing was brought forward in his favor, and he was hanged on the eve of Passover.”*

	•	“Hanged” in Jewish texts can refer to crucifixion or hanging on a tree, as in Deuteronomy 21:22.

	•	The accusations—sorcery and leading Israel astray—are common in Jewish polemic.

	•	The reference to Passover aligns with the New Testament.

	•	Some scholars argue this passage was redacted or censored, and some manuscripts omit or modify it.



2. Sanhedrin 67a (Baraita)

This text alludes to Yeshu as a sorcerer who led people astray, associating him with magical practices and heresy. It ties him to other figures accused of corrupting Jewish tradition.

	•	“He learned magic in Egypt” is a common charge.

	•	His followers are sometimes called “notzrim”—a term later used for Christians.



3. Toledot Yeshu (not Talmudic, but relevant)

This is a medieval Jewish parody-gospel, not part of the Talmud, but it expands on the Talmudic themes, portraying Jesus as a magician and false prophet who is eventually executed and dishonored.

	•	This reflects how the Talmudic seed stories evolved into broader Jewish folklore in response to Christian dominance.

	•	Highly polemical, and not taken as serious theology by mainstream Judaism.



Censorship Note

	•	Many references to Jesus were removed or altered in printed versions of the Talmud due to Christian censorship (especially in Europe from the 13th to 18th centuries).

	•	The Vatican and other Christian authorities forced changes in names, pronouns, or entire passages.

	•	Some uncensored versions were preserved in Yemenite, Babylonian, and manuscript traditions.



Scholarly Debate

	•	Some argue the “Yeshu” in the Talmud refers to a different person (e.g., from earlier centuries BCE).

	•	Others believe it refers to Jesus of Nazareth, but in a coded or polemical way due to danger of persecution.

	•	Modern scholarship tends to read these passages as anti-Christian polemics rather than historically accurate accounts.



4. The Word “Hanged” (תָּלוּי / talui) in Second Temple and Rabbinic Literature

Biblical Roots

The term “hanged” (תָּלוּי) in Jewish texts does not usually mean hanging by a noose, but rather exposure on a tree or post after execution, especially in capital punishment.



Deuteronomy 21:22-23:



*“If a man has committed a sin deserving of death, and he is put to death, and you hang him on a tree, his body shall not remain overnight on the tree…”*

	•	The person is typically killed first, then hung publicly to shame or curse them.

	•	The act of “hanging” here is often symbolic of being under a divine curse.



This verse is quoted by Paul in Galatians 3:13, in reference to Jesus:



*“Cursed is everyone who is hanged on a tree.”*

In Second Temple Literature

	•	In the Dead Sea Scrolls, hanging on a tree is linked to divine justice and public warning.

	•	Josephus sometimes uses “crucifixion” interchangeably with hanging, indicating that in Roman Palestine the two were seen as similar or identical.

	•	Roman crucifixion often entailed being nailed or tied to a stake or tree, which aligns with the imagery of being “hung.”



In Rabbinic Writings

	•	“Hanging” someone usually meant execution by stoning, followed by displaying the body on a stake or tree.

	•	Mishnah Sanhedrin 6:4: “All who are stoned are afterward hanged. The men are hanged facing the people… but women are not hanged.”

	•	The order is important: the person is stoned first, then hung for public disgrace.



So when Sanhedrin 43a says:

*“On the eve of Passover, Yeshu was hanged…”*

…it likely implies that:

	•	He was judged by a Jewish court.

	•	He was accused of sorcery and apostasy.

	•	He was executed and publicly shamed, which in later Roman-Jewish contexts parallels crucifixion.



5. Post-Talmudic Jewish Interpretations (Kabbalists and Later Commentators)

Many Kabbalists and mystical interpreters treated the references to Yeshu esoterically. Not all even identified him with Jesus of Nazareth. But for those who did, they often reframed the crucifixion in symbolic or cosmic terms.



A. Kabbalistic Polemics (e.g., 13th–16th c.)

	•	Some Jewish mystics saw Jesus as a klipah (a shell or husk)—a spiritual distortion of divine light.

	•	His crucifixion was interpreted as the manifestation of imbalance or judgment (*Gevurah*) overpowering *Chesed* (mercy).

	•	Isaac Luria’s school occasionally referenced the crucifixion symbolically—seeing it as a cosmic error or exile of divine sparks.



B. Sefer Nizzahon Vetus (13th c.)

	•	A Jewish polemical text written in response to Christian pressure.

	•	It denies Jesus was the Messiah and claims he was justly executed.

	•	His hanging is treated as a divine punishment for blasphemy, not as martyrdom.



C. Kabbalistic Commentary on “Hanging” as a Curse

	•	In Zoharic texts, “being hanged” is aligned with severance from the Tree of Life.

	•	To be “hung on a tree” in this metaphysical sense implies falling out of divine alignment—a soul suspended without center.



D. Hasidic and Modern Esoteric Views

Some later mystics (especially those influenced by interfaith or syncretic thought) reinterpreted Jesus more sympathetically:

	•	They saw his crucifixion as a tragic cosmic event misunderstood by both Jews and Christians.

	•	In a few rare Hasidic or mystical texts, Jesus is seen as a Tzadik who was distorted by history, but this is not mainstream.



6. Who is a Tzadik in Jewish Mysticism?

	•	A tzadik is not just a moral person but a mystical channel—someone who unites the upper and lower worlds, often likened to the sefirot of Yesod (Foundation).

	•	A tzadik bears the burden of others’ sins, intercedes for the world, and acts as a “merkavah” (chariot) for the divine presence (Shekhinah).

	•	In Hasidism, the tzadik becomes a reincarnation of Moses, Joseph, or Adam, repairing some primordial spiritual flaw.



7. Jesus as a Tzadik: The Mystical Reframing (Rare Views)

These interpretations appear very rarely and usually under the radar due to Christian persecution and Jewish internal taboos. But some Hasidic or Kabbalistic thinkers, particularly those with universalist or messianic inclinations, toyed with this concept:



A. Jesus as a Tragic Tzadik

Some Kabbalists or mystics suggested:

	•	Jesus may have been a Jewish mystic or would-be tzadik, possibly a student of spiritual secrets (e.g., Ma’aseh Merkavah, mystical chariot visions).

	•	He might have been misunderstood or betrayed—not only by Rome but by both Jews and Christians, who failed to grasp his inner mission.

	•	His suffering was interpreted through the lens of the tzadik who suffers for the generation—a concept rooted in Isaiah 53 and Talmudic stories of martyrs like Rabbi Akiva.



B. Rabbi Elijah Ben Solomon (the Vilna Gaon)

	•	Though not sympathetic to Christianity, the Vilna Gaon made cryptic statements about “a soul that erred in its mission”, which later commentators have speculated could allude to Jesus—not as the Messiah, but as a failed or broken tzadik whose mission was tragically redirected.



8. Jewish-Christian Kabbalistic Syncretism (16th–19th c.)

In places like Safed (Tzfat) or later Eastern Europe, where mystics lived in multi-religious settings:



A. Abraham Miguel Cardozo (17th c. Sabbatean)

	•	Taught that all religions had sparks of truth, and that the Christian image of Jesus as a crucified redeemer held a distorted version of the tzadik suffering for the world.

	•	Cardozo saw Jesus as a “fallen spark”—not divine, but a misdirected soul who channeled real spiritual light through a broken vessel.



B. Jakob Frank and Frankism (18th c.)

	•	Claimed Jesus was a tzadik with a secret esoteric mission—a precursor to Shabbetai Tzvi.

	•	Frank viewed Christianity as a necessary “fall into Edom”, a kind of divine exile of the Shekhinah through which ultimate redemption would come.

	•	Though heretical by Jewish standards, Frankist Jews interpreted the cross as a kabbalistic symbol of Tiferet and Malkhut in tension—Jesus as an unwilling bridge between divine opposites.



9. Hasidic Echoes and Mystical Universalism

A. Rebbe Nachman of Bratslav (though cautious)

	•	Frequently emphasized the idea of the hidden tzadik, the “Tzadik Nistar,” unknown even to himself or the world.

	•	Spoke cryptically of a redeemer mocked, rejected, and exiled, in language that can resonate with Christian typology, though he never identified this figure as Jesus.



B. Rav Kook (early 20th c. Jewish mystic and Zionist)

	•	Believed Christianity was part of God’s universal plan, and that its ideals of compassion and sacrifice were rooted in Judaism.

	•	He did not affirm Jesus as a tzadik directly, but suggested that Christianity’s core values reflect distorted sparks of the Torah.

	•	Jesus may have been a failed tzadik, but his ideals would one day be redeemed back into Israel.



10. Philosophical Echoes: Leo Baeck, Martin Buber

Some Jewish philosophers saw Jesus as:

	•	A Jewish prophetic figure, albeit misunderstood.

	•	Leo Baeck called him “a brother” who had been ripped from the family and mythologized.

	•	Martin Buber respected Jesus as a Hasidic-like figure, close to God in the “I–Thou” relationship, but never as the Messiah.



11. Isaiah 53: The Suffering Servant as Hidden Tzadik

Isaiah 53 is classically interpreted by Christians as a prophecy of Jesus, but in Judaism it’s often seen as referring to Israel, the righteous remnant, or a tzadik suffering on behalf of others.



Key verses:

*“He was despised and rejected by men, a man of sorrows and acquainted with grief… Yet he bore our sicknesses and carried our pains… He was pierced for our transgressions, crushed for our iniquities… And by his wounds we are healed.”* — Isaiah 53:3–5

Jewish Interpretations that Parallel Tzadik Doctrine:

	•	Midrash Tanhuma (c. 5th c.) links the suffering servant to Moses or other righteous men who bear expiation for the generation.

	•	Zohar II:212a interprets Isaiah 53 as referring to the Tzadik, whose soul is rooted in Yesod, the Sefirah that channels divine energy to the world.

	•	Talmud (Sotah 14a): “The Messiah, what is his name? The Rabbis say: ‘The leper scholar’… for it is written, ‘Surely he has borne our griefs.’” This directly links the Messiah to Isaiah 53 and suffering.



2. Zoharic Shekhinah Theology: Exile, Tzadik, and the Crucified Presence

In the Zohar, the Shekhinah is the feminine Divine Presence. She is:

	•	In exile with Israel.

	•	Wounded by sin, trapped in the lower realms.

	•	In need of reunion with her masculine counterpart (Tiferet or the Tzadik) to bring redemption.



Key Parallels:

	•	The Shekhinah suffers silently for the sins of the world—*just like the servant in Isaiah 53*.

	•	The tzadik in Zohar is the spiritual Moses or Joseph figure who helps elevate the Shekhinah back to her throne. He bears suffering willingly, often anonymously (*tzadik nistar*).

	•	The Sefirah of Yesod (linked to the tzadik) is called the “foundation of the world”—echoing Proverbs 10:25: *“The righteous one is the foundation of the world.”*

Zohar I:204a — “The righteous is the pillar that upholds the world… When he departs, judgment increases.”

This tzadik is symbolically “crucified” in the sense that he is suspended, torn between heaven and earth, bearing the sins of his generation until redemption.
