II. KEY TRANSITIONS BETWEEN PERIODS

From First to Second Temple:

	•	Exile shifted focus from land-based, temple-centric worship to portable forms: Torah, fasting, prayer.

	•	Loss of Davidic kingship led to messianic hope for restoration.

	•	Babylonian and Persian influence introduced new cosmological and ethical ideas (e.g. afterlife, angels, dualism).



From Second Temple to Rabbinic Judaism:

	•	Temple destruction (70 CE) was a seismic theological shift:

	•	Priestly class lost its central role.

	•	Rabbinic sages adapted the sacrificial system into halakhic frameworks and prayer rituals.

	•	Textual study became the new “sacrifice”—study as worship.

	•	Judaism became decentralized, adaptable, and resilient in exile.

IV. Summary of Evolution

	•	First Temple Judaism: Sacrificial, monarchic, tribal; Yahweh as Israel’s god among many; covenant and land-centered.

	•	Second Temple Judaism: Diverse, sectarian, apocalyptic; Torah-centric but temple-active; growing monotheism.

	•	Rabbinic Judaism: Scholarly, textual, diasporic; no temple or sacrifices; deepened halakhah and mystical streams.
