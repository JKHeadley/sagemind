# Brief Review of Jewish Kabbalah



Jewish Kabbalah, particularly as it developed in medieval Spain and France, represents one of the most profound and influential mystical traditions in Judaism. Here’s a thorough breakdown covering its historical roots, key texts, major figures, central concepts, and its philosophical/theological innovations, specifically in the 12th to 15th centuries.



1. Historical Context: Medieval Europe

France (Provence & Languedoc) and Spain (especially Catalonia and Castile) were major centers of Jewish life and thought during the High Middle Ages. This period saw an explosion of philosophical, mystical, and poetic activity among Jews.

	•	11th–12th centuries: Jewish rationalism and Neoplatonic philosophy dominated, especially influenced by Islamic thinkers like Avicenna and Averroes, and Jewish philosophers like Maimonides.

	•	Mid-to-late 12th century: In reaction to extreme rationalism, mystical traditions began to emerge more visibly in southern France (Provence) and spread to Spain.



2. Provençal Beginnings (France)

The earliest identifiable Kabbalists arose in Provence:

	•	Key Figures:

	•	Rabbi Isaac the Blind (1160–1235): Considered one of the first Kabbalists. He lived in Lunel and helped systematize early mystical speculation.

	•	Rabbi Azriel of Gerona and Rabbi Ezra of Gerona: Disciple of Isaac the Blind. Azriel began articulating ideas of the Sefirot using Neoplatonic language.



Main Themes:

	•	Ein Sof: God as the Infinite, utterly unknowable.

	•	Emanation: The idea that creation unfolds in a series of emanations, not through direct action.

	•	Sefirot: Ten divine attributes or vessels through which the Infinite interacts with the world.

	•	Symbolism and Meditation: Use of Hebrew letters and Divine Names for spiritual ascent.



3. Catalan and Castilian Kabbalah (Spain)

Kabbalah in Spain took a deeper metaphysical and symbolic turn and led to the creation of the Zohar, the most famous Kabbalistic text.



a. Gerona Circle

	•	Located in Catalonia; intellectual inheritors of the Provençal tradition.

	•	Key figures:

	•	Nachmanides (Ramban): Talmudist and Kabbalist who used mystical interpretation in his biblical commentary.

	•	Azriel and Ezra of Gerona: Continued speculative metaphysics of the Sefirot.



b. Castilian Kabbalists

	•	More oriented toward mythic-symbolic Kabbalah, less philosophical.

	•	Abraham Abulafia (1240–1291): Founder of Prophetic Kabbalah.

	•	Used techniques of letter permutation, breathing, visualization, and trance.

	•	Sought mystical union with God (devekut).

	•	Regarded as a controversial, almost messianic figure.



c. The Zohar and Moses de León (c. 1240–1305)

	•	The Zohar (“Book of Splendor”) appeared in Castile in the late 13th century.

	•	Attributed to Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, a 2nd-century sage, but likely authored (or compiled) by Moses de León.

	•	Written in a mystical Aramaic.

	•	Structure: Commentary on the Torah; filled with mythical dialogues, mystical interpretations, and symbolic imagery.

	•	Emphasized:

	•	The divine drama within the Godhead (between Sefirot).

	•	Shekhinah: Feminine aspect of divinity in exile; yearning for reunion with the male aspect.

	•	Human actions affect the harmony of divine realms.



4. Core Concepts of Spanish-French Kabbalah

a. Ein Sof (אין סוף) – The Infinite

	•	God is beyond comprehension or description.

	•	Not “God” in a personal sense—more like the source of all being.



b. Sefirot (ספירות) – Emanations

	•	Ten divine attributes or vessels that manifest God’s will.

	•	Arranged in a Tree of Life diagram, often in three columns (right, left, center).

	•	From top to bottom:

	1.	Keter (Crown)

	2.	Chokhmah (Wisdom)

	3.	Binah (Understanding)

	4.	Chesed (Mercy)

	5.	Gevurah (Judgment)

	6.	Tiferet (Beauty)

	7.	Netzach (Victory)

	8.	Hod (Glory)

	9.	Yesod (Foundation)

	10.	Malkhut (Kingdom / Shekhinah)



c. Tzimtzum (Contraction)

	•	Later developed, but early ideas appear:

	•	God had to “contract” Himself to make space for the world.

d. Shekhinah in Exile

	•	The feminine aspect of God is in exile along with Israel.

	•	Mystical purpose of Torah and mitzvot: to reunite Shekhinah with the higher Sefirot and restore cosmic harmony.



5. Ritual and Practice

	•	Meditative Techniques:

	•	Contemplation of Hebrew letters and Divine Names.

	•	Breathing patterns.

	•	Letter permutations (especially in Prophetic Kabbalah).

	•	Ethical emphasis: Righteous behavior has cosmic consequences.

	•	Mystical exegesis: The Torah has secret levels (Sod) that Kabbalah reveals.



6. Later Developments and Expulsion

	•	After the Spanish Expulsion of 1492, Kabbalists fled to places like Safed (Tzfat) in Ottoman Palestine, where Lurianic Kabbalah (Isaac Luria) emerged, heavily building on and transforming earlier Spanish-French traditions.

	•	The mysticism of exile and redemption became more intense post-expulsion.



7. Legacy

	•	The Spanish-French Kabbalists:

	•	Systematized and deeply enriched Jewish mysticism.

	•	Created a mythic language for the inner workings of the Divine.

	•	Inspired later Jewish messianic movements (e.g., Sabbateanism, Hasidism).

	•	Influenced Christian Kabbalah during the Renaissance (Pico della Mirandola, Reuchlin).

	•	Their metaphysical ideas resemble Neoplatonism, Gnosticism, and even Hermetic traditions.
