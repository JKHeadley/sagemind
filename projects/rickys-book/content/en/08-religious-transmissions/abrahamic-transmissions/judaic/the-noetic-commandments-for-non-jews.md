# The Noetic Commandments for Non-Jews



The Noetic Commandments, more accurately known as the Noachide Laws or Noahide Laws, are a set of seven universal moral laws that, according to Jewish tradition, were given by God to all humanity through Noah after the Flood. They form the ethical backbone of Jewish views on the obligations of non-Jews (Bnei Noach, or “Children of Noah”) and are seen as the minimum standards for righteous living outside the Jewish covenant.



These laws appear in Talmudic sources (e.g., Sanhedrin 56–60) and were later codified by medieval Jewish philosophers, including Maimonides. Though they aren’t found as a list in the Torah itself, rabbinic tradition derives them from Genesis and early biblical texts.

II. Sources and Foundations

	•	Genesis 9: God’s covenant with Noah and his descendants after the Flood.

	•	Talmud, Sanhedrin 56a–60a: Main rabbinic discussion of the Noahide laws.

	•	Maimonides, *Mishneh Torah, Laws of Kings and Wars 8–10*: Codifies and explains the Noahide obligations.



These laws are considered universal, predating Sinai and the Mosaic Law. Jews are obligated to follow the 613 mitzvot; non-Jews, only these seven, unless they choose to convert.



III. Broader Interpretations and Application

A. Ethical Framework

	•	These laws are minimum ethical standards for any civilized society.

	•	Their violation represents a breakdown in moral order and social cohesion.



B. Spiritual Purpose

	•	According to Maimonides, non-Jews who follow the Noahide laws because they are divinely commanded (not merely because of rational ethics) are considered righteous among the nations and have a share in the World to Come (*Olam HaBa*).



C. Expansion and Commentary

Later Jewish thinkers expanded each law into subcategories:

	•	“Do not murder” includes war ethics, abortion, and care for human life.

	•	“Do not steal” includes fair business practices and avoiding deceit.

	•	“Establish courts” includes ensuring justice for all and protecting the vulnerable.



Some Kabbalistic and Hasidic traditions see these laws as a gateway to higher spiritual awareness for non-Jews, a path to connecting with divine wisdom through ethical living.

V. Modern Movements and Outreach

1. Noahide Movement

	•	In recent decades, some Orthodox Jewish communities (especially Chabad-Lubavitch) promote the Noahide laws to non-Jews as a framework for righteous living.

	•	The movement encourages moral society without conversion to Judaism.



2. Legal and Cultural Advocacy

	•	In 1991, the U.S. Congress, via Public Law 102-14, recognized the Noahide laws as foundational to civilization and encouraged their observance as ethical guidance.
