# Review of Nazorean/Galilean Christians (Part 3. Speculative Gospel Reconstruction)



Reconstructing the Gospel of Matthew to fit the Nazorean (or Nazarene) sect of Christianity would require significant changes, as the Nazoreans are believed to have been a Jewish-Christian sect that maintained stricter adherence to Jewish law and saw Jesus as a fully human messianic figure rather than a divine being. Here’s how the Gospel of Matthew might be altered to align with their perspective:

1. Jesus as a Fully Human Prophet

	•	Matthew’s high Christology, which subtly presents Jesus as divine (e.g., the virgin birth in Matthew 1:23 citing Isaiah 7:14 as a prophecy of Jesus), would be downplayed or removed.

	•	Instead, Jesus would be portrayed primarily as the Jewish Messiah, a prophet, and a teacher of righteousness in the tradition of Moses and the prophets.

	•	His authority would be emphasized as coming from God, but not as divine in himself.

2. Reinforcing the Torah’s Eternal Validity

	•	Matthew 5:17-20 already emphasizes that Jesus did not come to abolish the Law but to fulfill it. This would likely be even stronger, perhaps stating explicitly that all followers should observe the Torah, including circumcision and dietary laws, however not obligated to.

	•	Any passages that suggest Jesus reinterpreted or modified the Law (e.g., Matthew 12:1-8, where Jesus allows his disciples to pick grain on the Sabbath) might be revised or removed.

	•	Jesus would be shown correcting misinterpretations of the Law rather than seeming to relax or transcend it.

3. Stronger Opposition to Paul’s Teachings

	•	The Nazoreans are believed to have rejected Paul as an apostate who distorted Jesus’ message.

	•	Any passages that could be interpreted as supporting Paul’s ideas of salvation through faith alone, rather than through works of the Law, would be altered to emphasize righteous action, obedience to the Torah, and repentance.

	•	A possible inclusion could be direct warnings against “false apostles” who teach against the Law.

4. A Stronger Jewish Identity and Less Gentile Inclusion

	•	The original Matthew already has a Jewish orientation, but passages like the Great Commission (Matthew 28:19-20), which instructs disciples to preach to all nations, might be reinterpreted to mean converting Gentiles into followers of Jewish law rather than simply making them believers.

	•	The portrayal of Gentile figures like the Roman centurion (Matthew 8:5-13) might be adjusted to emphasize his conversion to Jewish practice rather than just his faith in Jesus.

5. A Different Perspective on the Resurrection

	•	The Nazoreans likely saw Jesus’ resurrection as a spiritual vindication rather than an event proving his divinity.

	•	Instead of an emphasis on physical appearances and worship of the risen Christ (e.g., Matthew 28:9, where the women worship Jesus), the focus might be on his resurrection as a sign of God’s favor, similar to how Enoch and Elijah were taken up.

	•	More emphasis could be placed on Jesus’ role as a teacher whose message continues through his followers rather than as a figure to be worshiped.

6. Different Treatment of Pharisees and the Jewish Leadership

	•	While Matthew’s gospel is highly critical of the Pharisees (e.g., Matthew 23), the Nazorean version might depict a more nuanced view, acknowledging Pharisaic teachers who were righteous.

	•	More focus could be placed on internal Jewish reform rather than outright condemnation of Jewish leaders as a whole.

	•	Instead of placing primary blame for Jesus’ death on the Jewish authorities, the responsibility might shift more towards the Romans, aligning with the Nazorean view that Rome was the true enemy.

7. Stronger Apocalyptic and Messianic Expectations

	•	There might be more emphasis on Jesus as the forerunner of the final age and the coming of the “True Prophet” or an even greater Messianic fulfillment.
