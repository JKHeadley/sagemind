# Modern Neo-Templar Mythology



1. Core Claims of Modern Neo-Templar Mythology

	1.	Jesus did not die on the cross:

	•	Crucifixion was designed to be a long process. The Gospel narrative notes Jesus died unusually quickly.

	•	Modern Templar and esoteric interpretations claim this was a staged crucifixion.

	•	The sponge with vinegar and gall may have included a sedative (based on the Gospel of Matthew 27:48–50).

	•	Joseph of Arimathea (a secret disciple with high social standing) intervened to remove Jesus before death.

	2.	Jesus survived and went into exile:

	•	After the event, Jesus was revived in the tomb, and taken into exile.

	•	Accompanied by Mary Magdalene, possibly with children, and sometimes his mother Mary.

	•	They traveled by boat to Southern France, particularly the region of Provence, or Catalonia in Northeastern Spain.

	•	This is tied to the medieval legend of “The Three Marys” arriving in Saintes-Maries-de-la-Mer.

	3.	Mary Magdalene as Jesus’ wife:

	•	Mary Magdalene is interpreted not as a prostitute, but as a high initiatic priestess, sometimes associated with Egyptian or Gnostic traditions. Her sin was being educated in alchemy under Jesus’ tutelage.

	•	She was Jesus’ spiritual equal or consort.

	•	The Gospel of Philip refers to Mary as Jesus’ “companion” (Greek: *koinōnos*) and mentions Jesus “kissing her often.”

	4.	The bloodline (Sang Réal):

	•	Jesus and Mary Magdalene had at least one child, giving rise to a “Holy Blood” lineage (*Sang Réal* or “San Greal” = Holy Grail).

	•	This bloodline merged with Merovingian nobility, who ruled parts of France in the early medieval period (5th–8th centuries).

	•	The Merovingians were supposedly descendants of Jesus, and thus of divine or messianic blood.

	•	The Templars were the guardians of this lineage and its secrets.

	5.	The Priory of Sion and hidden custodians:

	•	A secret order, the Priory of Sion, supposedly preserved this knowledge.

	•	The Templars were aligned with the Priory or were its military wing.

	•	This narrative was widely popularized by Pierre Plantard, Henry Lincoln, Michael Baigent, Richard Leigh, and Dan Brown (*The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail*, *The Da Vinci Code*).

	6.	Templar exile and preservation in Spain:

	•	When the Templars were suppressed in 1307, many supposedly fled to Aragon, Catalonia, and Portugal.

	•	In these regions, they continued to guard esoteric relics, genealogies, and bloodlines.

	•	Some modern orders claim continuity with Spanish and Portuguese Templar branches, including:

	•	*Ordo Supremus Militaris Templi Hierosolymitani* (OSMTH)

	•	*Ordo Templi Orientis* (OTO) in its esoteric-magical form

	•	*Sovereign Military Order of the Temple of Jerusalem* (SMOTJ)



2. Key Textual and Esoteric Influences

	•	Gnostic Gospels (e.g. Gospel of Philip, Gospel of Mary): Emphasize hidden knowledge, sacred union, and resurrection as symbolic rather than literal.

	•	The Gospel of Barnabas (a medieval Islamic-influenced text): Claims Jesus was not crucified, but Judas was substituted in his place.

	•	Qur’anic account (Surah 4:157): “They did not kill him, nor did they crucify him, but it was made to appear so.”

	•	Cathar beliefs: The body was a prison; Jesus’ suffering was either symbolic or illusory. The Cathars were seen by modern Templars as ideological ancestors.

	•	Kabbalah and Grail literature: Allegorical readings of “divine kingship,” blood, and vessels (grail = womb, lineage).



3. Modern Templar Orders and Their Beliefs

Some neo-Templar or esoteric groups uphold this narrative as central or influential to their philosophy:



a. L’Ordre Souverain du Temple Initiatique (OSTI)

	•	Claims direct spiritual lineage from original Templars.

	•	Emphasizes Gnostic Christianity, sacred feminine, and the Magdalene.

b. L’Ordre de la Rose-Croix Véritas (ORCV)

	•	A Rosicrucian/Templar hybrid.

	•	Teaches that Jesus and Mary Magdalene were initiates of the Egyptian mysteries.

	•	Their union birthed the western esoteric tradition.

c. Rex Deus Theory (Graham Phillips, Tim Wallace-Murphy)

	•	“Rex Deus” = “King God” – claims that a priestly bloodline from Jesus and Mary Magdalene has guided Western history through secret societies.



d. Sovereign and Military Order of the Temple of Jerusalem (SMOTJ)

	•	More conservative and Christian-oriented.

	•	Does not officially hold the Jesus-survival theory, but individuals within or adjacent to it may.

4. Geographical Centers in France and Spain

	•	Rennes-le-Château:

	•	Village in Southern France central to Holy Grail mythology.

	•	Associated with Father Bérenger Saunière and alleged secrets of Jesus’ bloodline.

	•	Surrounding Languedoc region was a Cathar stronghold.

	•	Montserrat, Catalonia:

	•	Sacred mountain often associated with Marian visions.

	•	Some claim hidden relics or documents of the Templars exist in nearby monasteries.

	•	Navarre and Aragon:

	•	Regions where exiled Templars found refuge after their suppression in France.

	•	Some say the Grailean bloodline was protected among local noble families.



5. Symbolism in Their Beliefs

	•	The Grail = womb of Mary Magdalene.

	•	The Cross = esoteric tree of life or misunderstood Masonic symbol.

	•	The Rose = sacred feminine or the Magdalene.

	•	The Sword and Grail union = masculine-feminine balance, Templar duty to protect the “living vessel.”



6. Criticisms and Scholarly Perspective

	•	These beliefs are generally considered pseudohistory, often built on:

	•	Misinterpretations of Gnostic texts.

	•	Forgeries (like the Priory of Sion documents).

	•	Conflated legends and symbolic readings.

	•	However, even critics acknowledge these ideas have psychological and mythopoetic appeal, especially within esoteric and initiatory contexts.



7. The Magdalene Cult in Provence and Its Syncretism with Black Madonna Worship

This strand of tradition weaves together Catholic legend, folk mysticism, esoteric Christianity, and pre-Christian goddess remnants, all centered around Mary Magdalene’s alleged arrival in Southern France.



A. Arrival in Provence: The Legend

	•	According to medieval Provençal tradition, Mary Magdalene, along with:

	•	Mary Salome (mother of James and John)

	•	Mary Jacobe (sister of the Virgin Mary)

	•	Lazarus, her brother

	•	Maximinus, a disciple of Jesus

	•	Possibly Sarah la Kali, a mysterious dark-skinned servant or companion

	•	Fled the Holy Land after Jesus’ crucifixion and persecution of his followers.

	•	Landed at Les Saintes-Maries-de-la-Mer, in Camargue, a swampy region near Arles.

	•	Mary Magdalene eventually withdrew to a cave in La Sainte-Baume, where she lived in penitence for 30 years.



B. La Sainte-Baume and the Grotto Cult

	•	The grotto of Sainte-Baume became a pilgrimage site as early as the 5th century.

	•	Medieval pilgrims and even royalty (like Charles VII and King Louis XI) visited it.

	•	The cave was seen as a sacred womb-like space, connecting Magdalene to earth-goddess traditions.

	•	The nearby Basilica of Saint-Maximin-la-Sainte-Baume allegedly houses her relics (discovered in 1279 under Charles II of Naples).



C. Mary Magdalene as the Black Madonna

	•	The Black Madonna is a widespread European icon of a dark-skinned Virgin or female saint, often venerated in secluded mountain chapels, caves, or forest shrines.

	•	In many regions (including Provence, Auvergne, and Catalonia), the Black Madonna is interchangeable with Mary Magdalene.

	•	Some theories:

	•	Her darkness represents her Egyptian or Semitic roots.

	•	It symbolizes the alchemical nigredo – the first stage of spiritual transformation.

	•	It’s an echo of pre-Christian goddess worship, especially of Isis, Demeter, or Cybele.



D. Syncretic Layers

	•	The Magdalene cult merged with:

	•	Local goddess traditions (e.g., the Roman goddess Diana of Ephesus).

	•	Isiac rites, particularly through the port cities of Marseille and Arles.

	•	Gnostic symbols: the cave as the interior temple, the veil of Sophia, the descent into matter.

	•	Sarah la Kali, the Black Madonna venerated by the Romani people, is said to be:

	•	A servant of the Magdalene.

	•	Or her daughter.

	•	Or a syncretic Romani goddess merged with Magdalene’s lineage.

	•	Annual pilgrimages by the Gitan (Romani) people still occur every May to Saintes-Maries-de-la-Mer, carrying a statue of Sarah la Kali into the sea.



E. Templar and Cathar Connection

	•	The Cathars in nearby Languedoc revered Mary Magdalene as Sophia, the embodiment of divine wisdom and spiritual bride of the Logos.

	•	Templars were active in the same regions and are believed by neo-Templars to have:

	•	Protected the Magdalene cult.

	•	Hid heretical texts in grottos and remote chapels.

	•	Maintained esoteric teachings of hieros gamos (sacred union) and divine bloodlines.



8. Grailean Lineages and Templar Protection in Spain (Navarre and Aragon)

The second path focuses on how Spanish regions like Navarre, Aragon, and Catalonia became the new strongholds of Templar exiles and potential descendants of Jesus and Mary Magdalene.



A. Templar Refuge in Iberia

	•	After the suppression of the Templars in France (1307), many knights:

	•	Escaped across the Pyrenees into Aragon, Catalonia, and Navarre.

	•	Were absorbed into or disguised within other orders, especially:

	•	Order of Montesa (Crown of Aragon)

	•	Order of Christ (Portugal)

	•	Monarchs like James II of Aragon gave sanctuary and land to former Templars.



B. Grail Legend and Catalonia

	•	The Holy Grail legend was popularized in Spain via Chrétien de Troyes and Wolfram von Eschenbach (who based *Parzival* partly on Iberian sources).

	•	Montserrat, a mountain sanctuary in Catalonia, became linked to:

	•	The Grail mystery.

	•	Hidden Templar relics.

	•	Mysterious Black Madonnas.

	•	Templar chapels and carved reliefs in the region often show Grail imagery, knights, and feminine figures with vessels.



C. Noble Families and Hidden Bloodlines

	•	Certain Spanish noble houses were rumored to be descended from Jesus, including:

	•	The House of Lara (Castile)

	•	The Counts of Toulouse (France and Catalonia)

	•	The Counts of Foix and Béarn, associated with Occitan Catharism

	•	The House of Barcelona intermarried with both Merovingian and Visigothic lines, seen as preservers of sacred descent.



D. Documents and Myths

	•	Alleged secret Templar archives preserved in:

	•	Huesca, Aragon

	•	Montserrat monastery

	•	Private noble collections

	•	Modern groups claim these contain:

	•	Genealogies of the Grail bloodline

	•	Knowledge of Gnostic initiatory rites

	•	Sacred maps and instructions for Templar successors

E. Modern Revivalism

	•	Neo-Templar groups in Spain and France often trace their “mission” to:

	•	Protecting the royal bloodline of Jesus and Magdalene.

	•	Restoring Sophia-Christ gnosis.

	•	Preserving the feminine mysteries and Grail codes in art, geometry, and sacred geography.

	•	These orders include:

	•	The Order of the Temple of Montserrat

	•	The Templari de Catalunya

	•	Some branches of OTO and Martinist orders

Templars, Moorish Sufis, and the Hidden Jesus Bloodline

9. Historical Interface: The Spanish Frontier

	•	Between the 8th and 15th centuries, Al-Andalus (Muslim Spain) became a cosmopolitan center of philosophy, mysticism, science, and religious tolerance (at least in cycles).

	•	There was frequent exchange across enemy lines:

	•	Knights and monks traveled under truce.

	•	Muslim scholars translated Christian texts into Arabic and vice versa.

	•	Templars were known to respect Sufi mystics, even during the Crusades.



Key theory: Certain Moorish Sufi tariqas (orders) passed on esoteric knowledge to Templars, especially in regions like Toledo, Seville, and Granada, where Kabbalah, Christian mysticism, and Islamic esotericism mingled.



10. Sufi Orders Most Often Linked to Templar Transmission

A. The Ikhwān al-Ṣafā’ (“Brethren of Purity”)

	•	A secretive esoteric society active in Basra, Iraq (10th century) but whose encyclopedia of mystical philosophy made its way to Al-Andalus.

	•	Blended Greek Neoplatonism, Hermeticism, and Shi’a Ismaili gnosis.

	•	Later influenced Moorish scholars in Córdoba and Seville.

	•	Some modern Templar groups claim that the Ikhwān’s initiatory structure was mirrored in early Templar degrees.



B. The Shādhilī and Suhrawardī Orders

	•	These Sufi orders emphasized gnosis through divine light (*ishrāq*), similar to Templar solar symbolism.

	•	Shādhilīs were known to operate in North Africa and Iberia.

	•	Some fringe theories suggest they protected a hidden Nazarene wisdom tradition—one in which Jesus (ʿĪsā) didn’t die but escaped east or west.



11. The “Jesus-Sufi” Bridge in Al-Andalus

A. Muslim Views of Jesus (ʿĪsā)

	•	Islam holds that Jesus was not crucified but was:

	•	Raised (rafaʿa) to heaven.

	•	Or his image was placed on another man (a docetic interpretation).

	•	Many Sufis, however, went further:

	•	They saw Jesus as the Seal of the Saints (*khatm al-awliyā*ʾ), the spiritual mirror of Muhammad as the Seal of the Prophets.

	•	In Ibn ʿArabī’s cosmology, Jesus represents the Pole of Love (Quṭb al-ḥubb) — the spiritual heart of the world.



B. Andalusian Sufi View

	•	Ibn ʿArabī, born in Murcia (1165), wrote of Jesus as:

	•	The perfect human (*al-insān al-kāmil*).

	•	An initiator into divine gnosis.

	•	He taught that Christ’s return is symbolic of gnosis rising within the heart, not necessarily a literal Second Coming.



Templar connection: Esoteric Templar groups claim that these teachings survived in Spain, and that Templars absorbed them through contact with Andalusian mystics during truces and even secret pacts.



12. Moorish Custodians of the Bloodline

Some modern Templar and Grail groups (especially those influenced by Sufism, Martinism, or the Traditionalist School) believe:



A. Jesus Survived and Went West

	•	Jesus, with Magdalene and their daughter (often called Sarah), fled to Egypt, and later crossed into Berber North Africa and Andalusia.

	•	The Moors, particularly initiated Sufis, recognized the sanctity of the bloodline and acted as custodians of their gnosis and possibly descendants.



B. Sarah as the Sufi-Saint

	•	Sarah la Kali (venerated in France) may derive from:

	•	The “Kāli” archetype (dark goddess of transformation).

	•	Or from Sarah bat Yeshua, hidden among the Sufi lineages of North Africa.

	•	Some claim she was initiated by Moorish mystics and married into Berber or Gitano tribes, preserving the bloodline through gypsy networks protected by Sufi brotherhoods.
