# Review of Nazorean/Galilean Christians (Part 1.  John the Baptist and The 72 Followers)



The Nazoreans were an ancient religious group mentioned in the New Testament, particularly in relation to early Christianity. The term “Nazorean” (or “Nazarene”) is sometimes used to refer to early followers of Jesus Christ, often used interchangeably with “Christians” in certain biblical texts. However, the term may have different interpretations based on context.

	1.	Historical Context: The term likely refers to Jesus of Nazareth and his followers. “Nazorean” could mean people from Nazareth, the town where Jesus grew up, though it may have also signified a specific sect with distinctive beliefs.

	2.	Relation to the Essenes: Some scholars suggest that the Nazoreans might have been connected to the Essenes, a Jewish sect known for their ascetic lifestyle, monastic communities, and apocalyptic beliefs. While the exact connection is debated, some early Christian groups may have shared similar ideas with the Essenes.

	3.	Nazorean as a Sect: There were also groups in the early centuries that identified as Nazoreans, often viewed as Jewish Christians, distinct from the broader Roman Church. These groups maintained Jewish traditions and practices while following Jesus.

The Essenes were known for their strict ascetic lifestyle, which included celibacy as part of their commitment to purity. Some Essene communities, especially those at Qumran, practiced celibacy, believing that refraining from sexual relations was a way to remain spiritually pure and avoid defilement. They also had strict rules about marriage and procreation, seeing celibacy as a path to a higher form of spiritual dedication.

The Nazoreans, on the other hand, did not generally embrace celibacy in the same way. In fact, the teachings of Jesus, as recorded in the New Testament, do not advocate for celibacy but rather for a focus on spiritual matters and ethical behavior. While some early Christians may have chosen celibacy for personal or spiritual reasons (for instance, some followers of Jesus like Paul suggested it as a way of dedicating oneself fully to God), celibacy was not a central tenet of the Nazorean movement. In fact, Jesus himself is often depicted as interacting with families, eating meals with them, and even performing miracles related to family life (e.g., the healing of Peter’s mother-in-law), suggesting that marriage and family life were not inherently opposed to his teachings.

Therefore, the Essenes’ emphasis on celibacy likely stood in contrast to the more inclusive, family-oriented approach of the Nazoreans, being the ultimate contribution to their schism. This issue of celibacy vs. marriage could have been a theological and practical point of contention between the two groups, especially as early Christian teachings began to spread and attract followers from a broader range of backgrounds.
