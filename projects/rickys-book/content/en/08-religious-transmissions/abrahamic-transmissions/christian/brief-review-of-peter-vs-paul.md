# Brief Review of Peter vs Paul



Peter and Paul were both foundational figures in early Christianity, but they had distinct backgrounds, roles, and theological emphases. Here are their key differences:

1. Background and Identity

	•	Peter (Simon Peter): A Jewish fisherman from Galilee, one of the original twelve apostles chosen by Jesus. He was a close companion of Jesus and a leader among the disciples.

	•	Paul (Saul of Tarsus): A Roman citizen and highly educated Pharisee from Tarsus. Initially, he persecuted Christians before converting after a vision of Christ on the road to Damascus. Never met Jesus personally.



2. Role in Early Christianity

	•	Peter: Considered the leader of the early Christian church in Jerusalem. He primarily ministered to Jews and is traditionally seen as the first Pope in Catholic tradition.

	•	Paul: The “Apostle to the Gentiles.” He spread Christianity beyond Jewish communities, founding churches across the Roman Empire.



3. Theological Focus

	•	Peter: Focused on Jesus as the Messiah of Israel and upheld Jewish traditions, though he later accepted Gentiles into the faith.

	•	Paul: Emphasized salvation through faith in Christ alone, teaching that adherence to Jewish law (such as circumcision) was unnecessary for Gentile converts.



4. Relationship with the Law

	•	Peter: Initially hesitant about breaking Jewish dietary laws but eventually accepted the inclusion of Gentiles after a vision (Acts 10).

	•	Paul: Argued strongly against the necessity of the Jewish law for salvation, which led to conflicts with Jewish Christians.



5. Writings and Legacy

	•	Peter: Traditionally attributed with writing 1 and 2 Peter. His legacy is tied to the authority of the Church, especially in Catholicism.

	•	Paul: Wrote many of the New Testament epistles (Romans, Corinthians, Galatians, etc.), shaping Christian theology significantly.



6. Death

	•	Peter: Traditionally believed to have been crucified upside down in Rome under Emperor Nero.

	•	Paul: Beheaded in Rome, likely also under Nero, as he was a Roman citizen and thus exempt from crucifixion.

7. Israel and Prophecy

- Peter: Israel is still standing and prophecy is being fulfilled. (Acts 2:16-17; 3:21,24-26)
- Paul: Israel is fallen and the prophecies are postponed (Rom 3:10, 19; 11:11, 25; 17:25)

8. Apostolic Responsibility

- Peter: Salvation is of the Jews led by the twelve apostles. (John 4:22; Mat 19: 28; 1 Pet 1:10; Rev 21:14)
- Paul: Salvation sent to Gentiles through Paul the Appstle of the Gentiles. (Rom 11:13; Col 1:25; 2 Tim 1:11; 1 Cor 3:10)

9. Justification

- Peter: Salvation and the law; justification by faith in Jesus as the Messiah accompanied by necessary works. (Jam 2:24; 1 John 2:5; May 19:27; Heb 10:26)
- Paul: salvation apart from the law; justification by faith alone in the cross work of Jesus Christ. (Rom 4:5; Eph 2:8-9; Tit 3:5; 1 Cor 15:1-4)

10. The Church

- Peter: Separation between the Jew and Gentile; Israel is God’s chosen people. Gentiles blessed through Israel. (Mat 10:5; Luke 24:49; Acts 11:19)
- Paul: No longer Jew nor Gentile but all are part of a new creature in Christ called his body. (Gal 3:28; Col 3:11; Eph 2:15; 1 Cor 12:13)

11. God’s Representation

- Peter: God’s holy nation, the little flock of Israel, is his earthly representation from among the world. (Isa 61:6; Rev 7: 1-8; 1 Pet 2-5, 9)
- Paul: Gentile Ambassadors are his representatives to a world in total rebellion. (2 Cor 5:20; 1 Cor 12:20; 2 Tim 2:2)

12. Destiny

- Peter: Promised to receive an earthly inheritance in the kingdom. (1 Pet 1:4; Mat 6:10; Heb 13:14; Rev 21:2)
- Paul: Promise of an heavenly position in God’s universal dominion. (Phil 3:20; Eph 2:6; 1 Thess 4:17)
