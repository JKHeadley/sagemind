# Review of Nazorean/Galilean Christians (Part 2. Gospel Composition Dating)



What is the Gospel of the Nazoreans?

      •	 Language: Originally written in Aramaic or Hebrew — Jerome claimed to have seen a Hebrew Gospel used by Nazoreans in Syria, which many believe refers to this text.

	•	Used by: Early Jewish-Christian sects like the Nazoreans (Nazarenes) — Torah-observant followers of Jesus who did not accept Paul’s teachings and likely saw Jesus as a human prophet or teacher.

	•	Status: Lost; only survives through quotes and fragments cited by early Church Fathers (like Jerome and Origen).

	•	Content: Probably similar to Matthew, but more Jewish in tone, possibly closer to an original Semitic gospel source.



Fragments of the Gospel of the Nazoreans:

While the Gospel of the Nazoreans no longer exists as a complete text, a few fragments and references survive in the writings of early Church Fathers. These fragments aren’t preserved in manuscripts labeled *“Gospel of the Nazoreans”*, but rather as citations or quotes within other authors’ works.

Estimated Date of Composition:

Based on internal evidence, Church Father testimony, and comparison with related texts, we can estimate the date of composition for the Gospel of the Nazoreans as follows:

Approx. 50–85 CE, with some scholars suggesting it could be:

	•	Earlier than the final form of canonical Matthew (ca. 80–90 CE),

	•	Possibly contemporary with or even prior to Mark (ca. 65–70 CE),

	•	Or a variant or Aramaic source behind Matthew — a so-called “Hebrew Matthew.”



Supporting Evidence:

1. Jerome’s Testimony (Late 4th Century)

	•	Jerome writes (in *De Viris Illustribus*, §3):

*“Matthew, who also is called Levi… composed a gospel of Christ in Hebrew letters for the benefit of those of the circumcision who had believed. Who it was that afterwards translated it into Greek is not certainly known.”*

	•	Jerome claims to have seen this Hebrew gospel used by Nazoreans in Syria, and many scholars identify it with the Gospel of the Nazoreans.



2. Semitic Features

	•	Fragments preserve Aramaic idioms and midrashic interpretations consistent with 1st-century Jewish-Christian thought.

	•	It lacks the strong anti-Pharisee polemic found in canonical Matthew, suggesting it may have come from a time before the full rift between synagogue and church.



3. Theological Simplicity

	•	No divinity of Jesus.

	•	Affirms the Law.

	•	Virgin birth is present, but no Trinitarian language or developed Christology.

This suggests an early, pre-Pauline or non-Pauline Christology, closer to Jewish norms — more compatible with the kind of theology preserved in Islam.



4. Relationship to Canonical Matthew

	•	Most scholars believe the Gospel of the Nazoreans is either:

	•	An Aramaic precursor to Matthew,

	•	A parallel version from the same early community, or

	•	A later recension of Matthew revised for Jewish-Christian audiences.

	•	If it’s prior to or contemporary with Matthew, then it could date to 50–80 CE.
