# Brief Review of the Valentinian School



The Valentinian School was one of the most intellectually sophisticated and influential strands of early Christian Gnosticism, flourishing in the 2nd and 3rd centuries CE. Founded by Valentinus, a Christian teacher who nearly became Bishop of Rome, the school developed a complex cosmology, soteriology, and theology that sought to reconcile elements of Platonic philosophy, Christian doctrine, and Gnostic myth.



I. Foundations: Who Was Valentinus?

A. Life and Background

	•	Valentinus (c. 100–160 CE) was likely born in Egypt, educated in Alexandria, and studied Platonism, Stoicism, and possibly Hermeticism.

	•	Converted to Christianity, Valentinus was a prominent teacher in Rome between 130–160 CE.

	•	Tertullian reports that he nearly became bishop of Rome, but was rejected and turned to develop his own theology.



B. Core Motivation

	•	Valentinus aimed to present a mystical and philosophical Christianity, integrating Pauline theology, particularly themes from Ephesians, Romans, and Colossians, with Platonic metaphysics and Jewish apocalyptic.



II. Valentinian Cosmology

Valentinian cosmology is deeply mythic and symbolic, portraying creation as a divine drama within a Pleroma (fullness) of divine beings called Aeons.



A. The Pleroma

	•	The Pleroma is the divine fullness, the realm of light and spirit, housing a series of 30 Aeons (in most accounts), arranged as syzygies (male-female pairs).

	•	Aeons are emanations from the First Father (also called Bythos, “Depth”).

	•	Notable Aeons include:

	•	Nous (Mind) and Aletheia (Truth)

	•	Logos (Word) and Zoe (Life)

	•	Anthropos (Man) and Ecclesia (Church)

B. Sophia and the Fall

	•	The drama of Sophia (Wisdom) is central.

	•	Sophia, the youngest Aeon, desires to know the unknowable Bythos.

	•	Her passion (pathos) causes a disruption in the Pleroma.

	•	She gives birth to a defective offspring, often called Achamoth or Sophia Prunikos, who becomes the mother of the material world.

	•	Sophia is eventually restored by a higher Aeon (Christ/Logos), but her fallen aspect is exiled outside the Pleroma.



C. The Creation of the Material World

	•	The fallen Sophia produces the Demiurge, an ignorant creator god.

	•	The Demiurge is often equated with the Old Testament God (but less harshly than in Sethianism).

	•	He is unaware of the higher realm and fashions the material cosmos.

	•	The material world is a mixture of spiritual (pneumatic), psychic (soulful), and hylic (material) elements.



III. Anthropology: Three Types of Humans

Valentinians categorize humanity into three ontological types, each with different fates:

	1.	Hylics (material)

	•	Bound to matter, passions, and ignorance.

	•	Unable to attain salvation.

	2.	Psychics (soulful)

	•	Moral, ethical beings capable of faith and basic understanding.

	•	May attain soulish salvation, often equated with orthodox Christians.

	3.	Pneumatics (spiritual)

	•	Have a divine seed (from Sophia or Pleroma).

	•	Destined for full Gnosis and return to the Pleroma.



IV. Christology and Soteriology

A. Christ’s Role

	•	Christ is not simply the historical Jesus but a heavenly Aeon who descends through multiple layers of being.

	•	Two or three distinct entities are often involved in Jesus:

	•	The heavenly Christ or Savior Aeon

	•	The psychic Christ, who indwells Jesus at baptism

	•	The physical Jesus, who suffers and dies (in some texts)



B. Docetism and Realism

	•	Valentinians rejected radical docetism (which denies Jesus’ real body) and affirmed a real incarnation, but they still distinguished between the Aeonic Christ and the human Jesus.



C. Redemption through Gnosis

	•	Salvation comes from receiving Gnosis—a direct experiential knowledge of divine origin.

	•	Christ awakens the pneumatic seed through teaching, symbols, and mysteries.

	•	The sacraments and allegorical readings play a role in transmitting Gnosis.



V. Sacraments and Initiation

While similar to orthodox Christians in outward form, Valentinian rituals had esoteric interpretations.



A. The Sacraments (Mysteria)

	1.	Baptism (Redemption/Apolytrosis)

	•	Seen as the release from the Demiurge’s realm.

	•	Accompanied by invocations of divine Aeons and archangelic powers.

	2.	Anointing (Chrism)

	•	Symbolic of divine sealing and transmission of the pneumatic spark.

	3.	Eucharist

	•	Understood as a sacrament of spiritual union, symbolizing the reunion of the Aeons.

	4.	Bridal Chamber (Nymphōn)

	•	A mystical rite representing the reunification of the soul (Sophia) with its divine counterpart.

	•	Possibly involved sacred sexuality, though more likely symbolic or visionary.



VI. Texts and Sources

Valentinian ideas survive through:

	•	Writings of Church Fathers:

	•	Irenaeus, *Against Heresies*, Book I.

	•	Tertullian, *Against the Valentinians*.

	•	Often polemical but preserve system details.

	•	Nag Hammadi Texts (late 4th century copies of earlier Valentinian works):

	•	*Gospel of Truth* (attributed to Valentinus)

	•	*Tripartite Tractate*

	•	*Gospel of Philip*

	•	*Interpretation of Knowledge*

	•	*Excerpts of Theodotus* (preserved by Clement of Alexandria)

VII. Schools and Variants within Valentinianism

A. Eastern Valentinians

	•	Closer to the Platonic and Hellenistic roots.

	•	More speculative cosmology.

	•	Teachers: Theodotus, Heracleon.



B. Western Valentinians

	•	Emphasized moral life and symbolic sacraments.

	•	More integrative with the Roman Church.

	•	Teachers: Ptolemy, Marcus, Florinus.



VIII. Valentinian Exegesis and Hermeneutics

Valentinians were masters of allegorical and typological reading, often giving esoteric interpretations of:

	•	Pauline epistles: seen as Gnostic texts in disguise.

	•	Old Testament: symbolic of Demiurge and ignorance.

	•	New Testament events: parables for inner transformation.



Examples:

	•	Garden of Eden: symbolic of the fall of Sophia.

	•	Cross of Christ: symbolizes the division between the psychic and pneumatic.



IX. Controversies and Suppression

	•	Irenaeus, Tertullian, Epiphanius, and other heresiologists targeted Valentinianism as heretical.

	•	Valentinian teachers were often excommunicated, but continued teaching in private circles.

	•	The rise of orthodoxy under Constantine and subsequent councils suppressed Valentinianism.



X. Legacy and Influence

A. Influence on Christian Mysticism

	•	Ideas about the bridal chamber, the inner spark, and union with the divine influenced later mystics.



B. Echoes in Later Traditions

	•	Manichaeism inherited much of Valentinian cosmology.

	•	Medieval Cathars may have preserved similar dualistic ideas.

	•	Modern Jungian psychology and esoteric Christianity sometimes draw upon Valentinian archetypes.
