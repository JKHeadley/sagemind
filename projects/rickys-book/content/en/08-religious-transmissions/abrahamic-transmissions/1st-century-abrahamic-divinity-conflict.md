# 1st Century Abrahamic Divinity Conflict

The 1st-century Judean conception of divinity, particularly in the context of the Pharisees, Sadducees, and other Jewish groups of the time, centered on the strict monotheism of God as one, indivisible God. This belief was foundational in Judaism, where God was seen as wholly distinct from creation, transcendent, and indivisible.

Early Christianity, however, introduced the idea of the *Trinity*—God existing as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—especially after the resurrection of Jesus Christ. This was in tension with the Jewish understanding of God, which did not accommodate any division or multiplicity in the divine essence. Jesus’ divinity was a controversial concept for many Jews because it seemed to challenge the strict monotheism they adhered to, a belief that God could not be human or divided.

Thus, the conflict revolves around the notion of *divine unity* versus *divine multiplicity*—with early Christianity diverging from the Jewish tradition by introducing the idea of a multi-personal God (Father, Son, Holy Spirit), and Islam maintaining the strict monotheism that was foundational to the 1st-century Jewish understanding of Yahweh.
