# Review of Islamic Transmission Given to Templars (Part VI: The Nizari Ismaili State)



1. Followers of Nizari Ismaili

The Nizari Ismailis, did not have their own separate Hadiths in the traditional sense as other Islamic sects do. However, as a branch of Ismaili Shia Islam, they developed their own unique interpretations of Islamic teachings, particularly around the Quran, Hadith, and the role of the Imam. While they acknowledged the importance of the core Hadith literature accepted by Sunni and Shia Muslims, the Nizari Ismailis had their own specific traditions and texts that were highly esoteric and centered on the teachings of their imams.

- Esoteric Interpretation: The Nizari Ismailis are known for their esoteric interpretation of Islam. This means that while they recognized the general Hadith corpus, their interpretation of these texts was often more symbolic and hidden, with deeper meanings meant to be understood only by initiated members of their community, particularly by the Imam.
- The Role of the Imam: For the Nizari Ismailis, the Imam (leader of the community) was seen as the ultimate interpreter of the Quran and Hadith. They believed that the Imam possessed divine knowledge (known as ilm al-ladunni) that allowed him to provide deeper, esoteric meanings of the scriptures and guide the community. As a result, the teachings and interpretations of the Imam held greater authority than traditional Hadith collections.
- The “Hadith of the Imam”: While the Nizari Ismailis did not have their own independent Hadiths, they did have sayings and traditions associated with their own line of Imams, which were treated with similar reverence to Hadiths. These were often collected in specific texts that were central to the Ismaili tradition, such as the Al-Mu’ayyad Fi’l-Din al-Shirazi’s works, and were regarded as critical for understanding the faith.
- Ismaili Literature: The Ismailis produced a rich body of literature, including Tafsirs (Quranic commentaries), philosophical works, and du’as (prayers), many of which included interpretations of Hadith and Islamic teachings specific to their beliefs. Some of these works may have referenced Hadiths or included new interpretations attributed to the Imams, but they did not constitute a separate Hadith corpus.
- Secrecy and Initiation: The teachings of the Nizari Ismailis were often kept secret from outsiders, and much of their religious doctrine was transmitted orally or through special texts known only to initiated members. This esoteric approach to Islamic teachings made their religious practices and texts distinctive compared to the broader Sunni and Shia communities. 



2. Bātin 

The Nizari Ismailis, particularly those based in Alamut, were well-versed in a range of esoteric sciences, including alchemy, astrology, philology, and mythology—especially drawing from Persian, Egyptian, and Hellenistic traditions. This knowledge was deeply integrated into their esoteric (bātin) teachings and philosophical worldview:

- Alchemy

- The Nizaris had access to the philosophical and practical alchemical traditions inherited from earlier Ismaili, Hermetic, and Persian sources.
- Jabir ibn Hayyan (Geber), often linked to Ismaili thought, wrote extensively on alchemy and spiritual transformation, influencing later Nizari teachings.
- Alchemical symbolism was often spiritualized, with transmutation being a metaphor for inner enlightenment rather than just material change.

- Astrology

- The Ismailis, including the Nizaris, studied and practiced astrology (ilm al-nujum) as part of their cosmology.
- They inherited Sasanian Persian astrological traditions, which blended with Greek (Hellenistic) and Indian influences.
- The cycles of the stars were often linked to political and religious shifts, with astrological knowledge used for timing events and esoteric interpretations of history.

- Philology, Linguistics and Onomastics

- The Nizaris were known for their deep engagement with linguistics and philology, particularly in Arabic and Persian.
- Ismaili philosophy placed great importance on the hidden meanings of words and names (ta’wil), emphasizing symbolic interpretation.
- Many Nizari scholars were involved in the preservation, commentary, and evolution of Persian and Arabic literary traditions.

-  Egyptian and Persian Mythology

- The Fatimid roots of the Ismailis meant they inherited Egyptian esoteric traditions, particularly those related to Hermeticism, Neoplatonism, and Gnostic influences.
- Persian mythology, especially from Zoroastrianism and Sasanian royal ideology, was deeply embedded in Nizari thought.

	•	Mithraic themes: The idea of hidden knowledge, divine saviors, and cosmic struggle.

	•	Zurvanism: Cyclical time, destiny, and cosmic duality played roles in their esoteric teachings.

Some scholars argue that elements of ancient Egyptian priestly knowledge were preserved and adapted by the Ismailis during the Fatimid period.



2. Fedayeen

The Nizari Ismailis were not just philosophers and mystics—they were also elite warriors, expert spies, and master strategists. Their ability to wage asymmetric warfare, defend fortresses, and use psychological tactics allowed them to survive for nearly two centuries against overwhelming odds.

-  Unconventional Warfare & Espionage

The Nizaris developed advanced asymmetric warfare tactics, allowing them to survive against powerful adversaries:

- Targeted Assassinations:

- They eliminated key political and military figures using highly trained fida’is (self-sacrificing warriors).
- They specialized in striking where it would cause maximum disruption—such as during public gatherings or in fortresses.

- Psychological Warfare:

- The mere threat of assassination instilled fear in rulers. Some, like Saladin, had to take extreme precautions to avoid assassination.
- Nizaris would sometimes leave daggers and warning notes in a ruler’s bedchamber, showing they could strike at will.

- Infiltration & Intelligence Gathering:

- They planted agents within enemy courts and administrations, giving them vital information about enemy movements.
- Some of their spies posed as servants, scholars, or even religious figures to gain access to power circles.

- Equestrianism & Combat Training

The Nizaris were elite horsemen, drawing from Persian, Arab, and even Central Asian riding techniques:

- Mounted Guerrilla Warfare:

- Nizaris used hit-and-run tactics, harassing larger armies and retreating into mountainous strongholds.
- They relied on fast, agile horses, likely influenced by Persian cavalry traditions.

- Close-Quarters Combat:

- Their fida’is were trained in dagger combat, swordsmanship, and unarmed techniques for assassinations.
- They used small, curved daggers (khanjars) and were skilled in ambush tactics.

-  Fortress Defense & Strategic Warfare

The Nizaris were masters of fortified warfare, turning mountainous castles into nearly impregnable strongholds:

- Mountain Fortresses:

- Alamut and other Nizari castles were built atop steep cliffs with limited access points, making sieges difficult.
- Their fortresses had self-sustaining food and water supplies, allowing them to withstand long sieges.

- Defensive Warfare:

- They used boiling oil, rockslides, and archers to repel enemy forces.
- They had hidden escape tunnels for strategic retreats.

The Nizaris’ combination of espionage, assassination, and fortress warfare may have influenced the Knights Templar and other Crusader groups. Their use of psychological and asymmetric warfare had parallels with later guerrilla movements and found in modern day espionage.



3. Hassan II’s Qiyāmat Doctrine (1164 CE)

On Ramadan 8 August 1164 CE, Hassan II the successor of Muhammad ibn Buzurg-Ummid, who ruled the Nizari Ismailis in Persia, gathered his followers at Alamut and publicly declared the “Great Resurrection” (Qiyāma al-Qiyāma). He proclaimed that the Hidden Imam had spiritually reappeared and that the followers of Alamut were now living in an age of salvation. With this declaration, he abolished the outward practice of Sharia (Islamic law) for his followers, claiming they were now spiritually liberated. This meant that Ismailis no longer needed to observe prayer (salat), fasting (sawm), pilgrimage (hajj), and other obligations because they were spiritually enlightened. To mark this event, a banquet was held, where followers drank wine (forbidden in Islam) during Ramadan, and turned their backs to Medina, symbolizing their break from mainstream Islamic practice. Punishment for practicing traditional Islamic rituals: From that point onward, among Hassan II’s followers at Alamut, continuing to observe traditional Islamic rites such as fasting or daily prayers was punishable by death.



4. Rashid al-Din Sinan’s Rule in Syria (~1166–1193 CE)

The extent to which Rashid al-Din Sinan (the infamous “Old Man of the Mountain”) fully subscribed to Hassan II’s Qiyāmat doctrine is debated, but evidence suggests that he modified or downplayed it rather than rejecting it outright. Sinan was sent from Alamut to lead the Syrian Nizari Ismailis shortly after Hassan II’s death (1166 CE). He ruled semi-independently and had to adapt his policies to the political and military realities of Syria, where he dealt with Sunni Ayyubids, Crusaders, and rival factions. Some sources suggest that Sinan accepted Qiyāmat but did not enforce it as radically as in Alamut. Since he operated in a more hostile Sunni environment, he likely avoided public proclamations that would provoke his enemies. He may have kept the doctrine for initiates, while allowing outward Islamic practices to continue for political survival. Sunni chroniclers like Ibn al-Athir and Juvayni depict Sinan as an extreme heretic, which could indicate he held to Qiyāmat in some form. However, later Nizari traditions tend to emphasize Sinan’s independence from Alamut, suggesting he may have adapted or softened Hassan II’s doctrine.



5. Knights Templar Interaction

The Nizari Ismailis (Order of Assassins) and the Knights Templar were both active in the Levant during the Crusades, particularly in the 12th and early 13th centuries. While they were adversaries at times, there are accounts suggesting occasional diplomatic interactions and pragmatic alliances, especially against common enemies. The timeline of Nizari-Templar Interactions is as follows:

1.	Early Conflicts (Late 1100s - Early 1200s)

- The Nizaris in Syria, centered in Masyaf, frequently clashed with Crusader forces, including the Templars.
- However, they also made temporary truces with Crusader groups when it suited their interests.

2.	1180s: Truce Between Nizaris and the Crusaders

- The Nizaris of Syria, under Rashid al-Din Sinan (the “Old Man of the Mountain”), made a peace treaty with the Crusaders, facilitated by Raynald of Châtillon.
- This truce put the Nizaris in a position where they could indirectly cooperate with the Templars in specific situations.

3.	1192: The Assassination of Conrad of Montferrat

- The Nizaris assassinated Conrad of Montferrat, a key Crusader leader, just before he was to become the King of Jerusalem.
- Some sources suggest that Richard the Lionheart may have negotiated with the Nizaris for this assassination.
- The Templars were closely involved in these political dealings, though no direct evidence links them to the assassination.

4.	1210s–1220s: Tribute Payments to the Assassins

- The Templars and Hospitallers collected tribute payments from the Nizaris of Syria, essentially offering them protection in exchange for money.
- This implies a working relationship, even though the groups were religiously and ideologically opposed.

- Hell and paradise are interpreted as states of being
- The “end of the world” is the unveiling of divine truth
- History moves in cycles and each era has an imam who reveals deeper spiritual truths.
