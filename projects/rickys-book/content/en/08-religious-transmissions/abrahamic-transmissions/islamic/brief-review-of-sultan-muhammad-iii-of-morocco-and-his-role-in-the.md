# Brief Review of Sultan Muhammad III of Morocco and his Role in the Formation of  America



Sultan Muhammad III was not merely the first monarch to recognize the United States. He was a bridge between the Islamic Sufi world and the Enlightenment-Masonic world, operating within esoteric currents that viewed Morocco (Al-Maghrib) as a spiritual West, and America as its mirror in the physical West. This connection was not coincidental—it was initiatory, architectural, symbolic, and deeply encoded.



I. Prophetic Lineage and Divine Right

1. Sharifian Identity

	•	Muhammad III belonged to the ’Alawi dynasty, which claimed direct descent from the Prophet Muhammad through his grandson Hasan ibn Ali.

	•	In traditional Islamic governance, this descent was not merely political—it granted baraka (spiritual grace) and made him a guardian of the Muhammadan Light (*Nur Muhammad*), believed to be passed down through bloodlines and spiritual authority.



2. Sharifism and Legitimacy

	•	In Moroccan society, Sharifian descent provided legitimacy both politically and mystically:

	•	It linked Muhammad III with the hidden chain of awliya (saints) and the Qutb (spiritual pole) of the age.

	•	His actions carried not only worldly significance, but esoteric weight—as one who acts with the mantle of Prophethood in the temporal world.



3. Morocco as “Al-Maghrib al-Aqṣā” – The Furthest West

	•	In Islamic cosmology, al-Maghrib (the West) was always symbolic of mystery, sunset, and hidden realities.

	•	In Sufi thought, especially among North African initiates, “the West” is where light disappears to be reborn.

	•	This made Morocco a kind of sacred terminal—a land where the sun of ancient wisdom set, awaiting rebirth in a new land.



II. “America” as a Masonic Alteration of Al-Marrākush

1. Masonic Etymology (Symbolic, not Philological)

	•	Esoteric Masonic circles in the 18th century often used symbolic etymology, not linguistic etymology.

	•	They believed “America” was a cipher—a symbolic rebirth of “Al-Marrākush” (Marrakesh), representing:

	•	A land of initiation

	•	A new Atlantis

	•	The manifest destiny of the Western light

	•	Marrakesh itself means “Land of God” in Berber (*amur n akuch*), which Masonic mystics reinterpreted as “One Nation Under God”

2. Marrakesh and Masonry

	•	Marrakesh’s layout, with its concentric walls, sacred geometry, and hidden gardens, was seen as a model for sacred urban planning, much like Washington, D.C.

	•	The red city was considered a mirror to the rising White city—the future capital of a new spiritual empire (America).



III. The White House and “Al-Dar Al-Bayda” (Casablanca)

1. Symbolic Name Parallels

	•	White House (Dar al-Bayda) and Casablanca (also “Dar al-Bayda”) share the exact meaning: The White House / White Fortress.

	•	Some esotericists argue this is not a coincidence but a mirrored toponymic structure:

	•	Morocco = old sacred land

	•	America = new sacred land

	•	Just as Casablanca was the Western façade of the Maghreb, the White House became the architectural core of the new republic.



2. Architectural Parallels

	•	Both the White House and Moroccan palaces used neoclassical and Islamic geometry:

	•	Courtyard-focused design

	•	Axial symmetry

	•	Solar alignment (the White House sits on the Masonic axis of Washington D.C.)

	•	L’Enfant’s plan for Washington D.C. was influenced by Masonic and Islamic architectural canons—many of which filtered through Moorish Spain and Maghrebian models.



IV. Muhammad III’s Strategic Recognition of America (1777)

1. A Break from the Old World

	•	On December 20, 1777, Muhammad III opened Moroccan ports to American ships—before any European power.

	•	This wasn’t merely diplomacy. It was a ritual gesture:

	•	A Muslim sultan recognizes a new order, based not on monarchy but a republic of initiates (many Founding Fathers were Freemasons).

	•	It signified transference of the “light of the West” from the Islamic Maghreb to the New World.



2. The 1786 Treaty of Peace and Friendship

	•	Still in effect, the treaty was more than legal:

	•	It’s structured like a fraternal pact, emphasizing honor, protection, and symbolic brotherhood.

	•	The language echoes Masonic ideals: mutual respect, peace under divine sovereignty, order without tyranny.
