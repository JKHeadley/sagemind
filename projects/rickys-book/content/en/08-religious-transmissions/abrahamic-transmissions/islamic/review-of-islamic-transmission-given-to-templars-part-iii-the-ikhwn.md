# Review of Islamic Transmission Given to Templars (Part III:# The Ikhwān al-Ṣafā’)


The Ikhwān al-Ṣafā’ (Arabic: إخوان الصفا, *The Brethren of Purity*) were a mysterious and influential secretive philosophical society that emerged during the 10th century CE in Basra, Iraq, then part of the Abbasid Caliphate. Their main contribution to Islamic intellectual history was the encyclopedic work known as the Rasāʾil Ikhwān al-Ṣafā’ (Epistles of the Brethren of Purity) — a collection of 52 epistles (plus a 53rd summary treatise) covering a vast range of subjects: philosophy, science, metaphysics, religion, mathematics, music, astrology, and ethics.



I. ORIGINS AND IDENTITY

Who were they?

	•	The precise identity of the Brethren is deliberately obscured. Scholars suggest they were part of a clandestine Isma’ili Shi’a network, possibly linked to pre-Fatimid Daʿwa activities.

	•	They operated in a time of political fragmentation and sectarian conflict, seeking to revive a rational and spiritual synthesis.

	•	Their writings emphasize anonymity, cooperation, and the idea that truth should be pursued collectively.



Key historical context:

	•	10th-century Iraq: A vibrant intellectual environment influenced by Greek philosophy, Persian wisdom traditions, Hellenistic science, and Islamic theology.

	•	Sectarian tensions between Sunni, Twelver Shi’a, and Isma’ili groups.

	•	Flourishing Neoplatonism and Aristotelianism in Muslim philosophical circles.



II. THE STRUCTURE OF THE RASĀʾIL

The epistles are organized into four thematic divisions, showing a progressive ascent from physical sciences to metaphysics and theology.



1. Mathematical Sciences (14 epistles)

	•	Arithmetic, geometry, astronomy, music, logic.

	•	Aim: to ground the student in rational disciplines that train the intellect.

2. Natural Sciences (17 epistles)

	•	Physics, meteorology, mineralogy, botany, zoology, medicine, embryology.

	•	Heavily influenced by Aristotelian physics and Galenic medicine, integrated with Neoplatonic cosmology.



3. Psychological and Rational Sciences (10 epistles)

	•	Soul, intellect, the hierarchy of beings, the nature of man, resurrection, dream interpretation.

	•	Strong Neoplatonic influence: the soul’s descent and return to the One.



4. Theological and Mystical Sciences (11 epistles)

	•	Cosmology, spiritual purification, divine unity (*tawḥīd*), prophecy, eschatology, angelology, religious symbolism.

5. The 53rd Epistle – al-Risāla al-Jāmiʿa (The Comprehensive Treatise)

	•	Summarizes the content of the preceding epistles and presents an overarching philosophical and esoteric system.

III. CENTRAL THEMES AND TEACHINGS

1. Philosophical Syncretism

	•	Blended ideas from Plato, Aristotle, Plotinus (Neoplatonism), Pythagoras, Zoroastrianism, Hindu metaphysics, Hermeticism, and Islamic mysticism.

	•	Emphasized universal wisdom (ḥikma) across all cultures and epochs.



2. The Chain of Being

	•	Cosmic hierarchy: God → Intellect → Soul → Nature → Matter.

	•	Human beings are microcosms (ʿ*ālam ṣaghīr*) that reflect the macrocosm (ʿ*ālam kabīr*).



3. Theory of the Soul

	•	Soul is immortal, pre-exists the body, and aims to return to the divine realm through purification.

	•	Emphasized ethical behavior, spiritual knowledge, and ascetic practices.



4. Religious Universalism

	•	Advocated religious tolerance and the unity of all revealed religions.

	•	Viewed prophets as wise philosophers who adapted divine truths to the needs of their communities.

	•	Islam was seen as the culmination of previous truths.



5. Symbolism and Allegory

	•	Preferred allegorical interpretations of scripture.

	•	Many epistles contain fables and animal parables, such as the famous *Epistle of the Animals* (*Risālat al-Ḥayawān*) which critiques human arrogance.



6. Initiation and Esotericism

	•	Knowledge should be progressively revealed to worthy individuals.

	•	Advocated a kind of philosophical initiation and inner circle of purified souls.



IV. GOALS AND MISSION

The Ikhwān al-Ṣafā’ aimed to:

	•	Promote a harmonious synthesis of philosophy, religion, and science.

	•	Advance the spiritual refinement of individuals.

	•	Prepare the ground for a just and enlightened society, possibly anticipating a messianic Imam.

	•	Undermine dogmatic and literalist interpretations of religion.

	•	Cultivate fraternity (ukhuwwa) and collective pursuit of truth.



V. DOCTRINAL AND IDEOLOGICAL AFFILIATIONS

Connection to Isma’ili Shi’ism

	•	Many scholars argue the Ikhwān were proto-Isma’ilis or affiliated with the Fatimid daʿwa.

	•	Shared beliefs:

	•	Emphasis on taʾwīl (esoteric interpretation).

	•	Cyclical view of history with seven prophetic cycles culminating in a Qāʾim (messianic savior).

	•	Belief in the Imam as the true teacher who harmonizes reason and revelation.



Neoplatonism

	•	Structure of reality as a emanation from the One.

	•	Return of the soul to its divine source.

	•	Use of Platonic and Plotinian terminology to frame Islamic theological ideas.



VI. LEGACY AND INFLUENCE

1. Intellectual Influence

	•	Cited or absorbed by later Isma’ili thinkers, Sufis, Philosophers (faylasūf) like al-Fārābī, Avicenna, and even al-Ghazālī (who was critical but influenced).

	•	Anticipates later mystical syntheses in Sufism, Illuminationist (Ishrāqī) philosophy, and Western esotericism.



2. Cultural Impact

	•	Translated into Persian, Hebrew, and Latin, especially during the Andalusian and Fatimid periods.

	•	Influenced Jewish philosophers like Bahya ibn Paquda and Maimonides (indirectly).

	•	Key transmission point of Greek science and philosophy into Islamic and European thought.



3. Later Revivals

	•	Popular among esoteric Shi’a groups (including the Druze and Nusayris).

	•	In modern times, they’ve inspired Islamic modernists, perennialists, and comparative religion scholars.



VII. TEXTUAL TRANSMISSION AND MANUSCRIPTS

	•	The Rasāʾil have been preserved in various manuscript traditions.

	•	Arabic critical editions were produced by Fuat Sezgin, and more recently by the Institute of Ismaili Studies (IIS).

	•	Translations exist in English, French, Persian, and Urdu, with IIS working on annotated editions.



VIII. SELECT EPISODES AND SYMBOLS

	•	Epistle 22: The Debate of Animals vs. Humans: An allegorical trial where animals argue that they are morally superior to humans. It’s a critique of human vice and ecological arrogance.

	•	Cosmic Music: Musical theory is not just art but a reflection of the harmony of the spheres.

	•	Alchemy and Symbolic Sciences: Seen as metaphors for inner transformation, not just material processes.
