# Review of Islamic Transmission Given to Templars (Part V:# Ḥikmat al-Ishrāq)

The Illuminationist School of Suhrawardī (Ḥikmat al-Ishrāq)

Founder: Shihāb al-Dīn Yaḥyā Suhrawardī (d. 1191) – “The Murdered Suhrawardī”

	•	Born in Suhraward, Iran. Studied Avicennan philosophy, but eventually critiqued it.

	•	Executed in Aleppo, possibly at the instigation of Sunni jurists, for his esoteric teachings and perceived heresy.

⸻

Core Doctrines of Illuminationism

1. Light Metaphysics (Nūrī Ontology):

	•	Everything in existence is a gradation of light, emanating from al-Nūr al-Aṣfā (The Pure Light).

	•	All beings are either:

	•	Self-subsistent lights (e.g., angels, intellects),

	•	Or darknesses (bodies, material entities) that receive light.



2. Hierarchy of Lights:

	•	Similar to Neoplatonic emanation: divine light flows downward in stages.

	•	Inspired by Zoroastrian angelology, Platonism, Hermetism, and Avicennism.



3. Philosophical Illumination (Ishrāq):

	•	Knowledge is not acquired, but *illuminated* into the soul.

	•	Replaces logic as the highest tool of knowledge with direct intuitive knowledge (dhawq or shuhūd).



4. Angelic Cosmology:

	•	Angels are real metaphysical beings who mediate divine light.

	•	Soul ascends through levels of light toward the Source.

	•	Each individual has a celestial twin—a Platonic concept reworked in Islamic metaphysics.



5. Integration of Philosophy and Esotericism:

	•	Combines:

	•	Avicenna’s rationalist metaphysics,

	•	Plato’s doctrine of Forms,

	•	Zoroastrian dualism,

	•	Islamic mysticism (Ishraqis would influence theosophical Sufis like Ibn ʿArabī).

⸻

Major Works of Yaḥyā Suhrawardī

	•	Ḥikmat al-Ishrāq (The Philosophy of Illumination) – his magnum opus.

	•	Talwīḥāt (“Intimations”) and Maqāmāt al-Ṭuyūr (“Stations of the Birds”) – mystical allegories.

	•	Deeply symbolic, often blending philosophical reasoning with mystical storytelling.



⸻

Influence of Illuminationism

	•	Influenced later Persian philosophy, especially through:

	•	Mullā Ṣadrā (Transcendent Theosophy),

	•	Isfahan School,

	•	Iranian Shiʿi metaphysics.

	•	Integrated into Shiʿi gnosis, sometimes aligning with Imamology.

	•	In many ways, a bridge between Sufism, Hermeticism, and Islamic philosophy.
