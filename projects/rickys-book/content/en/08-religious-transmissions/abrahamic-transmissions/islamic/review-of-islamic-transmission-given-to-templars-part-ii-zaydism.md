1. Musnad al-Imam Zayd

Traditionally attributed to Zayd ibn Ali (d. 740 CE), the founder of the Zaydi movement. However, some scholars argue that the actual compilation was done later by Kufan scholars, particularly those influenced by the Hanafi school.



The collection is divided into different chapters (abwāb) covering a wide range of Islamic legal and theological topics, including:


- Fiqh (Islamic jurisprudence): Ritual purity, prayer, fasting, zakat, hajj, marriage, divorce, contracts, and inheritance.
- Ethics & morality: The virtues of honesty, patience, humility, and justice.
- Political and social governance: The role of the Imam as a just ruler, the obligations of rulers toward their people, and the legitimacy of rebellion against tyrannical rule.
- Theology (Kalam): The oneness of God (*tawhid*), divine justice, and free will.

Significance:

Highly revered in Zaydi jurisprudence as one of the earliest records of legal traditions followed by the Imams of Ahl al-Bayt. Shows strong Kufan and Hanafi influences, suggesting that Zayd’s followers were engaged with the broader legal debates of their time. Differs from Twelver and Sunni hadith collections in its approach to Imamate and leadership. It is considered more of a *Fiqh-oriented* hadith collection rather than a pure narration-based hadith compilation like Sunni *Sahih* works.

2. Amaali Ahmad ibn Isa

A collection of teachings (dictated lectures) given by Ahmad ibn Isa ibn Zayd (d. 849 CE), a grandson of Zayd ibn Ali. The work was compiled by Muhammad ibn Mansur al-Muradi, an early Zaydi scholar.



The book consists of *Amaali* (oral dictations), which were a common form of knowledge transmission in early Islam covering hadith, legal discussions, and theological debates. Main topics include:

- Hadith transmission and verification: Criteria for authenticating narrations.
- Fiqh (Islamic Law): Legal discussions on purity, prayer, fasting, and social contracts.
- Theological disputes: Arguments against fatalism (*Jabriyya*) and anthropomorphism in Islamic theology.
- Role of the Ahl al-Bayt: The necessity of following the Imams of the Prophet’s family for proper religious guidance.
- Legal reasoning: Ahmad ibn Isa’s independent reasoning (*ijtihad*) on various issues.

Significance:

Provides a direct link between the early Kufan Zaydi tradition and later Yemeni Zaydi thought. Emphasizes a *rationalist approach* to theology, rejecting deterministic and fatalistic views. Acts as a bridge between early Zaydi traditions and later legal developments.

Imamate under Zaydism

- Descent from the Ahl Al-Bayt
- Justice - high level of personal integrity, piety and moral uprightness. Not engage in sinful behavior or injustice.
- Knowledge - extensive knowledge of Islamic jurisprudence, theology, and the Qur’an
- Courage - Willing to take up arms to defend justice, oppose tyranny and uphold the principles of Islam.
- Capability - ability to govern effectively, administer justice and manage affairs
- Call to Arms - Must be an activist leader rather than a passive figure.
- Freedom from Physical Defects - no significant physical impairments or disabilities.
- Sound Reason - intellectual capacity to make informed and rational decisions.
- Adult Male - Must be a man of age reflecting societal norms.
- Willingness to Lead - must willingly and actively pursue the role of leadership.
- Opposition to Tyranny - Actively oppose unjust rulers and systems. A silent or complacent member cannot claim the Imamate.
- Support from the Community - Must gain sufficient support of the Muslim community particular ones willing to follow in resisting oppression.
- Knowledge of Warfare and Strategy - Must possess the ability to lead military campaigns, defend the community and implement strategies against unjust forces.
- Adherence to Islamic Principles - Adhere to the teachings of Islam, Upholding the Quran and Sunnah, and the values of justice, equality and righteousness.
