# Surah An-Nisa (4:157)



Surah An-Nisa (4:157) is a significant verse in Islamic theology, particularly regarding the crucifixion of Jesus (Isa, عليه السلام). Here is the verse in Arabic, followed by different translations and possible interpretations:

Arabic:

وَقَوْلِهِمْ إِنَّا قَتَلْنَا ٱلْمَسِيحَ عِيسَى ٱبْنَ مَرْيَمَ رَسُولَ ٱللَّهِ وَمَا قَتَلُوهُ وَمَا صَلَبُوهُ وَلَكِن شُبِّهَ لَهُمْ ۚ وَإِنَّ ٱلَّذِينَ ٱخْتَلَفُوا۟ فِيهِ لَفِى شَكٍّۢ مِّنْهُ ۚ مَا لَهُم بِهِۦ مِنْ عِلْمٍ إِلَّا ٱتِّبَاعَ ٱلظَّنِّ ۚ وَمَا قَتَلُوهُ يَقِينًۢا

Literal Translation:

*“And [for] their saying, ‘Indeed, we have killed the Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary, the Messenger of Allah.’ But they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him, but it was made to appear so to them. And indeed, those who differ over it are in doubt about it. They have no knowledge of it except the following of assumption. And they did not kill him, for certain.”*

Different Translations:

	1.	Sahih International:

*And [for] their saying, “Indeed, we have killed the Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary, the Messenger of Allah.” And they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but [another] was made to resemble him to them. And indeed, those who differ over it are in doubt about it. They have no knowledge of it except the following of assumption. And they did not kill him, for certain.*

	2.	Pickthall:

*And because of their saying: We slew the Messiah, Jesus son of Mary, Allah’s messenger—They slew him not nor crucified, but it appeared so unto them; and lo! those who disagree concerning it are in doubt thereof; they have no knowledge thereof save pursuit of a conjecture; they slew him not for certain.*

	3.	Yusuf Ali:

*That they said (in boast), “We killed Christ Jesus the son of Mary, the Messenger of Allah”;—but they killed him not, nor crucified him, but so it was made to appear to them, and those who differ therein are full of doubts, with no (certain) knowledge, but only conjecture to follow, for of a surety they killed him not.*

	4.	Shakir:

*And their saying: Surely we have killed the Messiah, Isa son of Maryam, the messenger of Allah; and they did not kill him nor did they crucify him, but it appeared to them so (like Isa); and most surely those who differ therein are only in a doubt about it; they have no knowledge respecting it, but only follow a conjecture, and they killed him not for sure.*

	5.	Muhammad Asad:

*And their boast, “Behold, we have slain the Christ Jesus, son of Mary, who claimed to be an apostle of God!” However, they did not slay him, and neither did they crucify him, but it only seemed so to them; and, verily, those who hold conflicting views thereon are indeed confused, having no real knowledge thereof, and following mere conjecture. For, of a certainty, they did not slay him.*

Possible Interpretations:

	1.	Jesus Was Not Crucified, But Someone Else Was Made to Resemble Him

	•	This is the traditional Islamic view held by most Sunni and Shia scholars. The phrase “shubbiha lahum” (شُبِّهَ لَهُمْ) means “it was made to appear so to them.” This suggests that another person was made to look like Jesus and was crucified in his place. Some theories suggest that this person was:

	•	Judas Iscariot (according to some later Islamic traditions)

	•	A volunteer or substitute

	•	A divine illusion placed on the crowd

	2.	The Crucifixion Happened, But Not to Jesus’ Essence (Docetism Influence?)

	•	Some interpret “they did not kill him” to mean that while Jesus’ physical body might have been crucified, his true essence (soul/spirit) was not affected. This has similarities with some early Christian sects like the Docetists, who believed that Jesus’ suffering was an illusion.

	3.	Metaphorical Interpretation: Jesus’ Enemies Failed to Kill His Mission

	•	Some scholars, particularly in modern times, argue that the verse does not mean Jesus was physically absent from the crucifixion, but rather that his enemies failed to truly “kill” him in a spiritual or theological sense. His message continued, and thus their victory was illusory.

	4.	The Event Was an Optical Illusion or Divine Deception

	•	Some interpretations suggest that Allah caused confusion among the people at the time, making it seem as though Jesus was crucified, when in reality he was taken up to heaven.

	5.	Jesus Survived the Crucifixion

	•	A minority of Muslim thinkers, influenced by the Ahmadiyya tradition, suggest that Jesus was indeed placed on the cross but did not die there. Instead, he was taken down and later migrated to another land (some claim Kashmir).

	6.	The Verse is a Direct Rejection of the Jewish Boast, Not a Detailed Explanation

	•	Some scholars argue that the verse is primarily aimed at rejecting the claim that the Jews were responsible for Jesus’ death. It does not necessarily describe what exactly happened, only that their claim is false.

Key Theological Points:

	•	Islam asserts that Jesus was not killed or crucified.

	•	Instead, Allah saved him and raised him up.

	•	The details of what exactly happened remain debated.

	•	The Quran challenges the certainty of those who claim knowledge about Jesus’ crucifixion, stating that they only follow conjecture.

1. Greek Vocabulary in Resurrection Passages

	•	ἐγείρω (egeirō):

Literally: *to wake up, to raise (someone or something), to awaken from sleep.*

Examples:

	•	Matthew 28:6 — *“He is not here; for he has been raised (ἠγέρθη – passive of egeirō), just as he said.”*

	•	1 Corinthians 15:4 — *“He was buried, and that he was raised (ἠγέρθη) on the third day…”*

	•	ἀνίστημι (anistēmi):

Means *to rise, to stand up, to be raised* — and also used for resurrection contexts.

	•	Acts 2:24 — *“God raised him up (ἀνέστησεν), loosing the pangs of death.”*

These verbs do not necessarily imply resurrection in the sense of dying and coming back to life. In classical and Koine Greek, they are used in everyday contexts, like someone getting out of bed, or standing up from sitting.



2. Metaphorical Use of Sleep and Waking Up

In the New Testament and broader Second Temple Jewish thought, death is often metaphorically called “sleep.” So when Jesus says, for example, in John 11:11:



*“Our friend Lazarus has fallen asleep; but I am going there to awaken (ἐξυπνίζω) him.”*

The disciples misunderstood him to mean literal sleep, but the text clarifies Jesus was referring to death. This shows how the metaphor of sleep and waking was commonly used for death and resurrection.



3. Implications for Interpretation

The point about the verb potentially meaning “waking” opens up alternate interpretations, particularly among:

	•	Mythicists or metaphorical interpreters who argue that Jesus’ “resurrection” was not a physical rising from death but a spiritual awakening or visionary experience.

	•	Some Islamic thinkers (and Ahmadiyya Muslims in particular) who suggest that Jesus never actually died on the cross, but rather lost consciousness and later recovered—pointing to such Greek nuances to support a “swoon theory.”

	•	Modern critical scholars who highlight that none of the earliest resurrection accounts describe anyone actually witnessing the moment Jesus came back to life. The discovery of an empty tomb and post-resurrection appearances are described—but the act of resurrection itself is assumed.

	•	Romans 6:4 — *“…as Christ was raised from the dead… so we too might walk in newness of life.”*

	•	Usage: new spiritual life

So, “شُبِّهَ لَهُمْ” = It was made to resemble (something) to them, or It was made to appear (as such) to them.

3. Linguistic Ambiguity

This phrase is intentionally vague — it does not specify:

	•	What was made to appear?

	•	To whom exactly did it appear?

	•	By whom was this done?

	•	Was it a miraculous illusion, a case of mistaken identity, or a divine substitution?



4. Major Interpretative Theories (Classical & Modern)

A. Substitution Theory (Classical Sunni and Shia)

	•	Someone else was made to look like Jesus and was crucified in his place (e.g., Judas Iscariot, Simon of Cyrene, or a volunteer).

	•	Supported by early Tafsir (e.g., al-Tabari, Ibn Kathir).

	•	Jesus was raised alive to heaven.



Philosophically: This implies divine intervention into empirical perception — a miraculous, deliberate illusion — possibly testing the crowd.



B. Docetic Interpretation (Gnostic Influence)

	•	Some early Gnostic Christians (e.g., Basilides) believed that Christ only seemed to suffer and die — his divine nature never touched death.

	•	The Quran’s wording matches this eerily well.



This aligns with Docetism, where Christ’s body was a phantom and never truly crucified.



C. Swoon Theory (Ahmadiyya and some rationalists)

	•	Jesus did not die on the cross but lost consciousness and later recovered.

	•	“It appeared he died” — but he was only near death or comatose.



Here, “shubbiha lahum” = their misinterpretation of events — no illusion, just mistaken belief.



D. Vision Theory (Philosophical/Sufi/Mu’tazilite)

	•	The entire crucifixion event was a collective vision or projection, not literal history.

	•	Truth lies in spiritual perception, not material appearances.



“Shubbiha lahum” means the sensory world is deceptive — aligning with Neoplatonic and Sufi metaphysics (e.g., Ibn Arabi).

1. Jesus in Fatimid Ismaili Thought

Jesus (ʿĪsā) is revered as a nātiq (speaking prophet) in the Ismaili cosmological cycle of prophecy. According to classical Ismaili doctrine:

	•	Each prophetic cycle has:

	•	A nātiq (speaker-prophet) who brings the ẓāhir (outer law),

	•	A wāṣī (legatee) who guards the bāṭin (inner meaning),

	•	A set of Imams who continue the esoteric mission,

	•	A qāʾim (resurrector) who reveals the full truth at the end of the cycle.



In this scheme:

	•	Jesus is the fifth nātiq.

	•	His wāṣī is traditionally understood to be Simon Peter or an esoteric counterpart.

	•	His sharīʿa (outer law) is fulfilled in spirit by Muhammad, the final nātiq.



Key Point: Jesus is not defined by historical crucifixion, but by his place in a metaphysical cycle and his transmission of divine wisdom.

2. al-Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān on Crucifixion (Implied and Symbolic)

While al-Qāḍī al-Nuʿmān doesn’t dwell at length on the crucifixion as a historical event, his epistemological and ta’wīl method gives us a clear way to interpret verses like 4:157.



In his major works like “Asās al-Ta’wīl” and “Daʿāʾim al-Islām”, he emphasizes:

	•	Ẓāhir (outer) events are veils for bāṭin (inner) truths.

	•	The death of a prophet never implies the death of his nūr (divine light).

	•	Persecuting a prophet or attempting to kill him often symbolizes the rejection of the inner truth, but that truth survives in the Imam.



So the crucifixion (in ta’wīl terms) symbolizes:

	•	The attempt of ẓāhirī authorities to destroy the true haqīqa (spiritual truth),

	•	But they fail, because the spiritual reality lives on through the Imams or inner community.



3. Imamology and Crucifixion

In Ismaili theology:

	•	The Imam is the bearer of the divine light (nūr al-imāmah).

	•	Just as Jesus could not be truly crucified, the Imam cannot be spiritually killed, even if physically martyred or hidden.



Thus:

	•	Crucifixion is not a unique event for Jesus, but a repeating pattern:

	•	Truth is veiled under worldly appearances,

	•	The apparent bearer is attacked,

	•	The divine light continues — hidden or transferred.



This aligns with Fatimid doctrine where truth is cyclical and perennial, not interrupted by political or material setbacks.



4. Esoteric Understanding of “Shubbiha Lahum”

The Fatimid exegetes would interpret the phrase “shubbiha lahum” as follows:



“They believed they had destroyed the truth (by crucifying Jesus), but in reality, they only saw what was *made to appear* — a veil that concealed the continuity of divine guidance.”

Parallels in Fatimid Ta’wīl:

	•	Pharaoh’s magicians (Qur’an 20:66) — illusion vs. true power of Moses.

	•	The apparent concealment of the Imam during times of persecution — misleads oppressors.

	•	Outward religious authorities thinking they uphold divine law, when they kill its spiritual carriers.

The official Nizari Ismaili interpretation of Surah An-Nisa 4:157 reflects their esoteric (bāṭinī) hermeneutic approach — where verses are not taken only at their outward, literal (ẓāhir) meaning but understood through a deeper symbolic or metaphysical lens.



1. Core Principles of Nizari Interpretation

	•	Imam-centered epistemology: True interpretation of the Qur’an comes from the guidance of the living Imam, who holds the esoteric knowledge (ta’wīl).

	•	Emphasis on spiritual reality over historical literalism: The Nizari tradition generally does not insist on literal-historical readings of prophetic events if a higher truth is being conveyed.

	•	Avoidance of theological conflict with reason and science: The Nizaris often interpret miraculous or controversial verses symbolically to avoid conflict with rational thought and modern understandings.



2. On Surah An-Nisa 4:157

The verse:

“And [for] their saying, ‘Indeed, we have killed the Messiah, Jesus, the son of Mary, the messenger of Allah.’ And they did not kill him, nor did they crucify him; but it was made to appear so to them…”

Nizari Interpretation:

	1.	Jesus did not truly die in the spiritual sense.

	•	His divine light (nūr) and spiritual mission continued, regardless of what occurred to his body.

	•	Even if he was crucified physically, that is not “death” in the Qur’anic sense, which often denotes the extinguishing of truth or divine light.

	2.	“It appeared so to them” = Misunderstanding of the nature of Christ.

	•	The crucifixion was a test of perception and spiritual insight.

	•	Those who judged Jesus as dead or defeated were caught in ẓāhir, the outer form.

	•	The spiritually aware understood that the truth of Jesus (the logos/nūr) was untouched.

	3.	No focus on substitution theory.

	•	Nizaris generally do not support the classical Sunni idea that someone else was made to look like Jesus.

	•	This is seen as a surface-level solution to a deeper theological question.

	4.	Jesus as a prototype of the Imam.

	•	Like Jesus, the Imam cannot be truly killed, because he is the bearer of divine knowledge and light (nūr al-imāmah).

	•	Even in times of concealment or martyrdom (e.g., Imams who were assassinated), the light continues through the next Imam.



Thus, the crucifixion story becomes a symbolic drama of truth versus illusion, much like in Nizari interpretations of Husayn’s martyrdom or the concealment of the Imam.

	•	What matters is the continuity of the haqīqa (truth), not the shell.

	•	Jesus’ “non-death” is a statement about the inviolability of divine wisdom, even when it appears outwardly defeated.
