# Brief Review of African Spirituality



I. RELIGIOUS PRACTICE IN AFRICA

A. Traditional African Religions (TARs)

1. Core Characteristics

	•	Polytheistic but hierarchical: Most systems have a Supreme Being (e.g., Olódùmarè, Nyame, Amma) and intermediary spirits or deities.

	•	Ancestors as intermediaries: Ancestor veneration is crucial; the dead are seen as guides and protectors.

	•	Ritual centrality: Life cycles (birth, initiation, marriage, death), farming seasons, war, and illness are ritually mediated.

	•	Orality: Myths, ethical codes, and cosmologies are transmitted orally through griots, priests, elders, or masquerade performances.

	•	Shrines and sacred spaces: Natural elements (trees, rivers, rocks) are often loci of divine presence.



2. Major Traditional Systems by Region

	•	West Africa: Yoruba (Ifá), Akan (Abosom), Igbo (Odinani), Fon-Ewe (Vodun), Dogon (Amma)

	•	Central Africa: Kongo (Bakongo cosmogram, nkisi), Luba, Fang-Beti

	•	Southern Africa: Zulu (Unkulunkulu), Sotho-Tswana, San spiritual practices

	•	Horn of Africa: Cushitic animisms, Oromo Waaqeffanna

	•	North Africa (pre-Islamic): Berber cults, Egyptian religion



B. Abrahamic Religions in Africa

1. Islam

	•	Introduced by trade and conquest: 7th–11th centuries CE, especially in North, Sahel, and East Africa.

	•	Sufi Orders: Tijaniyya, Qadiriyya, Muridiyya have localized Islam via mysticism, adapting it to local cosmologies.

	•	Islamic education: Qur’anic schools (madrasas), Ajami (African languages in Arabic script).

	•	Hybrid Islam: In many areas, Islamic practice is deeply fused with traditional cosmologies (e.g., maraboutism, spirit possession).



2. Christianity

	•	Oldest form: Coptic Christianity in Egypt (1st century) and Ethiopian Orthodox Church (4th century).

	•	Colonial spread: Missionary Christianity grew with colonialism (19th–20th centuries).

	•	Indigenous Churches: African Independent Churches (Zionists, Aladura, Kimbanguists) blend Christian dogma with African cosmology.



II. SPIRITUALITY IN AFRICA

A. Philosophy of Spirit (Vitalism)

	•	Life force: Known as *ase* (Yoruba), *nyama* (Mande), *nto* (Bantu) — spiritual energy that animates everything.

	•	Balance and reciprocity: Health and fortune depend on maintaining harmony between spiritual, natural, and social orders.

	•	Divination systems: Ifá (Yoruba), Hakata (Shona), Diloggún (Lucumí), Sand divination (Berber, Saharan groups).

	•	Dreams, possession, trance: Key methods for communication with spirits, ancestors, or deities.



III. SECRET SOCIETIES IN AFRICA (Primary Focus)

Secret societies in Africa function as religious, political, judicial, and initiation-based institutions that structure communal life. These are not “secret” in the modern Western sense but rather esoteric, initiatory, and hierarchical.



A. Functions of Secret Societies

	1.	Spiritual Authority

	•	Gatekeepers of sacred knowledge

	•	Conductors of initiation rites

	•	Mediators with the invisible world

	2.	Political Governance

	•	Many societies held real political power, regulating kingship, succession, and war.

	•	Kings were often subject to these societies (e.g., Ogboni in Yoruba land or Poro in Mande lands).

	3.	Judicial Enforcement

	•	Settled disputes, enforced taboos, issued punishments.

	•	Enforced through spiritual fear (e.g., “night masks” or masked spirits that acted as agents of the society).

	4.	Socialization & Education

	•	Youth initiation into adulthood.

	•	Moral codes, gender roles, survival skills, and cosmology were all taught in secret camps.

	5.	Resistance & Preservation

	•	During slavery and colonialism, these societies preserved African identity.

	•	Some even resisted colonial rule (e.g., Leopard societies in Congo, Mau Mau in Kenya).

B. Major Secret Societies by Region

1. West Africa

a. Poro and Sande (Mande-speaking groups: Sierra Leone, Liberia, Guinea)

	•	Poro: Male society, initiates boys into manhood, teaches cosmology, martial skills, and governance.

	•	Sande: Female society, focuses on fertility, marriage, womanhood, and female genital rites (controversial).

	•	Influence: Determines village leadership, manages forest lands, sanctions law.



b. Ogboni (Yoruba)

	•	Council of elder statesmen and priest-judges.

	•	Linked to Earth goddess (Onile) and ancestors.

	•	Act as spiritual check on kings (Obas).

	•	Used coded language, sacred symbols (Opa Ogboni), and rituals in iron.



c. Ekpe / Leopard Society (Efik, Ibibio, Igbo, Cameroon)

	•	Also known as Ngbe in Cross River region.

	•	Controlled trade routes and political decisions.

	•	Ekpe masquerade (Nsibidi writing used in communication).

	•	Important precursor to Cuban Abakuá society.



d. Atinga / Oro (Yoruba and Itsekiri)

	•	Oro is a powerful male society calling the dead and spirits through sound (bull-roarer).

	•	In charge of purification and punishment; feared for enforcing taboos.

e. Abakuá (Afro-Cuban continuation of Ekpe)

	•	Still active today in Cuba, especially in Havana and Matanzas.

	•	Highly hierarchical, initiatory, and full of encrypted liturgy and symbols.

2. Central Africa

a. Bwami Society (Lega people, DRC)

	•	Moral and philosophical society that uses masks and wooden figures to teach allegorical lessons.

	•	Highly stratified, with ascension based on wisdom and character.

	•	Artworks acted as mnemonic devices for moral education.



b. Nkisi Cults (Kongo)

	•	Sacred bundles used to trap spiritual forces (healing or punishing).

	•	Banganga (priests) were initiates into these societies.

	•	Later transformed into Nganga societies in diasporic religions (Palo, Vodou, Hoodoo).



c. Leopard Societies

	•	Involved in ritual executions and political enforcement.

	•	Used animal symbolism (leopard = power, invisibility).

	•	Blamed for some acts of resistance to colonialism.

3. Southern Africa

a. Basotho Initiation Schools (Lebollo)

	•	Organized by secret male societies to train boys in discipline, secrecy, and tribal identity.

	•	Includes circumcision and seclusion.

	•	Seen as essential for transitioning into adulthood and leadership.

b. Zulu Diviner Guilds (Sangoma networks)

	•	Not secret in the Western sense, but they require rigorous spiritual training and initiation.

	•	Inherited and passed down lineages of spirit possession and herbal knowledge.

4. North and Horn of Africa

	•	Less emphasis on formal secret societies due to early Islamic and Christian hegemony.

	•	However:

	•	Zār cults (Ethiopia, Sudan, Somalia): Spirit possession cults often led by women, with secret rituals and songs.

	•	Tuareg and Berber trance orders: Blend Islamic mysticism with pre-Islamic elements, sometimes only for initiates.



C. Symbols, Codes, and Aesthetics

	•	Nsibidi (Nigeria/Cameroon): Symbolic writing system used by Ekpe/Abakuá.

	•	Kongo Cosmogram (Yowa): Represents the four moments of the sun, reincarnation, and sacred order.

	•	Masquerades: Embodied spirits (e.g., Egungun, Mmo, Gule Wamkulu) that enact spiritual truths during public rituals.

	•	Totemic Animals: Leopards, snakes, crocodiles, birds often symbolize qualities of the society (power, cunning, silence, vision).



IV. INFLUENCE ON COMMUNAL LIFE

A. Law & Order

	•	Secret societies often act as unseen enforcers of moral codes. Their judgments carry more weight than civil law in some communities.

	•	Punishments can be spiritual, social (ostracism), or symbolic (masquerade visitations).

B. Initiation & Identity

	•	Belonging to a secret society can define one’s role in society — healer, warrior, judge, elder, or spiritual medium.

	•	Even modern politicians, police chiefs, and clergy in parts of Africa may retain affiliations with secret orders.

C. Cultural Memory & Resistance

	•	During slavery and colonization, these societies preserved endangered knowledge, language, and memory.

	•	In diaspora (Cuba, Brazil, Haiti, Suriname), they birthed systems like Palo, Abakuá, and Vodou.

D. Economics & Power

	•	In regions where state authority is weak, secret societies control land rights, water usage, and trade routes.

	•	Even today, in Sierra Leone or Liberia, the Poro society may determine electoral outcomes.

V. MODERN ADAPTATIONS & CONTROVERSIES

	•	Urbanization and secrecy: Societies have adapted to urban life and now meet secretly in cities.

	•	Political manipulation: Some groups are co-opted by politicians for fear or influence (e.g., “cultist” groups in Nigeria).

	•	Human rights concerns: Female genital mutilation and ritual killings have led to international outcry in cases of abuse by rogue societies.

	•	Diasporic revitalization: Interest in African secret societies is resurging among African diasporic communities, with a focus on spiritual reclamation and identity.
