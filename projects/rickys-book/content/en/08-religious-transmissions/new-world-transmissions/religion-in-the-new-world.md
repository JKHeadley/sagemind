# Religion in the New World



Religion in the New World (the Americas) is incredibly diverse and has gone through several major transformations due to colonization, resistance, syncretism, and revival. A thorough breakdown requires dividing it into major epochs and regional typologies, which include:

I. Pre-Columbian Indigenous Religions

These were incredibly diverse, deeply tied to cosmology, agriculture, and place, often mediated through ritual specialists like shamans, priests, and kings. Key features include:

A. Mesoamerica (Mexico and Central America)

	•	Aztec Religion: Polytheistic; major deities include Huitzilopochtli (sun/war), Quetzalcoatl (wind/knowledge), Tlaloc (rain), and Coatlicue (earth). Practices included human sacrifice and calendrical rituals.

	•	Maya Religion: Similar pantheon structures but more regionally diverse. Strong focus on cosmological cycles, ancestor worship, and sacred kingship. Sacred texts include the *Popol Vuh*.

	•	Olmec and Zapotec: Early theocratic systems, influencing later Mesoamerican deities and cosmologies.



B. Andes (South America)

	•	Inca Religion: Worship of Inti (Sun God), Viracocha (creator god), and Pachamama (Earth Mother). The Inca emperor was considered divine. Sacred geography and ancestor worship were key.

	•	Pre-Inca traditions: Mochica, Nazca, and Tiwanaku religious traditions with deities linked to fertility, water, and agriculture.



C. North America

	•	Mississippian Religion: Temple mound complexes, worship of fertility and storm deities, possibly linked to Mesoamerican influence.

	•	Great Plains/Northeastern Woodlands: Vision quests, animistic spirit world, totemism, and participation in rituals like the Sun Dance.

	•	Arctic/Subarctic: Shamanism central; interaction with animal spirits, hunting magic, and taboos governed by ecological relationships.



D. Caribbean & Amazonia

	•	Taino Religion: Zemis (ancestral spirits/deities), cohoba ritual, and a dual creator pair (Yúcahu and Atabey).

	•	Amazonian Religions: Shamanic cosmologies, ayahuasca rituals, jaguar and serpent symbolism, and fluid spirit-world borders.



II. Colonial Conversion & Syncretism (1492–1800s)

A. Catholic Hegemony

	•	Spanish and Portuguese empires enforced Catholicism through missions and religious orders (Jesuits, Franciscans, Dominicans).

	•	Indigenous religions were often suppressed, though elements were incorporated covertly into Catholic practice.

	•	Reductions (Jesuit missions) in Paraguay and Brazil preserved some indigenous autonomy while evangelizing.



B. African Diaspora Religions

	•	Enslaved Africans brought Yoruba, Kongo, Fon, and other religious systems.

	•	These merged with Catholicism and indigenous elements to form:

	•	Lukumí/Santería (Cuba)

	•	Candomblé (Brazil)

	•	Vodou (Haiti)

	•	Obeah and Myal (Caribbean)

	•	Hoodoo/Rootwork (Southern U.S.)



Each retained core deities (orishas, loa) mapped onto Catholic saints in a process of religious syncretism and survival under persecution.



C. Protestant Influence

	•	British colonies introduced Anglicanism, Puritanism, and later Methodism and Baptist movements.

	•	In some areas, Protestant missions suppressed or sought to assimilate African and Native spiritualities.

	•	Protestant denominations adapted to New World conditions, sometimes creating new sects (e.g., Mormonism in the U.S.).



III. Resistance, Revitalization, and Syncretic Movements (1800s–1900s)

A. Indigenous Revitalization

	•	Ghost Dance (Lakota and others): Millenarian movement combining Christian and indigenous beliefs to resist colonization.

	•	Native American Church: Legalized peyote use; combines Christian elements with traditional rituals.



B. Afro-Diasporic Consolidation

	•	Codification of Vodou, Santería, Candomblé, etc., into formal religious systems.

	•	Ritual specialists (houngans, babalawos) became respected figures; divination, trance possession, and sacrifice continued.

	•	Resistance to state suppression and Christian orthodoxy remained central.

C. Hybrid Religious Movements

	•	Spiritualism: 19th-century U.S. and Caribbean movements blended mediumship with Protestant and African beliefs.

	•	Rastafari (Jamaica): Christian-African reinterpretation centered on Ethiopia and resistance to Babylon (Western oppression).

	•	Mormonism and Jehovah’s Witnesses: Grew rapidly in the New World with unique cosmologies.



IV. Modern Religious Landscape (1900s–Present)

A. Latin America

	•	Still predominantly Catholic, but increasingly diverse:

	•	Rise of Pentecostalism and Evangelical Protestantism (particularly among the poor).

	•	Liberation Theology: Catholic-Marxist synthesis advocating for the oppressed.

	•	Growing interest in indigenous spirituality and neo-shamanism.



B. United States & Canada

	•	Highly pluralistic: Christianity still dominant (Protestant, Catholic), but with strong secular and multi-faith presence.

	•	Indigenous movements resurging with cultural preservation and land rights activism.

	•	Rise of New Age, Eastern religions (Buddhism, Hinduism), Neopaganism, and atheism/agnosticism.



C. Caribbean & Brazil

	•	Strong persistence of Afro-Diasporic religions, often alongside or integrated with Christianity.

	•	Spiritist traditions like Kardecism (especially in Brazil) blend Christian moralism with reincarnation and mediumship.
