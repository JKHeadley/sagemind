# Brief Review of Curanderismo



Curanderismo is a rich, complex, and deeply syncretic healing tradition practiced throughout Latin America, particularly in Mexico, Central America, and parts of the southwestern United States. It blends indigenous, Catholic, African, and sometimes Eastern influences into a holistic system of healing that encompasses physical, emotional, spiritual, and social well-being.



1. Etymology and Definition

	•	“Curanderismo” comes from the Spanish verb *curar*, meaning “to heal.”

	•	A curandero or curandera is the practitioner of this healing art. They are also called:

	•	Yerberos (herbalists)

	•	Sobadores (body workers)

	•	Hueseros (bone-setters)

	•	Espiritistas (spirit mediums)

	•	Brujos/as (witches; sometimes used pejoratively, but not always)

	•	Sanadores/as (healers)



Curanderismo is not a monolithic system but rather a pluralistic framework of interwoven traditions.



2. Historical Origins

A. Pre-Columbian Indigenous Traditions

	•	Rooted in Aztec, Maya, Mixtec, Zapotec, Inca, and other indigenous medicine systems.

	•	Focus on balance between the body, nature, and spiritual forces.

	•	Used temazcales (sweat lodges), sacred plants, and ritual calendars.

	•	Healing deities like:

	•	Ixchel (Maya goddess of healing and childbirth)

	•	Ixtlilton (Aztec god of medicine and healing)



B. Spanish Catholic Influence

	•	Curanderismo incorporated:

	•	Saint veneration

	•	Holy water, candles, rosaries

	•	Prayers, Psalms, and Biblical readings

	•	Virgin Mary syncretized with indigenous goddesses (e.g., Tonantzin and the Virgin of Guadalupe).



C. African Influence

	•	Particularly in Caribbean and coastal areas, African traditions (e.g., Yoruba, Bantu) added:

	•	Divination systems (like cowrie shells)

	•	Spirit possession

	•	Animal sacrifice (in some cases)

	•	Use of roots and baths (baños) for spiritual cleansing



D. Later Syncretic Currents

	•	19th–20th century elements:

	•	Spiritism (from Allan Kardec)

	•	Theosophy, Rosicrucianism, and other esoteric systems

	•	Eastern medicine and energy work (in more modern versions)



3. Core Components of Curanderismo

A. Concept of Illness

Illness is seen as having multiple origins:

	•	Natural: injury, infection, imbalance

	•	Supernatural: sorcery, spirit intrusion, envy (mal de ojo)

	•	Spiritual/Psychological: soul loss (espanto or susto), guilt, grief, unresolved trauma



B. Energetic Concepts

	•	Mal aire / Mal viento: “bad air” or negative energy absorbed through contact with cursed places or people

	•	Susto: “soul fright” – part of the soul leaves due to trauma or shock

	•	Envidia / Mal de ojo: envy or “evil eye” causing physical or emotional symptoms



C. Diagnosis Techniques

	•	Limpia (spiritual cleansing) often used diagnostically:

	•	Egg limpia (huevo): egg passed over body, then broken in water to read patterns

	•	Copal or incense smoke to scan the body

	•	Pendulum or crystal readings

	•	Spirit guides (espiritismo style)

5. Major Practices and Rituals

A. Limpias (Spiritual Cleansings)

	•	Done using:

	•	Herbs (ruda, rosemary, basil)

	•	Eggs

	•	Alcohol

	•	Copal or incense

	•	Flowers or citrus

	•	Purpose:

	•	Remove spiritual blockages

	•	Balance emotional energy

	•	Disconnect spiritual attachments

B. Plant Medicine (Herbolaria)

	•	Each herb has energetic and physical uses:

	•	Ruda (Rue): protection and exorcism

	•	Manzanilla (Chamomile): calming, for susto

	•	Romero (Rosemary): energetic cleansing

	•	Albahaca (Basil): purification and attracting good spirits



C. Prayers and Invocations

	•	Christian prayers: Our Father, Hail Mary, Psalms

	•	Indigenous chants or prayers

	•	Invocations of saints, angels, ancestors

D. Divination

	•	Tarot cards

	•	La baraja española (Spanish playing cards)

	•	Egg readings

	•	Mediumship/Spirit channeling

E. Ritual Baths (Baños)

	•	Infusions of herbs, flowers, perfumes, and holy water

	•	Used to break hexes, calm emotions, or invoke blessings

F. Offerings and Altars

	•	Altars may contain:

	•	Saints, candles, food, water, tobacco, personal items

	•	Offerings to Santa Muerte, Guadalupe, or other figures



6. Spiritual Cosmology

Curanderismo recognizes a layered spiritual reality:

	•	God / Creator

	•	Saints and angels

	•	Ancestral spirits

	•	Nature spirits (duendes, naguales)

	•	Lost souls or malevolent spirits

	•	Witches or sorcerers

	•	Dualities: Light/dark, good/bad, but often with fluidity (not absolute moral binaries)



7. Syncretism with Other Traditions

A. Catholicism

	•	Seen not just as religious but as ritual technology (e.g., prayers as invocations)

	•	Saints function as intercessors or deities

B. Indigenous Systems

	•	Medicine wheels, lunar calendars, and sacred geography are still used

	•	Ceremonial practices align with seasonal and agricultural cycles

C. Spiritism

	•	Belief in communication with the dead

	•	Many curanderos are also mediums, especially in urban or Caribbean contexts



8. Social Role of the Curandero

	•	Curanderos act as:

	•	Healers

	•	Psychologists

	•	Priests

	•	Community leaders

	•	Their knowledge is passed orally, often through apprenticeship or family lineages.

	•	They often work without formal payment, but accept gifts or donations.



9. Modern Developments

	•	Urban curanderismo is blending with:

	•	Reiki, acupuncture, yoga, Jungian psychology

	•	Popular figures: Teresa Urrea (Santa de Cabora), El Niño Fidencio, and modern TikTok curanderos

	•	Increasing academic study and revival of curanderismo as an alternative medicine



10. Controversies and Ethical Considerations

	•	Debate over:

	•	Cultural appropriation in New Age communities

	•	Ethical use of animal sacrifice or fear-based healing

	•	Distinction between brujo and curandero (blurry line)

	•	Exploitation of vulnerable people by false healers



11. Common Conditions Treated

	•	Susto (soul loss)

	•	Mal de ojo (evil eye)

	•	Empacho (digestive blockage, often spiritual in cause)

	•	Tristeza / depression

	•	Addiction, trauma, spiritual oppression
