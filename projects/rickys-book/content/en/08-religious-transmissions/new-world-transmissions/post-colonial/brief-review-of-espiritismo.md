# Brief Review of Espiritismo



Espiritismo is a broad term encompassing a variety of spiritualist beliefs and practices primarily found in Latin America and the Caribbean. It is often syncretic, blending Indigenous, African, Catholic, and European spiritual traditions. Below is a detailed breakdown of its origins, forms, beliefs, rituals, and regional variations.



I. Etymology and General Definition

	•	“Espiritismo” is Spanish for “spiritism” and refers to systems of belief and practice that center around communication with spirits.

	•	It differs from generic spiritualism by being deeply embedded in local religious traditions, often blending Catholic saints, African deities (orishas, loas), and Indigenous spirits.



II. Historical Origins

A. European Roots

	•	Espiritismo was significantly influenced by Kardecist Spiritism, codified by Allan Kardec (1804–1869), a French educator whose works (e.g., *The Spirits’ Book*) systematized spiritualist philosophy.

	•	Kardecism spread to Latin America in the 19th century, especially among literate elites in Brazil, Cuba, and Puerto Rico.



B. Colonial Syncretism

	•	African religious traditions (Yoruba, Kongo, Fon, Bantu) were brought through slavery, leading to syncretic systems like Santería, Palo Monte, Candomblé.

	•	Indigenous cosmologies (e.g., Taíno, Arawak, Andean traditions) also contributed.

	•	Espiritismo often acts as a mediating layer between Catholicism and these other traditions.



III. Core Beliefs

	1.	Spirits Exist and are in constant interaction with the living.

	2.	The Afterlife is a continuum where spirits evolve.

	3.	Mediumship is the ability to communicate with spirits through trance, automatic writing, or possession.

	4.	Moral Evolution: Spirits and humans are expected to evolve morally over time through reincarnation and spiritual development.

	5.	Healing and Protection: Spiritual entities can help heal the sick, guide the confused, and protect the vulnerable.

	6.	Syncretism: Spirits often appear as Catholic saints, African orishas, or Indigenous ancestors.



IV. Main Forms of Espiritismo

1. Kardecian Spiritism

	•	Based on Allan Kardec’s doctrine.

	•	Philosophical and moral focus on reincarnation, spirit hierarchy, and spiritual evolution.

	•	Popular in Brazil and parts of Puerto Rico and Cuba.

	•	Mediumship is usually practiced in formal settings like *centros espiritistas*.

	•	Emphasizes charity, education, and moral progress.



2. Espiritismo Cruzado (“Crossed Spiritism”)

	•	A blend of Kardecism, Santería (Lukumí), and Palo Monte.

	•	Spirits are classified into *cuadros espirituales* (spiritual courts), often appearing as historical figures (e.g., Indians, gypsies, Africans).

	•	Very common in Cuba, Puerto Rico, and among diasporic communities.



3. Santerismo / Espiritismo de Cordón

	•	Hybrid of Espiritismo, Santería, and Folk Catholicism.

	•	Cordón rituals involve circling, chanting, and rhythmic movement to build spiritual energy.

	•	The goal is possession or spiritual “mounting.”

	•	Centers on healing, cleansing, and *misa espiritual* (spirit mass).



4. 21 Divisiones (Dominican Republic)

	•	Syncretism of Espiritismo, Folk Catholicism, and Vodou.

	•	Organized into divisions (e.g., Indian, Black, White).

	•	Often involves possession rituals, spirit offerings, and divination.



5. Espiritismo Científico (Puerto Rico)

	•	Attempts to rationalize and formalize spirit interaction using Kardecist logic.

	•	Rejects African and Indigenous elements as “superstitious” — unlike cruzado forms.

	•	Tends to appeal to the middle/upper classes.

V. Key Practices

A. Misa Espiritual (Spirit Mass)

	•	A ceremony of communion with the dead.

	•	Conducted with prayers, spirit invocation, and often mediumistic contact.

	•	Participants may identify their *cuadro espiritual* or spirit guides.

B. Veladas / Seances

	•	Spirit circles for healing, guidance, or communication.

	•	Can be done with or without formal mediums.

	•	May involve *libretas espirituales* (spirit notebooks) for automatic writing.

C. Spiritual Baths and Cleansings

	•	Use of herbs, perfumes, candles, and prayers to cleanse negative energies or spiritual attachments.

	•	Often based on Afro-Caribbean herbal knowledge.



D. Possession / Trance Mediumship

	•	Spirit may “mount” the practitioner, delivering messages, diagnoses, or performing healing.

	•	Spirits may take on distinct personalities: e.g., *La India*, *El Gitano*, *El Congo*.

VI. Spiritual Hierarchies: Cuadro Espiritual

Spirits are categorized into “courts” or families:

	1.	Los Indios – Indigenous ancestors, noble and wise.

	2.	Los Negros – African spirits, powerful, protective, and linked to justice.

	3.	Los Gitanos – Gypsies, travelers, bring knowledge of divination and freedom.

	4.	Los Arabes – Spirits of wisdom, esotericism.

	5.	Los Médicos – Healers, often doctors or herbalists.

	6.	Los Ancestros – Personal ancestors guiding the practitioner.

	7.	Los Monjes / Santos – Saints, monks, and holy figures.



VII. Tools and Items Used

	•	Bóveda espiritual (spirit altar): Table with water glasses, candles, images of saints, flowers, rosaries.

	•	Libreta espiritual: Spiritual notebook to record messages from spirits.

	•	Candles: Used for light and communication.

	•	Statues and Icons: Blend of Catholic saints, orishas, and folk figures.

	•	Herbs and Perfumes: For baths and space-cleansing.

	•	Prayer books: Psalms, Catholic prayers, and Kardecist invocations.

IX. Social Function and Role

	•	Healing and Therapy: Emotional, physical, and spiritual.

	•	Community Building: Centers serve as spiritual and social support systems.

	•	Resistance and Identity: For Afro-descendant and Indigenous communities, Espiritismo preserves cultural memory and defies colonial suppression.



X. Criticism and Controversy

	•	Syncretism is sometimes seen as diluting traditional forms (especially by strict practitioners of Santería or Kardecism).

	•	Class-based divides: More elite groups may reject Afro-Indigenous forms.

	•	Persecution: Often stigmatized as witchcraft or superstition.

	•	Co-optation: In some contexts, it is commercialized or diluted for mass appeal.
