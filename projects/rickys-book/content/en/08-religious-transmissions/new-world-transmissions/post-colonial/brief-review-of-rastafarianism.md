# Brief Review of Rastafarianism



Rastafarianism, often referred to as the Rastafari movement, is a complex spiritual and socio-political tradition that emerged in Jamaica during the 1930s. It’s rooted in Afrocentric consciousness, resistance to colonialism and oppression, and a reimagining of biblical theology through an African lens.

I. Origins of Rastafarianism

A. Historical Context

	•	Post-Emancipation Jamaica (1838–1930s):

	•	Following the abolition of slavery in 1838, Jamaica’s Black population was left disenfranchised and economically marginalized.

	•	Christian missions taught subservient interpretations of the Bible, while colonial institutions maintained white supremacy.

	•	Pan-Africanism and Marcus Garvey:

	•	Marcus Mosiah Garvey, a Jamaican Black nationalist and founder of the *Universal Negro Improvement Association* (UNIA), advocated for African unity and return to Africa (Back-to-Africa movement).

	•	Garvey reportedly prophesied: “Look to Africa where a Black king shall be crowned; he shall be the Redeemer.”

B. The Coronation of Haile Selassie I

	•	In 1930, Ras Tafari Makonnen was crowned Emperor Haile Selassie I of Ethiopia, taking the titles *King of Kings, Lord of Lords, Conquering Lion of the Tribe of Judah*.

	•	Many in Jamaica interpreted this event as the fulfillment of Garvey’s prophecy, marking Selassie as the second coming of Christ, or God incarnate (*Jah*).



II. Core Beliefs of Rastafarianism

A. Divinity of Haile Selassie

	•	Rastafarians believe Haile Selassie is Jah, the living God.

	•	He is seen as the return of Jesus in a new form, fulfilling prophecies in Revelation.



B. Zion and Babylon

	•	Zion represents Africa, particularly Ethiopia, as the promised land.

	•	Babylon is the Western imperialist system of oppression, colonialism, and mental slavery.

	•	Rastafari ideology centers on the *liberation from Babylon* and the *return to Zion*, either spiritually or literally.



C. The Bible

	•	The Bible is respected but interpreted through an Afrocentric lens.

	•	Rastafarians emphasize the Old Testament, especially prophecies and Psalms.

	•	They believe the Bible has been edited to support colonial interests and prefer alternative texts like the *Kebra Nagast* (Ethiopian royal chronicle).

D. I-and-I Philosophy

	•	Symbolizes the unity of the individual with Jah and the collective self.

	•	“I-and-I” replaces pronouns like “me,” “you,” or “we” to affirm spiritual oneness.

E. Livity

	•	The spiritual energy and lifestyle aligned with natural law and divine consciousness.

	•	Rastas strive to live in harmony with nature and Jah’s will.

III. Practices and Symbols

A. Dreadlocks

	•	Symbolize the mane of the Lion of Judah and spiritual strength.

	•	Also represent a rejection of Babylonian grooming norms and a vow of Nazarite purity.

B. Ital Diet

	•	A natural, mostly vegan or vegetarian diet emphasizing unprocessed food.

	•	Many Rastas avoid salt, alcohol, and meat, especially pork.

C. Ganja (Cannabis)

	•	Used as a sacrament and spiritual tool.

	•	Believed to bring higher reasoning, meditation, and communion with Jah.

	•	Justified biblically (e.g., Genesis 1:29).

D. Colors: Red, Gold, Green (and Black)

	•	Red: blood of martyrs

	•	Gold: wealth of Africa

	•	Green: land and hope

	•	Black: the African people

	•	These reflect the Ethiopian flag and Pan-African symbolism.



E. Nyabinghi Chanting and Drumming

	•	Rituals involving call-and-response hymns, African drums, and chants.

	•	Named after the Ugandan anti-colonial Queen Nyabinghi, adapted to Jamaican resistance.

IV. Branches of Rastafarianism

1. Nyabinghi Order

	•	Oldest and most spiritual group.

	•	Focus on ritual, chanting, and resistance to political corruption.

2. Bobo Ashanti

	•	Founded by Prince Emmanuel Charles Edwards.

	•	Highly structured, priestly order.

	•	Members wear turbans and long robes; emphasize purity, strict gender roles, and celibacy before marriage.

3. Twelve Tribes of Israel

	•	Most Christian-oriented.

	•	Believe Jesus was a divine prophet but not Jah Himself.

	•	Members are divided into 12 tribes based on birth month.

V. Influence and Global Reach

A. Cultural Impact

	•	Spread through reggae music, most famously by Bob Marley, Peter Tosh, and Burning Spear.

	•	Reggae helped disseminate Rasta philosophy globally.



B. Political Resonance

	•	Themes of anti-colonialism, Black empowerment, Pan-Africanism, and spiritual resistance have found resonance across Africa, Latin America, and the Black diaspora.

VI. Costa Rican Freemason Founder: Leonard Howell

A. Biography

	•	Leonard Percival Howell (1898–1981), often called The First Rasta.

	•	Born in Jamaica, he traveled extensively—including to Panama, Costa Rica, and the U.S.



B. Time in Costa Rica

	•	During his travels through Costa Rica and Panama, Howell became familiar with:

	•	Pan-Africanist ideas.

	•	Freemasonry, particularly in the Afro-Caribbean and Masonic lodges active among West Indian migrant communities in Central America.

	•	These influences merged esoteric thought with anti-colonial Black consciousness.



C. Freemasonry and Influence

	•	While not officially confirmed as a “high-degree” Mason, Howell’s exposure to Masonic lodges in Limón, Costa Rica (home to many Afro-Jamaican immigrants), deeply influenced his theological structure.

	•	Howell’s doctrine reflects:

	•	Masonic ideas of *inner light, divine kingship*, and *initiation*.

	•	Ethiopian royal imagery as a counterpart to European secret society symbolism.



D. The Promised Key (1935)

	•	Howell’s foundational text for Rastafarian thought.

	•	Declares Haile Selassie as God, attacks colonialism, and lays out the esoteric and racial theology of Rastafari.

	•	Its structure borrows from secret society catechisms and esoteric Q&A formats.



VII. Modern Challenges and Legacy

	•	Mainstreaming: Rasta symbols have been commercialized, often detached from their deep spiritual meanings.

	•	Internal Debate: Whether Haile Selassie was divine (he denied being God).

	•	Social Activism: Rastas have engaged in environmentalism, repatriation efforts, prison reform, and marijuana legalization.

	•	Recognition: Ethiopia granted land to Rastafarians in Shashamane after Selassie’s offer in the 1950s.
