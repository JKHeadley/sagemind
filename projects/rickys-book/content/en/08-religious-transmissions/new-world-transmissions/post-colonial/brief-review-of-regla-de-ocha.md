# Brief Review of Regla De Ocha



Regla de Ocha—also known as La Regla de Osha-Ifá, Santería, or Lucumí—is a deeply syncretic Afro-Caribbean religious system rooted in the spiritual and ritual practices of the Yorùbá people of West Africa, primarily those brought to the Americas via the transatlantic slave trade. It evolved in Cuba, where it fused with elements of Roman Catholicism, Spiritism, and indigenous Caribbean cosmologies to form a richly complex, initiatory, divinatory, and devotional tradition.



Below is a detailed breakdown of Regla de Ocha, categorized into historical roots, cosmology, key practices, priesthood structure, rituals, theology, initiation, and syncretism.



⸻

I. HISTORICAL ORIGINS

1. Yoruba Foundations

	•	Regla de Ocha originates from the Òrìṣà religion of the Yorùbá people, especially from the Oyo, Egba, and Ife regions of Nigeria.

	•	The Yoruba cosmological, divinatory, and ritual system was preserved and adapted in Cuba, especially among slaves in Havana, Matanzas, and Santiago de Cuba.



2. Cuban Development

	•	In Cuba, Yorùbá religious practices became known as “Lucumí”, a term derived from a Yoruba greeting phrase (“oluku mi” – “my friend”).

	•	Slaves disguised worship of the Òrìṣà under the names of Catholic saints to avoid persecution—a process known as syncretism.

	•	By the 19th century, Regla de Ocha had developed its own priesthoods, initiation lineages, and liturgical language (a ritualized form of Yorùbá now called Lucumí).



II. COSMOLOGY AND THEOLOGY

1. Olodumare / Olorun

	•	The Supreme Being, all-powerful, all-knowing, yet distant from human affairs.

	•	Regla de Ocha is not about direct worship of Olodumare but through the intercession of Òrìṣà, divine archetypes or manifestations of divine energy.



2. The Òrìṣà

	•	Deified ancestors and natural forces, each governing specific aspects of nature and human life.

	•	Examples include:

	•	Elegguá: Trickster, guardian of the crossroads, opener of roads (syncretized with St. Anthony or El Niño de Atocha).

	•	Changó: God of lightning, masculinity, dance, and virility (St. Barbara).

	•	Yemayá: Mother of the seas, nurturer (Our Lady of Regla).

	•	Oyá: Wind, cemetery gates, storms, transformation (Our Lady of Candelaria).

	•	Obatalá: Creator of human bodies, associated with peace and justice (Our Lady of Mercy or Jesus in white robes).

	•	Oshún: River goddess of love, beauty, and diplomacy (Our Lady of Charity of El Cobre).

	•	Ogún: Warrior, iron, technology (St. Peter or St. George).

	•	Babalú Ayé: Healing, disease, especially smallpox (St. Lazarus).

	•	Each Òrìṣà has colors, foods, songs, numbers, taboos, dances, rhythms, and ritual objects.



3. Ashé

	•	The spiritual force or vital energy that animates all things. Òrìṣà are embodiments of different types of ashé.

	•	Rituals are designed to manipulate, direct, or align with ashé.



4. Egun

	•	Spirits of the ancestors, especially one’s blood ancestors.

	•	Honoring Egun is foundational and precedes Òrìṣà veneration.



III. THE STRUCTURE OF PRACTICE

1. Ilé / Casa

	•	A house of worship led by a Santero (m) or Santera (f) (also called Olorisha).

	•	Each ilé operates independently but within lineages (traced through godparents or initiators).



2. Priesthood Ranks

	•	Aleyo: Uninitiated devotee.

	•	Aborisha: Initiated devotee (received some Òrìṣà, usually Elegguá).

	•	Iyawó: “Bride of the Òrìṣà,” the title given during the first year after full initiation.

	•	Olorisha (Santero/Santera): Fully initiated priest of an Òrìṣà.

	•	Babalawo / Iyalawo: Priests of Ifá—an oracle system primarily associated with Orunmila, the spirit of wisdom.



3. Gender Roles

	•	Gender influences the role one may take, but Regla de Ocha has flexibility—female Babalawos are rare but exist in some communities.

	•	Trans and queer initiates have historically found acceptance, especially as the Òrìṣà often transcend gender categories.



IV. INITIATION RITUALS

1. Receiving the Warriors

	•	The devotee is given small consecrated icons of Elegguá, Ogún, Ochosi, and Osun, marking the first formal step into Ocha.



2. Receiving Necklaces (Eleke)

	•	Sacred beaded necklaces associated with specific Òrìṣà.

	•	Consecrated in ritual baths and worn as spiritual protection and identity markers.

3. The Kariocha (“Making of the Saint”)

	•	The full initiation ceremony, a seven-day seclusion where the initiate “dies” and is reborn under their tutelary Òrìṣà.

	•	The ceremony includes:

	•	Washing in omiero (herbal water).

	•	Head shaving (symbol of spiritual rebirth).

	•	Animal sacrifices to feed the Òrìṣà.

	•	Possession trance by the tutelary Òrìṣà (sometimes).

	•	Crowning of the head with the Òrìṣà (Orí).

	•	Presentation to the community (día del medio).

	•	After initiation, the iyawó must follow strict taboos and dress in white for a year.



V. DIVINATION SYSTEMS

1. Diloggún (Cowrie Shells)

	•	Divination performed by casting 16 cowries, interpreted by an Olorisha trained in the signs (odu).

	•	Odu give insight into the will of the Òrìṣà and how to resolve spiritual or material problems.



2. Ifá Divination

	•	Performed only by Babalawos, using ikin (sacred palm nuts) or the opon Ifá board.

	•	Offers deeper, more esoteric insights; includes a complex corpus of verses (patakí) that guide ethical and ritual action.



VI. RITUAL ELEMENTS AND SYMBOLS

1. Drumming and Dance (Toques de Santo)

	•	Used to invoke and embody Òrìṣà; features the batá drums, sacred rhythms, and songs in Lucumí.

	•	During public ceremonies, an Òrìṣà may possess a devotee.



2. Sacrifices

	•	Offerings include food, candles, herbal baths, and animal sacrifices.

	•	Sacrifice is a way to feed ashé and maintain spiritual balance.



3. Offerings and Altars

	•	Each Òrìṣà has specific offerings (ebó) and altars, which may include stones, shells, metal objects, or sacred pots (soperas).



VII. SYNCHRONICITY AND CATHOLIC INFLUENCE

	•	Catholic saints were used to mask the worship of the Òrìṣà. This syncretism is still honored in many rituals, especially in public-facing aspects.

	•	Major feast days for saints double as feast days for Òrìṣà (e.g., Our Lady of Charity of El Cobre is also Oshún’s feast day).



VIII. VARIANTS AND RELATED SYSTEMS

	•	Regla de Ifá: Branch specifically devoted to the practice of Ifá divination.

	•	Palo Monte (from Central African Congo traditions): Often practiced alongside Ocha but distinct.

	•	Espiritismo: Mediumship and ancestral work often practiced by Ocha adherents to complement Òrìṣà work.



IX. ETHICAL SYSTEM AND LIFE ORIENTATION

	•	Each odu (divination sign) contains moral lessons, taboos, and prescriptions.

	•	Emphasis on character (ìwà pẹ̀lẹ́), humility, balance, and reciprocity with the spiritual world.

	•	Practitioners are taught to balance ashé through right living and ritual.



X. CONTEMPORARY CONTEXT

	•	Regla de Ocha is practiced worldwide, especially in Cuba, Puerto Rico, Venezuela, the U.S., Brazil (as Candomblé or Umbanda), and parts of Europe.

	•	It has become a diasporic spiritual system with global influence, though with tensions over authenticity, commercialization, and cultural appropriation.
