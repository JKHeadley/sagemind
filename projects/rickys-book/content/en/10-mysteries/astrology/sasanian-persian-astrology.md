# Sasanian Persian Astrology



The Sasanian Empire (224–651 CE) preserved and synthesized one of the most important astrological traditions in late antiquity, blending Babylonian, Hellenistic, and Indian influences into a unique Persian astrological system that would later influence Islamic astrology and, through it, European and South Asian traditions.



1. Historical and Cultural Context

	•	Zoroastrianism was the state religion, and its cosmological and dualistic worldview influenced astrological interpretations.

	•	The Sasanian elite placed high value on omen literature, celestial divination, and astrology as tools for imperial legitimacy and governance.

	•	Astrology was closely tied to royal ideology, especially in predicting the fate of kings and empires (mundane astrology).



2. Sources and Texts

Key texts include:

a. Zij al-Shah (Zij-i Shah) (“Royal Astronomical Tables”)

	•	Said to be based on Indian and Hellenistic sources.

	•	Commissioned under the Sasanian king Khosrow I (Anushirvan) in the 6th century.

	•	Known in Arabic as the Zij al-Shah, later used by Islamic astronomers.



b. Kitab al-Mawalid (“Book of Nativities”)

	•	Sasanian works on natal astrology, translated into Arabic after the Islamic conquest.

	•	Often attributed to astrologers like Zaradusht, a legendary Zoroastrian sage (possibly a fusion of Zoroaster and later astrologers).



c. The “Zoroastrian” Book of Enoch (Mēnōg ī Xrad, Dēnkard)

	•	While not strictly astrological manuals, these Zoroastrian texts contain references to celestial order, fate, and metaphysical cosmology related to astrological ideas.



3. Astrological Techniques and Beliefs

a. Zodiac System

	•	Used the tropical zodiac (Greek influence), not the sidereal system of Indian astrology.

	•	12 zodiac signs associated with cosmic, seasonal, and mythological functions.



b. Planetary Deities

	•	Planets were personified and linked with Zoroastrian entities and deities:

	•	Ohrmazd (Ahura Mazda) with the Sun.

	•	Anāhitā (goddess of fertility and water) often associated with Venus.

	•	Tīr (associated with Mercury) was the deity of rain and messages.

	•	Saturn (Kayvān) and Mars (Bahram) had malefic associations, echoing both Babylonian and Greek interpretations.



c. Great Conjunctions

	•	Much emphasis was placed on Jupiter–Saturn conjunctions, interpreted as heralds of regime change or major historical shifts.

	•	These conjunctions were tracked and calculated in Sasanian astrology for imperial prophecy.



d. Firdaria (Time Lords)

	•	Possibly an early form of chronocratorship or planetary rulership over periods of life, later found in Islamic astrology.



e. Apotelesmatics (Predictive Astrology)

	•	Sasanian astrology practiced individual horoscope reading (nativities), interrogational astrology (horary), and mundane predictions (for kings and empires).



4. Integration with Indian and Hellenistic Astrology

	•	Under Khosrow I, Indian texts like the Siddhānta of Brahmagupta were translated into Middle Persian.

	•	Greek-Hellenistic astrological works (e.g., from Ptolemy, Dorotheus of Sidon) were also preserved or adapted into Persian.

	•	Result: A synthesis of Greek epicycles, Indian numerics and nakshatras, and Persian cosmology.



5. Astrologers and Court Diviners

	•	Zardusht ibn Shāpur and Bozorgmehr are legendary Sasanian astrologers and philosophers.

	•	They served as royal advisors and were tasked with interpreting celestial omens for major political decisions.

	•	Astrology was both sacred and strategic—used to time wars, marriages, coronations, etc.



6. Influence on Later Traditions

After the Arab conquest of Persia:

	•	Many Sasanian texts were translated into Arabic, forming the backbone of Islamic astrology.

	•	The House of Wisdom in Baghdad inherited and expanded on Sasanian models.

	•	Concepts like the Zij (astronomical tables), planetary periods, and mundane astrology influenced figures like Abu Maʿshar al-Balkhi, al-Kindi, and al-Sufi.



7. Esoteric and Ritual Aspects

	•	Astrology was tied to Zoroastrian cosmology:

	•	The fixed stars were part of the realm of Asha (order).

	•	Comets and eclipses were seen as incursions from Druj (chaos).

	•	Timing rituals, prayers, and purification ceremonies based on astrological timing was common.

	•	Astrology and dream interpretation were linked in court practices and Zoroastrian esotericism.

Deep Dive: Political Use of Astrology in the Sasanian Empire

The Sasanian Empire (224–651 CE) integrated astrology deeply into its imperial ideology and governance, using celestial knowledge not just as esoteric doctrine, but as a strategic and political tool.



1. Astrology as Imperial Legitimization

	•	The king (Shahanshah) was seen as divinely sanctioned, and his reign had cosmic implications.

	•	Astrological texts were used to predict and justify the rise of rulers.

	•	The king’s birth chart, planetary alignments at coronation, or major conjunctions (e.g., Saturn–Jupiter) were interpreted as omens of legitimacy.



Example: Khosrow I was said to have commissioned astrological texts that confirmed the righteousness and cosmic timing of his reign.



2. Mundane Astrology: Empires and Conjunctions

	•	Sasanian astrologers were obsessed with great conjunctions (Jupiter-Saturn every ~20 years), believed to bring major changes to dynasties and empires.

	•	These conjunctions were mapped to zodiacal triplicities (fire, earth, air, water), each signifying different eras of civilization.

	•	They used mundane astrology to predict:

	•	The fall of rival empires (e.g., Rome or Byzantium).

	•	The rise of Arab or Turkic powers (seen in late Sasanian forecasts).

	•	Natural disasters or revolts.



3. Strategic Decision-Making

	•	Major decisions—wars, treaties, royal marriages, temple consecrations, legal reforms—were often timed according to astrologically favorable windows.

	•	Court astrologers (like Bozorgmehr) acted as state advisors, not just mystics.



4. Eschatology and Millenarian Beliefs

	•	Zoroastrian doctrine divided time into 12,000-year epochs, and astrology helped calculate humanity’s position in the cosmic drama.

	•	The idea of Saoshyant (world savior) was linked to planetary configurations, similar to messianic and apocalyptic expectations elsewhere.

	•	This apocalyptic astrology helped rally legitimacy during Sasanian struggles (e.g., with Byzantine wars or internal dynastic crises).



5. Astrology as Intelligence and Counterintelligence

	•	Spies and emissaries were often trained in astrology or carried astrologers with them to interpret omens.

	•	Byzantine and Sasanian courts exchanged astrological texts, creating an astrological arms race in late antiquity.

	•	Astrology functioned as both propaganda and psychological warfare, claiming “the stars favor us.”



6. Integration with Zoroastrian Ritual Calendar

	•	The Zoroastrian calendar (with festivals like Nowruz) was aligned with solar and stellar cycles.

	•	Priests used astrology to:

	•	Time rituals for cleansing, blessing, or apotropaic action.

	•	Forecast ecological shifts (e.g., droughts, plagues).

	•	Conduct fire temple dedication at astrologically powerful times.



7. Transmission and Secrecy

	•	Sasanian astrologers carefully guarded their technical manuals and calculations, passing them down through priestly or elite initiates.

	•	After the Islamic conquest, these materials were:

	•	Translated by Persian scholars into Arabic.

	•	Incorporated into Islamic court astrology, especially under Abbasids.

	•	Later re-translated into Latin and spread to Europe.
