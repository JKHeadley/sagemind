# History of Magic in War



1. Prehistoric & Tribal Warfare

Key Traits: Animism, sympathetic magic, ancestor invocation

	•	Fetishes in battle: Warriors carried carved talismans, animal bones, or pieces of enemy bodies believed to house spiritual power or grant protection.

	•	Witchcraft: Shamans or sorcerers cursed enemies through effigies (proto-voodoo dolls) and invoked natural forces.

	•	Example: Kongo minkisi and Luba figures with embedded nails and blades, used to “activate” spirits in war fetishes.



2. Ancient Civilizations

Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Persia

	•	Curse tablets (defixiones): Written spells to curse enemy kings or armies, buried in sacred or liminal spaces.

	•	Protective amulets and rituals: Soldiers wore divine symbols (e.g., Eye of Horus) for protection in battle.

	•	Magical texts like the *Maqlû* series included anti-witchcraft rites used in times of national crisis.



Greece and Rome

	•	War gods & magical acts: Ares/Mars were invoked, and soldiers carried relics blessed by priests.

	•	Binding spells: Greek hoplites and Roman legionnaires sometimes commissioned curse tablets to spiritually “bind” enemy generals or disrupt formations.

	•	Divination: Entrails, flight of birds, or spontaneous omens interpreted before battle.



3. African & Indigenous Traditions

	•	War fetishes: Nkondi (nail fetishes) in Central Africa invoked spirits of vengeance and justice; often “fed” with blood or offerings before war.

	•	Zulu izinyanga (war doctors): Prepared warriors with medicinal and magical rituals to make them “bulletproof.”

	•	Native American traditions: Used spiritual objects (medicine bundles, painted symbols) in war dances and battle prep.



4. Islamic and Christian Traditions

Islamic

	•	Talismanic shirts (khirqa): Qur’anic verses written on garments worn by warriors during jihad.

	•	Sufi magic: Lamas or saints inscribed protective prayers on paper or metal for soldiers.



Christian

	•	Holy relics: Carried into battle by Crusaders—e.g., pieces of the True Cross.

	•	Exorcisms and blessings: Knights underwent blessings and wore crosses or holy medallions as spiritual armor.

	•	Witchcraft panic: Some Christian armies feared enemy witches could “call storms” or curse troops—leading to witch trials during wartime.



5. Early Modern & Colonial Warfare

	•	Fetish-fueled resistance: Anti-colonial fighters in Haiti, Benin, or Angola used magical protections, calling spirits into fetishes or potions.

	•	*Example*: The Mau Mau uprising in Kenya involved oath-taking rituals and war charms.

	•	European sorcery in war: John Dee (court magician of Elizabeth I) used scrying and angelic rituals to influence imperial policy.

	•	The Witch of Endor revival: Folklore and mysticism influenced elite advisors in both Protestant and Catholic courts.



6. 20th Century Occult Warfare

Nazi Occultism

	•	Thule Society, Vril, and Ahnenerbe explored rune magic, Atlantis myths, and psychic warfare.

	•	Hitler and Himmler saw warfare as a spiritual battle—fetishizing relics like the Spear of Destiny.

	•	Soviet psyops and counter-magic units rumored to exist in response.

Allied Responses

	•	British warlocks like Dion Fortune and Gerald Gardner performed mass rituals to “protect Britain from Nazi invasion.”

	•	Aleister Crowley claimed involvement in symbolic warfare, suggesting occult sigils embedded in propaganda.



7. Contemporary Warfare and Symbolic Magic

Even in today’s era of high-tech warfare, symbolic and magical thinking persists:

Inscribing weapons

	•	Profanities or mockery on bombs/bullets:

	•	Vietnam War: “Eat this, Charlie!” or “From LBJ with love.”

	•	Iraq/Afghanistan: “This one’s for 9/11,” “Allah who?”

	•	These function like sympathetic magic—projecting psychic aggression into the weapon, transforming it into a fetish of vengeance or intimidation.



Unit sigils and war paint

	•	Special forces use skulls, flames, and other totems as intimidation (e.g., Punisher symbol).

	•	Graffiti or symbolic words on tanks, aircraft, or uniforms act as personalizing talismans.

Cyberwarfare & psychological ops

	•	Modern equivalents to curses: disinfo campaigns, meme warfare, AI-generated propaganda meant to “psychically fracture” enemy morale.

8. Psychological and Anthropological Interpretation

	•	Weapons as fetishes: In anthropology, a “fetish” is any object believed to hold spiritual power—modern soldiers often form emotional bonds with gear or ritualize its maintenance.

	•	War magic as performance: Magic boosts morale, enacts control over chaos, and helps soldiers navigate the existential horror of killing.

	•	Ritualized violence: Execution ceremonies, symbolic acts (e.g., burning flags or effigies) mirror archaic sacrificial rites.



9. Parallels in Pop Culture and Art

	•	Magical thinking in military fiction: From Tolkien’s enchanted swords to anime like *Akira*, war and magic intertwine symbolically.

	•	Performance art: Paul McCarthy and other artists explore war magic through grotesque fetishized costumes and ritual disfigurement.



Conclusion:

From bone charms and blood-soaked idols to laser-guided bombs marked with vulgar graffiti, magic in warfare is not dead—it has evolved. Whether through ancestral invocation, psychological warfare, or sacred symbols defaced with mockery, magic in war remains a tool for:

	•	Projection

	•	Protection

	•	Power through symbolism

The weapons may change, but the soul of war magic—manipulating fear, fate, and the unseen—remains eerily constant.
