# Common Language of Theurgy



1. Exhortation

Definition:

A passionate, emphatic appeal, urging, or admonition. It often takes the form of a speech, command, or written statement encouraging moral or spiritual action.



Etymology:

From Latin *exhortari* – “to incite, encourage,” from *ex-* (out) + *hortari* (to encourage or urge).



Context and Function:

	•	Religious/Spiritual: A preacher may deliver an exhortation to stir moral awakening or inspire repentance.

	•	Magical/Esoteric: Used rhetorically or ritualistically to *command* or *energize* intention—often when addressing others or oneself.

	•	Psychological: A motivational force; it rallies willpower or community energy.



Key Traits:

	•	Persuasive and motivational

	•	Directed outward (toward others or collective psyche)

	•	Often verbal, fiery, or impassioned



2. Consecration

Definition:

The formal act of making something sacred, dedicating it to divine service or aligning it with higher spiritual purpose.



Etymology:

From Latin *consecrare* – “to make sacred,” from *con-* (together, with) + *sacrare* (to make sacred).



Context and Function:

	•	Religious: Consecration of altars, churches, priests, or objects (e.g., Eucharist).

	•	Magical: Tools, talismans, or spaces are consecrated to purify them and align them with the operator’s intent or divine energies.

	•	Psychospiritual: Acts of inner dedication (e.g., vows, sacrifices, devotion).



Key Traits:

	•	Ritualized dedication

	•	Transfer or infusion of *sacred status*

	•	Often involves specific rites, symbols, or words

3. Conjuration

Definition:

The act of summoning a spirit, entity, or force through ritual, words, or magical means; also, a solemn appeal or oath.



Etymology:

From Latin *conjurare* – “to swear together, conspire,” later “to summon by oath or spell,” from *con-* (together) + *jurare* (to swear).



Context and Function:

	•	Magic/Occult: Common in grimoires; conjurations are formal summoning spells, often using sigils, names of power, or ritual circles.

	•	Religious: Less common, but overlaps with exorcisms or calling upon saints.

	•	Legal/Ancient: Could also mean a binding oath.



Key Traits:

	•	Strong verbal component (spells, chants, names)

	•	Often seeks to *compel* or *bind* an entity

	•	Structured, formulaic, sometimes adversarial



4. Saturation

Definition:

A state or process of being thoroughly soaked, filled, or permeated—either physically, mentally, or spiritually.



Etymology:

From Latin *saturare* – “to fill, glut,” from *satur* (full, sated).



Context and Function:

	•	Alchemy/Esotericism: Refers to a phase of total absorption or infusion (e.g., when a substance or soul is saturated with a force).

	•	Magical Practice: Charging an object with energy until it “overflows”; immersing a space or self with intention.

	•	Psychospiritual: A meditative or initiatic state of full immersion in a force, archetype, or experience.



Key Traits:

	•	Passive or active process

	•	Indicates completion or totalization

	•	Can be transformative or overwhelming



5. Invocation

Definition:

The act of calling upon a deity, spirit, or force to *enter*, *bless*, or *empower* the practitioner, often through prayer, ritual, or visualization.



Etymology:

From Latin *invocare* – “to call upon,” from *in-* (into) + *vocare* (to call).



Context and Function:

	•	Theurgical/Magical: Aimed at inviting benevolent or divine forces into one’s being; e.g., invoking archangels, gods, or higher self.

	•	Mysticism: Often meditative or internal; used in rituals of divine union.

	•	Religious: Common in liturgy, where blessings or divine presence is invoked.



Key Traits:

	•	Seeks *union* or *presence*

	•	Invites (does not command)

	•	Often emotional, reverent, or ecstatic

6. Evocation

Definition:

The act of calling forth or summoning a spirit, intelligence, or force into a defined space—distinct from invocation in its *external focus*.



Etymology:

From Latin *evocare* – “to call out,” from *e-* (out) + *vocare* (to call).



Context and Function:

	•	Ceremonial Magic: Involves summoning a spirit into a triangle or space to communicate, command, or learn from it. Usually more “controlling” than invocation.

	•	Mystical Traditions: Bringing forth inner archetypes or latent forces into conscious awareness.

	•	Spirituality: Can be used symbolically—evoking memories, spirits of place, etc.



Key Traits:

	•	External and objective (compared to internal invocation)

	•	Often uses tools (pentacles, sigils, incense, etc.)

	•	Usually involves a boundary or containment
