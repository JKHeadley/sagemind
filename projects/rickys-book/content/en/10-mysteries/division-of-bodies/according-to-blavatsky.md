# According to Blavatsky



Blavatsky’s Sevenfold Human Constitution


This is the core of her esoteric anthropology:

	1. Sthūla-śarīra (स्थूल शरीर)

	•	Sanskrit Breakdown:

	•	*Sthūla* (स्थूल) = gross, dense, material, thick

	•	*Śarīra* (शरीर) = body, form, that which decays

	•	Meaning: “Gross body” — the visible, material, physical body.

	•	The dense, visible material body.

	•	It is inert and perishable.

	2. Liṅga-śarīra (लिङ्ग शरीर)

	•	Sanskrit Breakdown:

	•	*Liṅga* (लिङ्ग) = sign, mark, subtle form, symbol; in Sāṅkhya: the subtle body or indicator

	•	*Śarīra* (शरीर) = body, form, that which decays

	•	Meaning: “Subtle body” or “symbolic body” — a template or double of the physical form.

	•	A subtle form or double of the physical body.

	•	Acts as the blueprint and intermediary between higher and lower states.

	3. Prāṇa (प्राण)

	•	Sanskrit Breakdown:

	•	*Pra-* (प्र) = forward, before, vital

	•	*-āṇa* (आण) = breathing, movement

	•	Meaning: “Breath” or “life-force” — vital energy that sustains living beings.

	•	Note: In classical Indian thought, prāṇa is also subdivided into five vāyus (vital winds).

	•	The animating energy that vitalizes the body.

	•	It is not a body, but a principle that flows through the system.

	4. Kāma (काम)

	•	Sanskrit Breakdown:

	•	*Kāma* (काम) = desire, longing, sensuality, wish, passion

	•	Meaning: The desire principle — associated with emotional drives, cravings, and passions.

	•	The seat of passions, emotions, and personal cravings.

	•	Blavatsky often referred to this as the animal soul.

	5. Manas (मनस्)

	•	Sanskrit Breakdown:

	•	*Manas* (मनस्) = mind, thought, mental faculty, cognition

	•	Root: √*man* (मन्) = to think, to perceive

	•	Meaning: The thinking principle — the seat of cognition, memory, and ego-consciousness.

	•	Blavatsky divides it into:

	•	*Lower Manas* — personal mind (bound to desire)

	•	*Higher Manas* — spiritual intellect (linked with Buddhi)

	•	The principle of individual consciousness and thought.

	•	Divided into:

	•	Lower Manas: associated with the personality, ego, reason.

	•	Higher Manas: spiritual aspect, drawn toward Buddhi.

	6. Buddhi (बुद्धि)

	•	Sanskrit Breakdown:

	•	*Buddhi* (बुद्धि) = intelligence, insight, discrimination, spiritual perception

	•	Root: √*budh* (बुध्) = to awaken, to know, to perceive

	•	Meaning: The intuitive soul or spiritual discernment — the faculty that perceives truth directly.

	•	The vehicle of divine wisdom, intuition, and spiritual insight.

	•	Blavatsky called this the receptive vehicle of Atma.

	7. Ātman / Ātma (आत्मन् / आत्मा)

	•	Sanskrit Breakdown:

	•	*Ātman* (आत्मन्) = self, spirit, breath, essence; eternal principle

	•	Root: Possibly √*an* (to breathe) or √*at* (to move, pervade)

	•	Meaning: The universal Self or divine essence — the most abstract, formless principle in man.

	•	Note: Blavatsky usually wrote *Atma*, but the accurate Sanskrit is Ātman (with nasalization). 7.	Ātma — *Universal Spirit*

	•	The highest, most abstract principle.

	•	Not personal; a ray of the universal Absolute.



While Blavatsky did not enumerate “planes” in the later formal sense, she implied levels of being through these principles, roughly corresponding to layers of existence. In her writings, she sometimes refers to:

	•	Three Higher Principles:

	•	Ātma

	•	Buddhi

	•	Higher Manas

	•	Four Lower Principles:

	•	Lower Manas

	•	Kāma

	•	Prāṇa

	•	Liṅga-Śarīra / Sthūla-Śarīra



These seven principles interpenetrate, and consciousness operates on whichever principle dominates in a given moment—whether physical, emotional, mental, or spiritual.



Key Points Blavatsky Emphasized

	•	She warned against rigid classifications of planes and “bodies.”

	•	She emphasized the fluid interaction of principles rather than fixed layers.

	•	Her model was influenced by Vedānta, Sāṃkhya, and Buddhist Abhidharma, adapted for Western esotericism.

	•	The Higher Triad (Ātma–Buddhi–Manas) survives death and carries karmic continuity.

	•	The Lower Quaternary (body, vitality, desire, and lower mind) disintegrates after death.



No Etheric, Astral, Mental, Buddhic Planes?

Correct. Blavatsky:

	•	Did use the word “astral”, especially in “astral body” and “astral light” (akin to the subtle world),

	•	But she never divided the cosmos into formal “planes” called “mental plane,” “buddhic plane,” or “monadic plane.”



These were later systematizations by Besant and Leadbeater, which she did not endorse and sometimes criticized as speculative or misleading.
