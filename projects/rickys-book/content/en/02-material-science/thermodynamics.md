# Thermodynamics



I. INTRODUCTION TO THERMODYNAMICS

Thermodynamics is the branch of physics concerned with heat, energy, and work and how they relate to matter and its transformations. It studies the macroscopic behavior of systems, abstracting away the microscopic details.



It applies to everything from engines to black holes, and even to philosophical questions about irreversibility, time, and entropy.

III. THE FOUR LAWS OF THERMODYNAMICS

Zeroth Law (Thermal Equilibrium)

If A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B is with C, then A is with C.

Implication: This justifies the use of temperature as a transitive and measurable property.



First Law (Conservation of Energy)

ΔU = Q - W

	•	ΔU: Change in internal energy

	•	Q: Heat added to the system

	•	W: Work done by the system on the surroundings



Implication: Energy can’t be created or destroyed, only transformed or transferred.



Second Law (Entropy and Irreversibility)

The entropy of an isolated system always increases over time.



Forms:

	•	Kelvin-Planck Statement: No engine can convert all heat into work.

	•	Clausius Statement: Heat cannot spontaneously flow from cold to hot.



Entropy (S) measures disorder or multiplicity of microstates.



For a reversible process:

ΔS = ∫(dQ_rev / T)

Third Law (Entropy at Absolute Zero)

As T → 0 K, the entropy of a perfect crystal approaches zero.

This law establishes absolute zero as a theoretical lower bound and implies the unattainability of 0 K.

VI. ENTROPY: MICROSCOPIC INTERPRETATION

Boltzmann Formula:



S = k * ln(Ω)

	•	k: Boltzmann constant

	•	Ω: Number of microstates



This shows entropy as a measure of multiplicity: more ways to arrange the system microscopically ⇒ higher entropy.

VIII. PHASE TRANSITIONS

At constant T and P, phase changes involve:

	•	Latent heat (e.g., fusion, vaporization)

	•	No temperature change during transition

	•	Entropy and enthalpy increase when moving to more disordered phases (e.g., solid → liquid → gas)



IX. NON-EQUILIBRIUM THERMODYNAMICS

Most real systems aren’t in perfect equilibrium. Topics include:

	•	Entropy production

	•	Irreversible processes

	•	Transport phenomena (heat conduction, diffusion)

	•	Onsager relations: linear response theory



X. STATISTICAL THERMODYNAMICS

Statistical mechanics bridges microstates and macrostates.



Key concepts:

	•	Partition function (Z): Encodes statistical weights

	•	Probability of microstates:

P(i) = e^(-Eᵢ / kT) / Z

	•	Thermodynamic quantities from Z:

	•	U = -∂lnZ/∂β

	•	F = -kT lnZ

	•	S = -∑ P ln P



XI. APPLICATIONS AND BEYOND

	•	Chemistry: Gibbs energy for reaction spontaneity

	•	Biology: ATP metabolism, entropy in cell membranes

	•	Cosmology: Black hole thermodynamics, cosmic entropy

	•	Information Theory: Entropy = information uncertainty (Shannon)

	•	Economics/Systems Theory: Entropic models in complex systems



XII. SYMBOLIC AND PHILOSOPHICAL ASPECTS

Thermodynamics has inspired philosophical thought:

	•	Thermodynamic death: Universe’s heat death

	•	Information = Entropy: Physical and epistemological limits

	•	Karma-like metaphors: Irreversibility, feedback, correction
