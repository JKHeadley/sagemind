# The Quintessential Myth



The Eleusinian Story:

According to Ovid’s *Metamorphoses* (Book 5), Venus, the goddess of love, wants to extend her influence over the underworld. She notices that Pluto (Hades), the ruler of the dead, remains untouched by love and passion. Seeking to change this, she instructs her son Cupid to strike Pluto with one of his enchanted arrows, ensuring that he falls in love.

Obeying his mother, Cupid shoots Pluto, and soon after, Pluto sees Persephone (Proserpina), the daughter of Demeter (Ceres), picking flowers in a meadow. Overcome with passion due to Cupid’s arrow, Pluto abducts Persephone and takes her to the underworld, setting off a series of events that lead to the cycle of the seasons.

The Yoruba Story:

In Yoruba mythology, Ogun, the orisha of iron, war, and technology, once retreated into the forest, refusing to engage with society. Without Ogun’s tools and craftsmanship, civilization began to decline, and the orishas struggled to function without his skills.

Ochún, the orisha of love, beauty, and sweet waters, was tasked with bringing Ogun back. Rather than confronting him with force or reason, she used her charm, dance, and laughter. She moved gracefully through the forest, singing and dancing seductively, drawing Ogun out of his hiding place. Overcome with desire, he followed her back to civilization, restoring balance and progress.

The Hindu Story:

Shiva, the ascetic god of destruction and transformation, retreats into deep meditation in the mountains, detaching himself from the world. However, for the cosmos to remain in balance, Shiva must be drawn back into engagement, especially for his union with Parvati, the goddess of love, fertility, and devotion.

Determined to win Shiva’s affection, Parvati performs intense tapas (spiritual asceticism) to prove her dedication. However, just like Venus and Ochún, the god of love—Kama (the Hindu equivalent of Cupid)—plays a crucial role. At the request of the other gods, Kama shoots an arrow of desire at Shiva to awaken his passions. Enraged, Shiva incinerates Kama with his third eye, but the seed of love has been planted.



Over time, Parvati’s devotion softens Shiva’s heart, and he eventually accepts her as his consort, leading to their divine union and the birth of Kartikeya (Skanda), a war god essential to cosmic order.



The Egyptian Story:

- Isis and Osiris

Osiris, the wise and just king of Egypt, is murdered by his jealous brother Set and cast into the underworld. His wife, Isis, the goddess of magic, love, and motherhood, refuses to accept his death. Using her powers, she searches for him, gathers his scattered body parts, and resurrects him long enough to conceive their son, Horus. Though Osiris ultimately remains in the underworld, he becomes its ruler, guiding souls in the afterlife.



- Ra and Hathor

Another relevant Egyptian myth involves Hathor, the goddess of love, beauty, and joy, and Ra, the sun god. When Ra grows old and weary, he withdraws from ruling the cosmos, allowing chaos to rise. Hathor, sometimes in her playful and sensual form, coaxes Ra out of his gloom with laughter, dance, and joy, restoring cosmic balance.

The Aztec Story:

In Aztec mythology, the best parallel is found in the interactions between Xochiquetzal (meaning “Precious Flower Feather”) the Aztec goddess of love, beauty, fertility, sensuality and Tezcatlipoca, the god of night, sorcery, and fate, a withdrawn, powerful figure, depicted as a chaotic trickster.



In some myths, Tezcatlipoca abducts Xochiquetzal, much like Pluto abducting Persephone. This act removes her influence from the world, disrupting fertility and love. In other versions, Xochiquetzal is sent to seduce Tezcatlipoca or to soften his darker tendencies, similar to how Ochún draws Ogun out of the forest or Hathor coaxes Ra out of his gloom.



The Mayan Story:
Ix Chel is a powerful rainbow goddess associated with love, fertility, and healing. Itzamná is a wise but somewhat distant creator god, linked to the sun and cosmic order.



Ix Chel as Itzamná’s consort, uses her influence to help guide creation and maintain balance. Without her presence, the world loses fertility and harmony. 

The Norse Story:Freyr, the Norse god of fertility, prosperity, and sunlight, becomes melancholic and withdrawn after he secretly sits on Odin’s high seat (Hlidskjalf) and sees Gerðr, a beautiful jötunn (giantess). Upon seeing her, Freyr is overcome with love and longing, but he cannot approach her, leading to his sorrow, withdrawal, and loss of joy. His distress affects the world, as Freyr is associated with fertility and prosperity, meaning that if he remains withdrawn, the land may suffer from barrenness.



Freyr is unable to act, so his servant Skírnir (whose name means “shining one”) takes it upon himself to win Gerðr’s hand for his master. Skírnir travels to Gerðr and offers her gifts, persuasion, and even threats, eventually convincing her to agree to meet Freyr after nine nights. Once Gerðr agrees, Freyr rejoices, and his despondency disappears.





The Japanese Story:Amaterasu, the Shinto sun goddess, is the ruler of the heavens. However, after a conflict with her brother, Susanoo, the storm god, she becomes deeply distressed and hides inside a cave, plunging the world into darkness.



The other gods realize they need to bring Amaterasu back to restore light and balance. Ame-no-Uzume, the goddess of joy, mirth, and fertility, performs a wild, seductive dance, causing the other gods to laugh and celebrate. Intrigued by the noise and festivity, Amaterasu peeks out, and the gods pull her from the cave, restoring sunlight to the world.



Vajrayana Buddhism:

In Vajrayana (Tibetan) Buddhism, a similar pattern exists with the dakini Vajrayogini, a powerful, enlightened female deity who awakens wisdom and action in male figures.



Vajrayogini is a tantric goddess (dakini) who embodies wisdom, transformation, and energy. She guides male deities or practitioners toward enlightenment. Vajrayogini often appears to yogis in visions, inspiring them to act or reach higher states of awareness—a function similar to how fertility or love goddesses awaken dormant deities or forces in other traditions.



Cross Cultural Parallels:

- A Withdrawn or Distant Male (or Solar) Deity
- Pluto (underworld), Ogun (forest), Shiva (meditation), Osiris (dead/underworld), Ahura Mazda (distant cosmic force), Tezcatlipoca (night), Itzamná (distant cosmic creator), Freyr (loss of joy) Amaterasu (hidden sun), Vajrayana masters needing dakini wisdom.
- A Love/Fertility/Wisdom Goddess Awakening or Restoring Him
- Venus, Ochún, Parvati, Isis, Hathor, Anahita, Xochiquetzal, Ix Chel, Gerðr, Uzume, Vajrayogini.
- A Trick, Seduction, or Magic Is Often Used
- Cupid’s arrow (Venus-Pluto), dance/seduction (Ochún-Ogun, Hathor-Ra, Uzume-Amaterasu), devotion and tapas (Parvati-Shiva), resurrection magic (Isis-Osiris), abduction (Tezcatlipoca-Xochiquetzal), manipulation (Gerðr-Freyr), esoteric wisdom (Vajrayogini).
- Cosmic Balance Is Restored
- The seasons begin (Pluto & Persephone), Civilization resumes (Ogun & Ochún) Divine offspring are born (Shiva & Parvati → Kartikeya, Osiris & Isis → Horus) Rain returns (Xochiquetzal & Ix Chel) Order is reaffirmed (Anahita & Mithra, Hathor & Ra, Amaterasu returns, Vajrayogini guiding enlightenment)
