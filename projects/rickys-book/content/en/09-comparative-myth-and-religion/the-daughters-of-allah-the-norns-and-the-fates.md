# The Daughters of Allah, The Norns and the Fates



The term often translated as “cranes” in Arabic—*ghirnīq*, *ghurnūq*, *ghirnawq*, and *ghurnayq*—refers not just to elegant birds, but symbolically to divine beings. These forms also echo names for other powerful birds such as ravens, crows, and eagles—creatures often linked with fate, prophecy, war, and transcendence.

These “Birds of Paradise” are the three goddesses venerated in pre-Islamic Arabia: Al-Lāt, Al-‘Uzzā, and Manāt. They were not merely idols, but personifications of fate, time, and destiny—forces governing both war and peace, life and death, protection and destruction. Their roles find powerful parallels across Indo-European and other mythological systems.



Al-Lāt — *“The Mistress”*

Symbol: Gazelle, Crescent, Cubic Stone

Domains: Fortune, Peace, Sanctuary, Cycles of Time

She is the gentle face of fate, associated with sanctuary and maternal protection. Her image echoes Skuld, the Norse Norn whose name derives from *skulu*—“what should become.” Skuld represents the future and the necessity of unfolding destiny.

She is also parallel to Atropos of the Greek Moirai—“the inexorable,” she who cuts the thread of life with her dreaded shears, determining the moment of death.

Spiritual Parallel: Nirvana—the quiet inevitability of the end of becoming.



Manāt — *“She Who Determines”*

Symbol: Cup of Death

Domains: Finality, Judgment, Death, Allotment

The eldest of the three, Manāt governs the measure of one’s allotted time and fate. She resonates with Verðandi, the Norse Norn of the Present—*that which is happening now*.

She parallels Lachesis, the Greek Fate who measures the thread of life, determining its span.

Spiritual Parallel: Dharma—the law in motion, the unfolding consequence of one’s position and action in the cosmos.



Al-‘Uzzā — *“The Mightiest”*

Symbol: Acacia Tree

Domains: Power, Protection, War, Passion

A fierce and radiant deity, Al-‘Uzzā is the force of vitality and defense. She reflects Urðr, the Norse Norn of the Past—*that which has become*, the bedrock of what is.

She is akin to Clotho, the spinner of the thread of life from her spindle—she who initiates the journey.

Spiritual Parallel: Karma—the stored potency of action, the imprint of the past upon the present.



Together, these three divine figures form a triad of fate: Becoming, Being, and Unbeing—or Karma, Dharma, and Nirvana. In their flight as cranes, ravens, or eagles, they carry the weight of time and the wind of transformation.

They are the Daughters of Allah—the Cranes of Heaven, weaving the threads of destiny in air and myth.
