# Brief Explication of Tropes



A trope is a storytelling device, theme, or motif that recurs across literature, media, and art, often carrying symbolic or structural significance. The word comes from the Greek *tropos* meaning “turn” or “way,” originally used in rhetoric to refer to a figurative or metaphorical use of a word or phrase. Over time, the term expanded to include recurring patterns and conventions used in narratives.



I. Etymology & Original Use

	•	Greek Origin: *Tropos* (τροπος) — a turn, direction, or manner.

	•	Rhetorical Use: In classical rhetoric, a *trope* referred to the figurative or non-literal use of language (e.g., metaphor, irony, hyperbole).

Examples of rhetorical tropes:

	•	Metaphor: “Time is a thief.”

	•	Metonymy: “The crown will decide” (meaning “the monarch”).

	•	Synecdoche: “All hands on deck” (where “hands” stands for people).

	•	Irony: Saying “Great job!” when someone messes up.



In rhetoric, tropes were tools to *turn* the meaning of words; in narrative and pop culture, tropes are tools to *turn* the direction of a story, character, or theme.



II. Modern Use in Media and Literature

In contemporary literary and media theory, tropes are:

	•	Recognizable narrative patterns or devices

	•	Used to communicate ideas quickly

	•	Often operate as cultural shorthand

	•	Can be archetypal or formulaic

Examples:

	•	The Chosen One: A seemingly ordinary protagonist who turns out to be destined for greatness (e.g., Neo in *The Matrix*, Harry Potter).

	•	Evil Twin: A doppelgänger who represents the dark side of a character.

	•	Redemption Arc: A villain or morally gray character seeking to make amends.

	•	Love Triangle: A romantic plot with three people where one is caught between two lovers.



III. Types of Tropes

	1.	Character Tropes:

	•	The Wise Mentor (Gandalf, Dumbledore)

	•	The Femme Fatale

	•	The Reluctant Hero

	•	The Comic Relief Sidekick

	2.	Plot Tropes:

	•	Rags to Riches

	•	The Quest

	•	The Hero’s Journey

	•	Enemies to Lovers

	3.	Setting Tropes:

	•	The Haunted House

	•	The Small Town with Big Secrets

	•	The Post-Apocalyptic Wasteland

	4.	Genre Tropes:

	•	Noir detective monologue

	•	Fantasy prophecy

	•	Sci-fi time loop

	•	Romance misunderstanding

	5.	Visual Tropes:

	•	Lens flares in sci-fi

	•	Color coding (e.g., red = passion/danger)

	•	Slow-motion walk away from explosions



IV. Functions of Tropes

	•	Cognitive shortcut: They allow creators to convey meaning, emotion, or stakes quickly without lengthy exposition.

	•	Cultural storytelling grammar: Tropes form part of the shared “language” audiences understand.

	•	Evocative resonance: Many tropes tap into deep cultural, mythic, or psychological patterns (archetypes).

	•	Subversion and satire: Tropes can be played straight, inverted, or deconstructed for commentary or humor.



V. Critical Analysis: Tropes vs. Clichés

	•	A trope is not inherently bad; it becomes a cliché when it’s overused without originality or depth.

	•	Trope = structural pattern (neutral or positive).

	•	Cliché = stale repetition (negative connotation).



For example:

	•	Trope: The “reluctant hero” (used creatively, it still works).

	•	Cliché: A reluctant hero who predictably says “I’m not a hero” and then saves the world exactly as expected.



VI. Tropes in Comparative Myth and Religion

Tropes often appear in sacred texts and mythologies as recurring symbols or story structures:

	•	Flood narrative (Mesopotamian, Biblical, Mayan)

	•	Savior archetype (Christ, Prometheus, Quetzalcoatl)

	•	Underworld descent (Inanna, Orpheus, Jesus, Odin)

	•	Sacrificial scapegoat (Levitical goat, Christ, Azazel)



These mythic tropes serve both psychological and cosmological functions, as explained by thinkers like Joseph Campbell (monomyth), Carl Jung (archetypes), and Mircea Eliade (sacred time).

VII. Tropes and the Unconscious

Some scholars and theorists see tropes as expressions of collective unconscious patterns (Jungian archetypes) or cultural coding (semiotics):

	•	Jung: Tropes may correspond to universal psychological motifs.

	•	Barthes: Tropes are “myths” of modern culture, carrying ideological meaning.

	•	Freud: Repetitive patterns (e.g. father figures, returns, betrayals) hint at repressed content.



VIII. Modern Applications: TV Tropes & Metamodernism

	•	The website TV Tropes popularized the identification of tropes in all media.

	•	Contemporary creators often play with tropes self-consciously, leading to:

	•	Deconstruction: Breaking down a trope to examine its assumptions (*Watchmen*).

	•	Reconstruction: Returning to classic tropes but with sincerity (*The Mandalorian*).

	•	Metafiction: Tropes become part of the story’s self-awareness (*Community*, *Deadpool*).



IX. Frameworks That Use Tropes

	•	Narratology (Propp, Todorov): Tropes as narrative functions (e.g., the donor, the hero, the helper).

	•	Structuralism: Tropes as semiotic structures.

	•	Myth Criticism (Northrop Frye): Tropes as part of cyclical mythic patterns—comedy, tragedy, romance, irony.

	•	Media Theory: Tropes as memetic and viral units of storytelling.
