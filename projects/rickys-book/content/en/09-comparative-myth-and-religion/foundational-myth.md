# Foundational Myth



“Foundational myth” refers to a symbolic narrative that a community, civilization, religion, or political system uses to explain its origin, purpose, and identity. These myths often blend history, cosmology, theology, and political ideology, serving as a form of charter for social order and collective memory.

I. Definition and Function

1. What is a Foundational Myth?

A foundational myth (also called an *origin myth* or *charter myth*) is a narrative that explains how a community, institution, or worldview came into being. Unlike scientific accounts of origin, it is not primarily concerned with objective chronology but with meaning, legitimacy, and identity.



2. Functions

	•	Charter Function: Grants legitimacy to a current order (e.g., political authority, ritual system, caste structure).

	•	Collective Identity: Establishes a shared sense of belonging.

	•	Moral Instruction: Embeds ethical codes and taboos into origin stories.

	•	Cosmic Orientation: Places the group within a larger cosmological or metaphysical framework.

	•	Psychological: Reduces existential anxiety by providing continuity and purpose.



II. Core Components of Foundational Myth

1. The Chaos or Void

	•	Many foundational myths begin with chaos, primordial waters, darkness, or a formless void (e.g., *Tehom* in Genesis, Ginnungagap in Norse myth, *Nun* in Egyptian myth).

	•	Symbolizes undifferentiated potential.



2. Cosmogony (Creation of the World)

	•	Often involves divine acts, sacrifice, or division of primordial matter (e.g., Tiamat split by Marduk, Purusha dismembered in Vedas).

	•	Establishes cosmic order (cosmos) out of disorder (chaos).



3. Anthropogony (Creation of Humanity)

	•	Humans may be made from clay, blood, maize, bones, or divine breath.

	•	Usually implies humanity’s purpose and relationship to the divine.



4. The Founding Hero/Ancestor

	•	Often semi-divine, miraculously born, or raised by supernatural beings.

	•	Brings law, agriculture, writing, or religion.

	•	Sometimes sacrifices themselves or undergoes exile or trial.

5. The Sacred Covenant or Pact

	•	Between god(s) and the people (e.g., Abrahamic covenant, Inca Sun pact).

	•	Often involves oaths, taboos, and divine rewards or punishments.

6. The Sacred Geography

	•	The homeland, sacred mountain, river, or city is tied to divine intervention.

	•	Reinforces territorial legitimacy (e.g., Rome’s seven hills, Mount Zion, Mecca).

7. The Fall or Violation

	•	Foundational myths often involve a transgression (e.g., eating forbidden fruit, breaking a taboo), leading to suffering or exile.

	•	Reinforces obedience to the established order.

IV. Examples of Foundational Myths by Civilization

1. Mesopotamian: Enuma Elish

	•	Marduk defeats Tiamat (chaos) and creates the world from her body.

	•	Marduk founds Babylon and is made king of the gods.

	•	Humans created from the blood of a rebellious god to serve the gods.

2. Hebrew Bible: Genesis + Exodus

	•	Yahweh creates the world in six days.

	•	Adam and Eve fall; humanity descends into sin.

	•	God chooses Abraham’s descendants.

	•	Exodus from Egypt becomes the national myth of liberation and divine covenant.



3. Greek: Prometheus + Deucalion

	•	Prometheus gives humans fire, punished by Zeus.

	•	Deucalion survives flood, repopulates the world.

	•	Athens mythically founded by Athena; Sparta claims descent from Heracles.

4. Norse: Ymir and Ginnungagap

	•	Ymir, a primordial being, is slain by Odin and brothers.

	•	His body becomes the world; his blood the seas.

	•	Humans made from driftwood; gods establish order in Midgard.

5. Inca Myth: Inti and the Ayar Brothers

	•	Children of the Sun God sent to civilize the Andes.

	•	Manco Cápac and Mama Ocllo rise from Lake Titicaca.

	•	The Inca ruler is “Son of the Sun.”

6. Yoruba: Oduduwa and the Chain

	•	Oduduwa descends from heaven with a chain and sand to create land.

	•	Ile-Ife becomes the sacred center of Yoruba civilization.

	•	Political and religious systems trace their origin to this descent.

7. Mayan: Popol Vuh

	•	Gods create humans from maize.

	•	Earlier attempts failed (mud people, wood people).

	•	Twins Hunahpú and Xbalanqué defeat the lords of death in Xibalba.

V. Theoretical Approaches to Foundational Myth

1. Structuralist (Lévi-Strauss)

	•	Myths resolve binary oppositions (life/death, order/chaos).

	•	Foundational myths encode symbolic logic of a culture.

2. Psychoanalytic (Freud, Jung)

	•	Myths are projections of the collective unconscious.

	•	Foundational myths replay the Oedipus complex, hero archetype, etc.

3. Phenomenological (Eliade)

	•	Myths return us to *illud tempus* (“that time”)—a sacred time outside history.

	•	Rituals reenact foundational moments to sacralize the present.

4. Political Theology (Schmitt, Assmann)

	•	Myths are used to sacralize political authority.

	•	Egypt and Israel provide models: one with divine kingship, one with covenantal law.

5. Narrative-Hermeneutic

	•	Myths are interpretive frameworks for historical memory.

	•	They’re rewritten and reinterpreted depending on political and cultural needs.

VI. Foundational Myth vs. History

	•	Myth: Symbolic truth, collective meaning, transhistorical.

	•	History: Empirical, linear, potentially disenchanted.

	•	However, myths often co-opt history, and history is often mythologized.

	•	E.g., the American Founding Fathers as near-mythic figures.

	•	The Haitian Revolution is both historical and foundational-mythic in Haiti.



VII. Modern and Postmodern Uses

1. Nationalism and State Building

	•	Modern states often invent or revive myths: e.g., “Manifest Destiny,” Zionism, Aryan myth in Nazi Germany.

2. Revolutionary Myths

	•	French Revolution’s Cult of Reason, Marxist myth of proletariat redemption, Pan-African and Rastafarian myths.

3. Postcolonial Myths

	•	New nations often create hybrid myths blending indigenous traditions with anti-colonial resistance (e.g., Negritude, the Mau Mau myth in Kenya).

4. Myth in Media

	•	Superhero origin stories as new civil religion.

	•	Star Wars, Lord of the Rings, and The Matrix contain foundational myth structures.

IX. Initiatory Aspect of Foundational Myth

	•	Many myths mirror initiation rituals:

	•	Separation → Ordeal → Rebirth

	•	E.g., Moses in the desert, Odin hanging on Yggdrasil, Jesus’ crucifixion and resurrection.

	•	Suggests societies undergo ritual death and rebirth to be renewed.
